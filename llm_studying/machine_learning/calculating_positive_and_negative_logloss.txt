Your implementation of **class-conditional log-loss** is spot-on. Here’s an intuitive walkthrough:

1. **Clip probabilities** to avoid taking `log(0)`  
   ```python
   eps = 1e-15
   p = np.clip(y_pred_proba, eps, 1 - eps)
   ```
   Ensures every \(p_i\in[\varepsilon,1-\varepsilon]\).

2. **Positive-class log-loss**  
   \[
     \text{LL}_{\text{pos}}
     = -\frac1{N_{+}}
       \sum_{i: y_i=1} \log\bigl(p_i\bigr)
     = -\mathrm{mean}\!\bigl(\log(p[y==1])\bigr)
   \]  
   In code:
   ```python
   ll_pos = -np.mean(np.log(p[y == 1]))
   ```  
   You’re averaging \(-\log p\) over only the true-positive cases.

3. **Negative-class log-loss**  
   \[
     \text{LL}_{\text{neg}}
     = -\frac1{N_{-}}
       \sum_{i: y_i=0} \log\bigl(1 - p_i\bigr)
     = -\mathrm{mean}\!\bigl(\log(1 - p[y==0])\bigr)
   \]  
   In code:
   ```python
   ll_neg = -np.mean(np.log(1 - p[y == 0]))
   ```  
   That’s averaging \(-\log (1-p)\) over the true-negative cases.

---

### Why this makes sense

- The **full** binary log-loss is  
  \[
    -\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N
      \bigl[y_i\log(p_i) + (1-y_i)\log(1-p_i)\bigr].
  \]  
- By splitting the sum, you get two separate averages:
  - One over the \(y=1\) terms \(\bigl(-\log p_i\bigr)\).  
  - One over the \(y=0\) terms \(\bigl(-\log(1-p_i)\bigr)\).

Computing them separately gives you **per-class** insight into how well your model’s probabilities align with reality for each label. 

---

### Quick sanity check

- If your model is **perfect** on positives (\(p_i=1\) whenever \(y_i=1\)), then \(\log(p_i)=0\) → \(\text{LL}_{\text{pos}}=0\).  
- If it’s **confidently wrong** (\(p_i\to0\) for a positive), then \(-\log(p_i)\to\infty\), penalizing heavily.

Same story for negatives with \(\log(1-p_i)\).

That matches exactly what you’ve coded—so you can trust those positive/negative log-loss numbers as class-specific cross-entropy.

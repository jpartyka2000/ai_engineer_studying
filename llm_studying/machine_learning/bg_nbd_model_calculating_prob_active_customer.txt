In the BG/NBD model, the probability that a customer is still active is calculated using a formula that combines their purchase history with the model's parameters. Here's how it works:

The probability a customer is still active (often denoted as P(alive)) is calculated by:

1. First, the model looks at three key pieces of information about the customer:
   - x: How many purchases they've made (frequency)
   - tx: When they made their last purchase (recency)
   - T: How long they've been a customer (tenure/age)

2. The model combines these customer-specific data points with four parameters it has learned from your entire dataset:
   - r and α (alpha): Parameters related to the purchase frequency distribution
   - s and β (beta): Parameters related to the dropout rate distribution

3. The actual mathematical formula is complex, but conceptually it's calculating:
   • What's more likely: that this customer is still active but hasn't made a purchase for a while, OR that they've dropped out?

4. For customers with similar purchase counts (x), the model generally assigns:
   • Higher P(alive) to customers with more recent purchases (higher tx)
   • Lower P(alive) to customers with older last purchases

5. The model also considers the overall purchase frequency - customers who buy frequently but suddenly stop are treated differently than those who always bought infrequently.

The beauty of this approach is that it handles the uncertainty appropriately. A customer who typically purchases every few months and hasn't bought anything for a year will likely have a lower P(alive) than a customer who typically purchases once a year and hasn't bought anything for a year.

This probability is extremely useful for marketing decisions - you might decide to focus retention efforts on customers with mid-range P(alive) values, since those with very high values are likely to return without intervention, and those with very low values may be too costly to try to win back.

Yes, what you're describing — **model underprediction for specific subsets** (e.g., `degree_offering` + `year_quarter`) — is a **common real-world calibration problem**, especially in **imbalanced classification tasks**.

And yes — there are **valid ways to reduce underprediction**, either by **adjusting thresholds per subgroup**, **post-processing predictions**, or **incorporating group-awareness into training**.

---

## ✅ Why underprediction happens in subsets

Even with 5-fold CV and a globally optimal threshold, the model:

* Optimizes for **global performance** (not per-group)
* May struggle with **minority subsets** that have:

  * Few training examples
  * Different feature distributions
  * Temporal drift (`year_quarter` often proxies seasonality)

So even if the model is **globally well-calibrated**, some subsets can be **underpredicted or overpredicted**.

---

## ✅ Valid Strategies to Reduce Subset Underprediction

### ✅ 1. **Per-subgroup prediction thresholds (post hoc)**

You can compute and apply **custom thresholds per group** like `(degree_offering, year_quarter)`.

#### Example:

```python
thresholds = (
    df.groupby(["degree_offering", "year_quarter"])
      .apply(lambda g: get_optimal_prediction_threshold(g['actual'], g['probability']))
)
```

Then apply these thresholds per row during final prediction.

**Pros**:

* Simple and interpretable
* No model retraining required

**Cons**:

* Risk of overfitting to small groups
* Thresholds can fluctuate wildly for small-n groups

---

### ✅ 2. **Train a meta-calibration model (probability adjustment)**

Train a second-stage regression model to adjust predicted probabilities per group:

```python
df["adjusted_prob"] = calibrator_model.predict(df[["probability", "degree_offering", "year_quarter"]])
```

Can be a:

* **Gradient boosting regressor**
* **Isotonic regression per group**
* **Platt-style logistic model with interaction terms**

---

### ✅ 3. **Train group-aware models**

If some groups consistently underperform:

* Add `degree_offering` and `year_quarter` as **categorical features**
* Use LightGBM with `categorical_feature=...`
* Optionally train **group-specific models** for large-enough groups

---

### ✅ 4. **Weighted loss or instance-level weighting**

If you want the model to focus more on underperforming groups:

* Track underpredicted groups
* Assign higher weights to those instances in training
* LightGBM supports `sample_weight` per instance

---

### ✅ 5. **Hybrid: keep global threshold but audit bias**

Keep a single threshold, but:

* Measure **per-group calibration curves**
* Use **bias correction** formulas or intercept shifts post-training
* Flag or re-score subgroups with consistently poor recall

---

## 🧠 Strategy Selection Cheat Sheet

| Strategy                | Best for                      | Complexity | Risk of Overfitting |
| ----------------------- | ----------------------------- | ---------- | ------------------- |
| Per-subgroup thresholds | Medium/large groups           | Low–Med    | Medium              |
| Meta-calibration model  | Prob. smoothing across groups | Medium     | Low–Med             |
| Group-aware training    | Stable, structural underpred. | High       | Low                 |
| Weighted training       | Rare but important groups     | Medium     | Medium              |

---

### ✅ TL;DR

> Yes — you can reduce underprediction per group by applying **custom thresholds**, **calibrating**, or **training smarter models**.

✅ Per-subgroup thresholding is valid if:

* You have enough samples per group
* You're careful about not overfitting

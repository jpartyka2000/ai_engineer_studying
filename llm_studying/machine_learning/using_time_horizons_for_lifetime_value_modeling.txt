In practice, CLV models are often computed over a specific time horizon rather than assuming an infinite future. While the BG/NBD model is designed to predict a customer's remaining transaction behavior over their “lifetime,” you typically need to specify what that lifetime means in your context.

For example, many businesses choose to calculate CLV for the next 6 or 12 months because:

- **Actionable Insights:**  
  Forecasting over a fixed period (e.g., 6 months, 12 months) aligns with budgeting and marketing planning cycles.

- **Data and Censoring:**  
  It’s easier to reliably estimate future behavior for a finite period, especially when customers are still active (censored data). An infinite horizon prediction would require strong assumptions about eventual churn and long-term behavior.

- **Flexibility:**  
  Models like BG/NBD allow you to specify any future time frame when predicting the expected number of transactions. You can then combine these forecasts with an average profit per transaction (using, for example, a Gamma-Gamma model) to estimate CLV over that chosen horizon.

Thus, while you could interpret the model’s output as a “lifetime” value if you assume a sufficiently long horizon, most practical applications focus on one or more specific time frames (like 6 months or 12 months) to support decision-making. My overall preference would be to use finite, well-defined horizons that align with business needs and reduce the uncertainty inherent in long-term predictions.

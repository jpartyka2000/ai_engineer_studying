Not at all—a “stupid” question—it’s fundamental! The **training** and **validation** sets serve very different roles:

1. **Training set**  
   - Used to **fit** the model’s parameters (tree splits, weights in a neural net, etc.).  
   - The algorithm adjusts itself to minimize error on this data.

2. **Validation set**  
   - Held out during fitting.  
   - Used to **monitor** generalization: tune hyperparameters, trigger early stopping, compare architectures, etc.  
   - Never influences weight updates—so its metrics give an unbiased signal of “how would the model do on new data?”

---

### Why not just evaluate on the training set?

- **Optimistic bias**  
  A model can “memorize” the training examples. Its performance on data it has already seen will almost always look better than on unseen data—even if the learned patterns are just noise.  

- **No guardrail against overfitting**  
  If you measure error on the same data you train on, there’s no way to tell whether improvements come from real signal or simply memorizing quirks of those particular samples.  

- **Hyperparameter tuning would overfit**  
  Imagine you pick the number of trees or learning rate by maximizing accuracy on the training set—that choice will almost certainly exploit noise, not underlying patterns, and will fail on fresh data.

---

### Bottom line

By **separating** training and validation data, frameworks give you a “practice test” that the model never studies. That unbiased feedback is essential for:  
- Knowing when to stop training (early stopping)  
- Selecting hyperparameters (grid search, Bayesian optimization)  
- Preventing overfitting and ensuring your model actually learns patterns that generalize.

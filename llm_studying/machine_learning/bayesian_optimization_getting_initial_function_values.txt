In **Bayesian Optimization**, even though you don’t know the *closed-form* of the function \(f\), you do have a way to **evaluate** it at any input \(x\). That’s what makes it a **black-box function**: you can’t write down a simple formula for \(f(x)\), but you *can* “query” the function and get a numerical outcome. Typically, this evaluation is either:

1. **An Experiment or Measurement** – You try a certain setting \(x\) (e.g., a chemical recipe, a hardware configuration) and measure the output \(y\) (e.g., yield, runtime, accuracy).  
2. **A Costly Simulation** – You feed input parameters \(x\) into a simulator (e.g., a physics model, a neural network training run) and record the result \(y\).  

So, even though the *functional relationship* is not known analytically, you still have a “black box” that lets you *input* \(x\) and get a *measured or simulated* value \(y\). 

---

## **Example**

- **Neural Network Hyperparameter Tuning**: You pick two sets of hyperparameters \(x_1\) and \(x_2\). For each one:
  - You *train* the network with those hyperparameters (the black-box evaluation).  
  - You measure the performance metric \(y\) (e.g., validation error, accuracy).  
  - Now you have \((x_1, y_1)\) and \((x_2, y_2)\) to initialize Bayesian Optimization.

---

### **Why This Is Enough**

Even though you don’t have a simple formula \(f(x)\), each trial or experiment physically (or computationally) *provides* the output \(y\). Bayesian Optimization then:

1. **Builds a Surrogate Model** (often a Gaussian Process) using \((x,y)\) pairs.  
2. Uses an **acquisition function** to decide *which \(x\) to evaluate next* to maximize improvements.  
3. Repeats until you’ve used up your budget or converged.

So the **\(\mathbf{y}\) values come from actually *running or measuring* the black-box process** for your chosen \(\mathbf{x}\). That’s the crux: you cannot write down \(f\) in closed form, but you *can* “press a button” and get \(f(x)\) for any specific \(x\).

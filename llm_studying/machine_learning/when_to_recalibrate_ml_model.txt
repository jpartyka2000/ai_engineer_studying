Here’s how to think about “how often” to recalibrate (retrain or re‑tune) a model in production to guard against data drift:

---

## 1. Key factors that drive your cadence

* **Data velocity & volume**

  * High‑throughput domains (real‑time bidding, click‑through prediction, fraud detection) often see feature distributions shift within days or hours.
  * Slower domains (yearly budget planning, long‑term credit scoring) may be stable for months.

* **Business risk & impact**

  * If a bad prediction costs you money (e.g. mispriced insurance) or reputation (e.g. loan denials), you’ll want tighter controls.
  * For low‑stakes models (e.g. movie recommendations), you can tolerate drift longer.

* **Monitoring & thresholds**

  * Rather than blindly retraining on a calendar, set up drift‑detection tests (population stability index, KL divergence, Earth Mover’s Distance) or performance gates (monitor key metrics like ROC‑AUC, calibration error).
  * Trigger retraining when drift or performance degradation exceeds a pre‑defined threshold.

* **Cost of retraining**

  * Large models or pipelines that take days to run push you toward fewer, more deliberate retraining runs.
  * Lightweight models with rapid training cycles can support daily or on‑demand retraining.

---

## 2. Common cadences by domain

| Domain / Use‑Case                    | Typical Recalibration Interval          |
| ------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------- |
| Real‑time bidding / Ad tech          | **Daily** or even **hourly**            |
| Fraud detection / Intrusion alerts   | **Daily** to **weekly**                 |
| E‑commerce recommendations           | **Weekly** to **monthly**               |
| Demand forecasting (retail / energy) | **Weekly** to **bi‑weekly**             |
| Credit scoring / Insurance scoring   | **Quarterly** to **annually**           |
| Long‑term financial models           | **Annually** or on major policy changes |

---

## 3. Event‑driven retraining

1. **Automated drift detection**

   * Run statistical tests on incoming feature batches (e.g. PSI > 0.1 or KL divergence beyond threshold).
   * If triggered, launch a retraining job immediately.

2. **Performance‑based triggers**

   * Monitor live inference metrics (e.g. accuracy, F1, calibration error).
   * If they drop X % below baseline (commonly **5–10 %**), kick off recalibration.

3. **Hybrid approach**

   * Combine a **baseline schedule** (e.g. monthly) with drift/performance alerts for out‑of‑cycle retraining.

---

## 4. Best practices

* **Baseline schedule + alerts**
  Pick a sensible calendar cadence (e.g. monthly) but don’t rely on it alone—augment with drift/performance triggers.
* **Canary or rolling deployments**
  Before full rollout, test the newly retrained model on a slice of traffic to validate stability.
* **Version & compare**
  Keep old and new models side‑by‑side, track key metrics, and perform champion‑challenger tests.
* **Automate everything**
  Use a MLOps tool (Kubeflow, MLflow pipelines, Evidently.ai) to schedule retraining, run checks, and deploy new versions seamlessly.

---

### Bottom line

There’s no one‑size‑fits‑all interval.

1. **Fast‑moving, high‑risk** → daily or weekly.
2. **Medium‑velocity** → weekly to monthly.
3. **Slow or low‑risk** → quarterly to annually.

But whatever you choose, anchor it with **automated drift/performance monitoring** so you only retrain when it truly matters.

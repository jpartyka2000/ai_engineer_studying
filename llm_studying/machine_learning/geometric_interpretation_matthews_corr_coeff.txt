Awesome — here’s the geometric intuition for MCC:


---

Imagine this:

Your true labels (0s and 1s) can be seen as a vector in a high-dimensional space.

Your model’s predictions (0s and 1s) are also a vector in the same space.


You can think of each example (each row) as a dimension in space.
Example:

If you have 5 examples, then the true labels are something like [1, 0, 1, 0, 1]

Your predictions are maybe [1, 0, 1, 1, 0]


So now you have two vectors.


---

MCC is measuring the cosine of the angle between these two vectors.
(Just like Pearson correlation!)

If the two vectors are perfectly aligned → angle = 0 degrees → cos(0) = 1 → MCC = +1

If the vectors are completely opposed → angle = 180 degrees → cos(180) = –1 → MCC = –1

If the vectors are orthogonal (independent/random) → angle = 90 degrees → cos(90) = 0 → MCC = 0



---

Why does this make sense?

Because:

When you predict perfectly, every 1 in the true label matches a 1 in the prediction → vectors point the same way.

When you are always wrong, your 1s match 0s, and vice versa → vectors point opposite directions.

When you predict randomly, there’s no strong relationship between your prediction vector and the true label vector → vectors are orthogonal.

Final Thought:

That’s why MCC is so beautiful mathematically:
it literally measures how much your model's predictions "line up" with the truth, and it handles imbalance automatically because correlation doesn't care if you have 90% 0s and 10% 1s — it just measures how well the patterns match.

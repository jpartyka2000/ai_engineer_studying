Below is a **step-by-step** toy example showing how you might carry out *Gaussian Process (GP) regression* with a small data set. We’ll:

1. Define a tiny training set.  
2. Pick a simple kernel (covariance) function.  
3. Compute the Gaussian Process posterior mean and variance for a **single new test point**.  

> **Note**: We’ll do all computations explicitly so you can see how each matrix and vector is formed and combined.

---

## **Step 1: Define the Training Data**

Suppose you have **2 training points**:

\[
\begin{aligned}
x_1 &= 0, \quad y_1 = 1.0, \\
x_2 &= 1, \quad y_2 = 0.8.
\end{aligned}
\]

We collect them into:

- **Training inputs**: \(\mathbf{X} = (0, 1)\).  
- **Observed outputs**: \(\mathbf{y} = (1.0,\, 0.8)^\top.\)

We want to predict the function value at a **test point** \(x_* = 0.5\).

---

## **Step 2: Choose a Kernel Function**

We’ll use the **Radial Basis Function (RBF)** or “Gaussian” kernel with **variance** \(\sigma^2 = 1\) and **length scale** \(\ell = 1\). Recall:

\[
k(x, x') 
= \exp\!\Bigl(-\frac{(x - x')^2}{2\,\ell^2}\Bigr).
\]

Since \(\ell = 1\), it simplifies to:

\[
k(x, x') 
= \exp\!\Bigl(-\frac{(x - x')^2}{2}\Bigr).
\]

---

## **Step 3: Build the Covariance Matrix \(\mathbf{K}\)**

The covariance matrix \(\mathbf{K}(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{X})\) among the training inputs is:

\[
\mathbf{K} 
= 
\begin{pmatrix}
k(x_1, x_1) & k(x_1, x_2) \\
k(x_2, x_1) & k(x_2, x_2)
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Substitute \(x_1=0\) and \(x_2=1\):

1. \(k(0, 0) = e^{-((0 - 0)^2 / 2)} = e^0 = 1.\)  
2. \(k(0, 1) = e^{-((0 - 1)^2 / 2)} = e^{-0.5} \approx 0.60653.\)  
3. \(k(1, 0) = k(0, 1)\) by symmetry = \(0.60653.\)  
4. \(k(1, 1) = e^{-((1 - 1)^2 / 2)} = 1.\)

Thus,

\[
\mathbf{K}(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{X}) 
= 
\begin{pmatrix}
1.0 & 0.60653 \\
0.60653 & 1.0
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

*(We assume zero observation noise for simplicity; otherwise we’d add \(\sigma_n^2\mathbf{I}\) on the diagonal.)*

---

## **Step 4: Invert the Covariance Matrix**

We’ll need \(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\). For a \(2\times2\) matrix 
\(\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ b & a \end{pmatrix}\),
the determinant is \(a^2 - b^2\). So:

1. \(\det(\mathbf{K}) = 1\cdot 1 - 0.60653 \times 0.60653 \approx 1 - 0.3679 = 0.6321.\)  
2. The inverse is
   \[
   \mathbf{K}^{-1}
   =
   \frac{1}{0.6321}
   \begin{pmatrix}
   1.0 & -0.60653 \\
   -0.60653 & 1.0
   \end{pmatrix}
   \approx
   \begin{pmatrix}
   1.581 & -0.959 \\
   -0.959 & 1.581
   \end{pmatrix}.
   \]

---

## **Step 5: Build the Cross-Covariance Vector \(\mathbf{k}_*(x_*)\)**

For a **test point** \(x_* = 0.5\), we compute its covariance with each training input:

\[
\mathbf{k}_*(x_*) 
= 
\begin{pmatrix}
k(x_1, x_*) \\
k(x_2, x_*)
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
k(0, 0.5) \\
k(1, 0.5)
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

1. \(k(0, 0.5) = e^{-((0-0.5)^2 / 2)} = e^{-0.25/2} = e^{-0.125} \approx 0.8825.\)  
2. \(k(1, 0.5) = e^{-((1-0.5)^2 / 2)} = e^{-0.25/2} = e^{-0.125} \approx 0.8825.\)

Hence,

\[
\mathbf{k}_*(0.5)
\approx
\begin{pmatrix}
0.8825 \\
0.8825
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

---

## **Step 6: Posterior Mean**

In a noiseless GP, the **predictive mean** at \(x_*\) is:

\[
\mu(x_*) 
= 
\mathbf{k}_*(x_*)^\top \,\mathbf{K}^{-1}\,\mathbf{y}.
\]

1. First compute \(\boldsymbol{\alpha} = \mathbf{K}^{-1}\,\mathbf{y}\):

   \[
   \boldsymbol{\alpha}
   =
   \begin{pmatrix}
   1.581 & -0.959 \\
   -0.959 & 1.581
   \end{pmatrix}
   \begin{pmatrix}
   1.0 \\
   0.8
   \end{pmatrix}
   =
   \begin{pmatrix}
   1.581*1.0 - 0.959*0.8 \\
   -0.959*1.0 + 1.581*0.8
   \end{pmatrix}.
   \]
   
   Numerically,
   \[
   \boldsymbol{\alpha}
   \approx
   \begin{pmatrix}
   1.581 - 0.7672 \\
   -0.959 + 1.2648
   \end{pmatrix}
   =
   \begin{pmatrix}
   0.8138 \\
   0.3058
   \end{pmatrix}.
   \]

2. Then,
   \[
   \mu(0.5) 
   = 
   \mathbf{k}_*(0.5)^\top \boldsymbol{\alpha}
   =
   \begin{pmatrix}0.8825 & 0.8825\end{pmatrix}
   \begin{pmatrix}0.8138 \\ 0.3058\end{pmatrix}
   \approx
   0.8825 \times 0.8138 +\, 0.8825 \times 0.3058 
   \approx
   0.7187 + 0.2700 
   =
   0.9887.
   \]

So the GP predicts the function’s **mean** at \(x_* = 0.5\) is around **0.989**.

---

## **Step 7: Posterior Variance**

The **predictive variance** at \(x_*\) (no noise) is given by:

\[
\sigma^2(x_*) 
= 
k(x_*, x_*) \;-\; \mathbf{k}_*(x_*)^\top \,\mathbf{K}^{-1}\,\mathbf{k}_*(x_*).
\]

- First, \(k(0.5, 0.5) = e^{-((0.5 - 0.5)^2 / 2)} = e^0 = 1.\)  
- We already have \(\mathbf{k}_*(0.5)\) and \(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\). So:

  1. Compute 
     \[
     \mathbf{v}
     = 
     \mathbf{K}^{-1}\,\mathbf{k}_*(0.5).
     \]
     Numerically:
     \[
     \mathbf{v}
     =
     \begin{pmatrix}
     1.581 & -0.959 \\
     -0.959 & 1.581
     \end{pmatrix}
     \begin{pmatrix}
     0.8825 \\ 
     0.8825
     \end{pmatrix}
     =
     \begin{pmatrix}
     1.581 \times 0.8825 - 0.959 \times 0.8825 \\
     -0.959 \times 0.8825 + 1.581 \times 0.8825
     \end{pmatrix}.
     \]
     Factoring out \(0.8825\):
     \[
     \mathbf{v}
     =
     0.8825 \times
     \begin{pmatrix}
     1.581 - 0.959 \\
     -0.959 + 1.581
     \end{pmatrix}
     =
     0.8825 \times
     \begin{pmatrix}
     0.622 \\
     0.622
     \end{pmatrix}
     \approx
     \begin{pmatrix}
     0.549 \\ 
     0.549
     \end{pmatrix}.
     \]

  2. Then,
     \[
     \mathbf{k}_*(0.5)^\top\,\mathbf{v}
     =
     (0.8825,\; 0.8825)
     \begin{pmatrix}
     0.549 \\
     0.549
     \end{pmatrix}
     =
     0.8825 \times 0.549 + 0.8825 \times 0.549
     =
     2 \times 0.8825 \times 0.549
     \approx 
     0.969.
     \]

- Therefore:
  \[
  \sigma^2(0.5) 
  = 
  1 \;-\; 0.969 
  = 
  0.031.
  \]

Hence, the posterior **variance** at \(x_*\) is \(0.031\), and the standard deviation is about \(\sqrt{0.031} \approx 0.176\).

---

## **Summary of Final GP Prediction**

- **Posterior Mean** at \(x_* = 0.5\): \(\;\approx 0.989\).  
- **Posterior Variance** at \(x_* = 0.5\): \(\; \approx 0.031 \; (\text{std} \approx 0.176).\)

We see that, according to this mini GP model and the highly correlated training points, the model fairly confidently predicts a value close to **0.99** in between the training points (0,1) with observed outputs 1.0 and 0.8.

---

### Why This Illustrates GP Regression

1. We started with only two observations.  
2. We modeled them as being drawn from a Gaussian Process with a chosen **kernel** (RBF).  
3. Using the GP formulas, we found the **posterior distribution** over possible function values at a new location \(x_* = 0.5\).  
4. We get not only a **mean prediction** (\(\approx 0.99\)) but also an **uncertainty** measure (variance \(= 0.031\))—a core advantage of GPs.

You can expand this approach to more data points, different kernels, noise terms, and multi-dimensional inputs. The basic linear-algebra steps—forming the covariance matrix, inverting it, and using the GP posterior equations—remain the same.

Yes, it is possible that a group of predictors—each of which appears weak individually in a SHAP summary plot—can be jointly informative when taken together in a model. Here's why:


---

1. Interaction Effects

SHAP summary plots primarily show main effects (i.e., how much each feature contributes on average across all predictions).

But predictors might interact in nonlinear ways: while feature A alone and feature B alone don’t have much impact, their combination (e.g., A * B, or A when B is above a threshold) may drive predictions.

SHAP does support interaction values, but a standard summary plot doesn't reveal them.



---

2. Redundancy and Redundancy Reduction

Several weak features might be correlated or mildly redundant, and together they may form a stronger latent signal (e.g., multiple noisy signals of socioeconomic status).

A single feature may be too noisy or partial to be useful, but aggregated signals can reinforce one another.



---

3. Nonlinear Models Can Exploit Joint Patterns

Tree-based models like LightGBM, XGBoost, and Random Forests can exploit combinations of features through splits across multiple columns.

For example, if x1 is only predictive when x2 > 5, a tree model will learn this conditional dependency even if x1 alone seems useless.



---

4. SHAP Can Underrepresent Grouped Effects

SHAP values are additive and attributed per feature, so they may understate contributions from feature sets that only matter together.

The total model gain from a group of features may be greater than the sum of their SHAP values shown independently.



---

How to Investigate Further

Use SHAP interaction values (shap.TreeExplainer(model).shap_interaction_values(X)) to see pairwise synergies.

Try PCA or feature clustering to find if these features form a useful low-dimensional space.

Compare model performance with and without the group of weak features.

Test grouped feature importances using permutation importance on the whole group.



---

Bottom line:
Just because each predictor appears weak alone doesn't mean the group lacks predictive power. Modeling is often about structure in combinations, not isolated signals.

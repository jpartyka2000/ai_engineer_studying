Below is a **small, concrete example** with just two training points illustrating how \(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\) “de-correlates” them when weighting their contributions to a Gaussian Process posterior. We’ll see in the final weights that **high correlation** between points forces the GP to “downweight” each point’s unique contribution relative to the case where they would be uncorrelated.

---

## 1. Setup

### Training Points
Let’s say we have two training inputs:
\[
\mathbf{x}_1,\;\mathbf{x}_2 
\]
and two corresponding observed outputs:
\[
y_1,\; y_2.
\]
We collect them into vectors:
\[
\mathbf{X} = \{\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2\}, 
\quad 
\mathbf{y} = \begin{pmatrix}y_1 \\ y_2\end{pmatrix}.
\]

### Covariance Matrix \(\mathbf{K}\)
For a Gaussian Process, we have a kernel function \(k(\cdot,\cdot)\) giving rise to a covariance matrix \(\mathbf{K}\) among these points:
\[
\mathbf{K}(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{X}) 
= 
\begin{pmatrix}
k(\mathbf{x}_1,\mathbf{x}_1) & k(\mathbf{x}_1,\mathbf{x}_2) \\
k(\mathbf{x}_2,\mathbf{x}_1) & k(\mathbf{x}_2,\mathbf{x}_2)
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

#### Example Numerical Values
Let’s pick a simple scenario:
\[
\mathbf{K}(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{X}) 
= 
\begin{pmatrix}
1.0 & 0.8 \\
0.8 & 1.0
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
- Diagonal entries are each 1.0, indicating each point is perfectly correlated with itself.
- Off-diagonal entries are 0.8, indicating **high correlation** between \(\mathbf{x}_1\) and \(\mathbf{x}_2\).

Finally, imagine the observed outputs:
\[
\mathbf{y} 
= 
\begin{pmatrix}
1.0 \\
1.0
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

---

## 2. Weighting by \(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\)

When making predictions at a new point (in a noiseless GP for simplicity), the **posterior mean** at a test location \(\mathbf{x}_*\) can be written as:

\[
\mu(\mathbf{x}_*) 
= 
\mathbf{k}_*(\mathbf{x}_*)^\top \,\mathbf{K}^{-1}\,\mathbf{y},
\]

where \(\mathbf{k}_*(\mathbf{x}_*)\) is the vector of covariances between \(\mathbf{x}_*\) and the training points, and 
\(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\mathbf{y}\) gives the effective *weights* for each observation \(y_i\).

### Calculate \(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\)
For our \(2\times2\) example,
\[
\mathbf{K} 
= 
\begin{pmatrix}
1.0 & 0.8 \\
0.8 & 1.0
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The determinant is:
\[
\det(\mathbf{K}) = 1.0 \times 1.0 - 0.8 \times 0.8 = 1 - 0.64 = 0.36.
\]
The inverse of a \(2\times2\) matrix \(\begin{pmatrix}a & b \\ b & a\end{pmatrix}\) is
\[
\mathbf{K}^{-1}
=
\frac{1}{a^2 - b^2}
\begin{pmatrix}
a & -b \\
-b & a
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Thus,
\[
\mathbf{K}^{-1}
=
\frac{1}{0.36}
\begin{pmatrix}
1.0 & -0.8 \\
-0.8 & 1.0
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
2.777\ldots & -2.222\ldots \\
-2.222\ldots & 2.777\ldots
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

### Multiply by \(\mathbf{y}\)
Now compute 
\[
\mathbf{\alpha} 
= 
\mathbf{K}^{-1}\mathbf{y}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
2.777\ldots & -2.222\ldots \\
-2.222\ldots & 2.777\ldots
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
1.0 \\
1.0
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
2.777 - 2.222 \\
-2.222 + 2.777
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
0.555\ldots \\
0.555\ldots
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
So, each training point’s output gets a final “weight” of about \(0.555\). Notice that **both are less than 1.0**, which indicates that the GP is *downweighting* each individual point because they provide largely **redundant information** (they are 80% correlated in the kernel). 

> **If** \(\mathbf{K}\) were the identity matrix (no correlation), then \(\mathbf{K}^{-1} = \mathbf{I}\), and the weights would just be \(\mathbf{y} = (1,1)^\top\). That would mean each point is counted “fully,” with weight = 1. But here, the high correlation 0.8 means “We’ve basically seen the same information twice,” so each point's unique contribution is smaller.

---

## 3. Interpretation: De-Correlation

- The off-diagonal \((0.8)\) entries in \(\mathbf{K}\) say: “\(y_1\) and \(y_2\) are strongly correlated—knowing one tells me a lot about the other.”  
- By inverting \(\mathbf{K}\), we **cancel out** the parts of \(y_1\) that are explained by \(y_2\) (and vice versa).  
- The resulting weights \(\mathbf{\alpha}\) reflect each training point’s *unique* information—no double-counting.

Hence, **\(\mathbf{K}^{-1}\) “de-correlates” the training data** by adjusting their weights to avoid overemphasizing points that are highly redundant in the kernel space.

---

## 4. Key Takeaway

This example, though tiny, illustrates the essential idea: **when two data points are strongly correlated, the inverse covariance matrix reduces each point’s direct influence.** This is precisely how Gaussian Processes avoid double-counting redundant observations and maintain a sensible posterior mean.

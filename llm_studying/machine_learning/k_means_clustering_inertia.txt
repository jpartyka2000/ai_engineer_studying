In the context of **K-Means clustering**, **inertia** is a measure of how well the clusters fit the data. It is defined as the **sum of squared distances** of each data point to the centroid of its assigned cluster:

\[
\text{Inertia} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \| x_i - c_{\text{cluster}(x_i)} \|^2
\]

where:
- \( x_i \) is a data point,
- \( c_{\text{cluster}(x_i)} \) is the centroid of the cluster that \( x_i \) is assigned to,
- \( \| \cdot \|^2 \) represents the squared Euclidean distance.

### **Key Properties of Inertia:**
1. **Lower inertia is better** – A smaller value indicates that the data points are closer to their cluster centroids, meaning the clustering is tight.
2. **Not a perfect metric** – It tends to decrease as the number of clusters (\( k \)) increases, which can lead to overfitting.
3. **Used in the Elbow Method** – Inertia is often plotted against \( k \) to determine the optimal number of clusters.

### **Example in Scikit-Learn**
```python
from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
import numpy as np

# Generate random data
X = np.random.rand(100, 2)

# Fit K-Means
kmeans = KMeans(n_clusters=3, random_state=42)
kmeans.fit(X)

# Get inertia
print("Inertia:", kmeans.inertia_)
```

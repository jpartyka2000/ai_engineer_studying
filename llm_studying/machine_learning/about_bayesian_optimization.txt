### Why Bayesian Optimization Improves Over Time
Bayesian optimization is an iterative process that aims to find the optimal solution (in this case, the best set of predictors) using **informed guesses** rather than random search. It leverages past evaluations to make increasingly better decisions. Here's how it works and why it improves over time:

1. **Probabilistic Model**:
   - Bayesian optimization uses a probabilistic model, typically a **Gaussian Process (GP)**, to model the relationship between the feature subsets and the performance of your model (e.g., accuracy, recall, or F1 score).
   - Initially, the optimizer knows very little about which features are the best, so its predictions are mostly driven by the prior assumptions.

2. **Exploration and Exploitation**:
   - At each iteration, Bayesian optimization balances **exploration** (trying new subsets of features) and **exploitation** (focusing on areas that have shown promise in previous iterations).
   - In early iterations, it tends to explore a variety of feature subsets to gather information.
   - As more data points are collected, it gets better at **exploiting** the feature combinations that seem to perform well.

3. **Using Past Results to Make Better Predictions**:
   - After each evaluation, Bayesian optimization updates its model to incorporate the new data point (i.e., the performance score for a specific set of features).
   - The updated model allows it to make more informed decisions about which feature subsets to test next.
   - Over time, it becomes more likely to choose subsets that have a higher probability of yielding better results, based on what it has learned so far.

4. **Convergence to the Optimal Solution**:
   - As iterations continue, the optimization process **converges** to a solution that is close to the best possible set of predictors.
   - The more iterations you run, the better it becomes at fine-tuning the feature subset to maximize model performance.
   - However, Bayesian optimization is generally quite efficient, meaning it can find near-optimal solutions in fewer iterations compared to other methods like random search or grid search.

### Example: Bayesian Optimization for Feature Selection
In your case, you're using Bayesian optimization to select the best set of predictors for a given model (e.g., `ExtraTreesClassifier`). Here’s what happens over the iterations:

- **Initial Iterations**: The optimizer tries different combinations of features, gathering information about how each subset performs.
- **Mid Iterations**: As it learns which features contribute positively to the model’s performance, it focuses more on subsets that include those features.
- **Later Iterations**: The optimizer refines its selection to maximize the model’s performance, based on the accumulated knowledge of which features are beneficial and which are not.

### Visualizing the Learning Process
If you plot the best scores obtained over the iterations, you'll often see a **learning curve** where the performance improves as the optimization progresses. The curve may plateau once the optimizer converges on a near-optimal solution.

### Practical Considerations
- **Number of Iterations**: The performance typically improves with more iterations, but there are diminishing returns after a certain point.
- **Quality of the Initial Surrogate Model**: Bayesian optimization is effective, but its initial iterations may not be very accurate due to limited information. Over time, as it collects more data points, the surrogate model becomes more accurate.
- **Trade-Off**: If you run too few iterations, you might miss out on the best predictors. Running too many can be computationally expensive, though it generally pays off with better results.

### Conclusion
Yes, as Bayesian optimization continues, it indeed gets better at choosing better predictors due to its ability to learn from past evaluations and make more informed decisions over time. This is what makes Bayesian optimization particularly powerful for feature selection and hyperparameter tuning compared to brute force or random search methods.

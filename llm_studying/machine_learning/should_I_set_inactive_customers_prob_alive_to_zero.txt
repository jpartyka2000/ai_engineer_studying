In practice, you typically don't want to force a customer's probability of being "alive" to exactly 0—even if they appear inactive. Here’s why:

1. **Uncertainty and Measurement Error:**  
   Even if a customer hasn't renewed or appears inactive, there is always some uncertainty. The BG/NBD model, for example, gives you a probability that reflects that uncertainty. A value extremely close to 0 might be appropriate, but setting it to 0 ignores the possibility—even if very small—that the customer might reactivate.

2. **Downstream Calculations:**  
   Having an absolute zero probability can sometimes cause issues in downstream calculations (for example, when you multiply or take logs). It is often better to have a small “floor” value instead of 0.

3. **Model Interpretation:**  
   In many implementations of the BG/NBD model, inactive customers tend to have a probability very near 0, but not exactly 0. This reflects the idea that while they are highly unlikely to make another purchase, the model still leaves a small probability of reactivation.

### Best Practice

Allow the model to output very low probabilities when customers are inactive, but avoid manually setting them to 0 unless you have strong domain reasons and safeguards in your downstream processes. If needed, you can define a minimum threshold (say, 0.01 or 0.05) below which you consider a customer inactive, but maintain that value instead of 0.

So, rather than forcing a probability of 0, it's often more realistic to "inch it up" a bit to a very small number, which better reflects uncertainty and can be more robust in subsequent calculations.

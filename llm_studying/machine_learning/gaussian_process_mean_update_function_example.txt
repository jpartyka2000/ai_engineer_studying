Sure! Let's go step by step with a simple, concrete numerical example.

---

### **Problem Setup**
Suppose we are given **two training data points**:  

\[
X = [1, 3]
\]
\[
y = [2, 0]
\]

And we want to predict the function value at **a new point**:  

\[
x^* = 2
\]

We will use a **simple kernel function** (like an RBF kernel) and apply the **Gaussian Process mean update formula**:

\[
m_*(x) = K(x, X) K(X, X)^{-1} y
\]

---

### **Step 1: Compute Kernel Values**
We define the kernel function as:

\[
K(a, b) = e^{-\frac{(a - b)^2}{2}}
\]

Now, let's compute the kernel matrices:

#### **1. Compute \( K(X, X) \) (Kernel between training points)**
\[
K(X, X) =
\begin{bmatrix}
K(1,1) & K(1,3) \\
K(3,1) & K(3,3)
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
e^{-\frac{(1 - 1)^2}{2}} & e^{-\frac{(1 - 3)^2}{2}} \\
e^{-\frac{(3 - 1)^2}{2}} & e^{-\frac{(3 - 3)^2}{2}}
\end{bmatrix}
\]

\[
=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & e^{-2} \\
e^{-2} & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\]

Approximating \( e^{-2} \approx 0.1353 \):

\[
K(X, X) =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0.1353 \\
0.1353 & 1
\end{bmatrix}
\]

#### **2. Compute \( K(X, X)^{-1} \)**
Using a numerical inverse:

\[
K(X, X)^{-1} =
\begin{bmatrix}
1.0187 & -0.1378 \\
-0.1378 & 1.0187
\end{bmatrix}
\]

#### **3. Compute \( K(x^*, X) \) (Kernel between new point and training points)**
\[
K(x^*, X) =
\begin{bmatrix}
K(2,1) & K(2,3)
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
e^{-\frac{(2 - 1)^2}{2}} & e^{-\frac{(2 - 3)^2}{2}}
\end{bmatrix}
\]

Approximating \( e^{-0.5} \approx 0.6065 \):

\[
K(x^*, X) = \begin{bmatrix} 0.6065 & 0.6065 \end{bmatrix}
\]

---

### **Step 2: Compute Updated Mean**
Now, we compute:

\[
m_*(x^*) = K(x^*, X) K(X, X)^{-1} y
\]

First, compute:

\[
K(X, X)^{-1} y =
\begin{bmatrix}
1.0187 & -0.1378 \\
-0.1378 & 1.0187
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
2 \\
0
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
(1.0187 \times 2) + (-0.1378 \times 0) \\
(-0.1378 \times 2) + (1.0187 \times 0)
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
2.0374 \\
-0.2756
\end{bmatrix}
\]

Now multiply by \( K(x^*, X) \):

\[
m_*(x^*) = \begin{bmatrix} 0.6065 & 0.6065 \end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix} 2.0374 \\ -0.2756 \end{bmatrix}
\]

\[
= (0.6065 \times 2.0374) + (0.6065 \times -0.2756)
\]

\[
= 1.2355 - 0.1672
\]

\[
= 1.0683
\]

---

### **Final Answer**
The **updated mean function at \( x^* = 2 \)** is:

\[
m_*(2) \approx 1.07
\]

This is the GP's best guess at the function value at \( x = 2 \), given the training points \( (1,2) \) and \( (3,0) \).

---

### **Intuition Behind the Math**
- The updated mean **smoothly interpolates** between the given data points.
- The **kernel values determine how much influence each point has**.
- If \( x^* \) is closer to a training point, it gets **weighted more heavily**.

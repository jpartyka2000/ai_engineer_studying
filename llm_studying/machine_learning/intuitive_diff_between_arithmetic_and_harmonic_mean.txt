Think of the arithmetic mean as “sharing out” a total equally, whereas the harmonic mean is “sharing out” a rate or ratio over a fixed total.

* **Arithmetic mean**: If you have five apples and five people, you’d give each person $5/5=1$ apple.  It’s $\tfrac{x_1 + x_2 + \cdots + x_n}{n}$, so every value contributes equally to the sum.
* **Harmonic mean**: If you drive the same distance twice—once at 30 mph and once at 60 mph—your *average speed* over the whole trip isn’t $(30+60)/2=45$ mph, because you spend more time driving slowly.  Instead you use

  $$
    \text{HM} = \frac{2}{\tfrac1{30} + \tfrac1{60}} = 40\text{ mph}.
  $$

  Here each rate is “weighted” by how long it applies, so the slower speed pulls down the average more.

**Intuitively**: the arithmetic mean treats all values equally, but the harmonic mean treats all *units* equally (e.g. all miles or all “work units”), which gives more weight to smaller numbers.  That’s why it’s the right choice whenever you’re averaging rates, densities, or ratios over a shared total.

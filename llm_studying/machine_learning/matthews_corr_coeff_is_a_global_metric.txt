The Matthews Correlation Coefficient (MCC) is intrinsically a **single, global** measure of binary classification performance—there isn’t really a “per-class” MCC in the way you think of precision, recall or F1 per class. Here’s why:

---

## 1. MCC formula ties together all four confusion‐matrix cells

For binary labels \(y\in\{0,1\}\) and predictions \(\hat y\), MCC is defined as

\[
\text{MCC}
= \frac{ \;TP\times TN \;-\; FP\times FN\;}
       {\sqrt{(TP+FP)\,(TP+FN)\,(TN+FP)\,(TN+FN)}}.
\]

- **TP, TN, FP, FN** are all used.
- You get **one** numerator and **one** denominator → **one** MCC value.

Because it measures the *correlation* between the true labels and predicted labels across the **whole** dataset, you can’t isolate “MCC among positives” vs. “MCC among negatives” without throwing away half the data—and then you’d just end up back at class‐specific recall or precision.

---

## 2. Why you wouldn’t split it

- If you tried to compute MCC on just the positive class (i.e. looking only at true 1’s and false 1’s), you’d be ignoring the negatives completely—and your denominator would collapse (you’d be missing TN and FN), making the metric undefined or meaningless.  
- Likewise for the negative class. MCC only makes sense when you account for the balance of both classes.

---

## 3. What to use for per-class insights

If you need a **per-class** summary, stick with metrics that are naturally defined per label:

- **Precision\(_c\)** and **Recall\(_c\)** (sensitivity for positives, specificity for negatives).  
- **F1\(_c\)** (harmonic mean of precision\(_c\) & recall\(_c\)).  
- **Class‐conditional log‐loss** or **Brier score**.  

Those will give you two clear numbers (one for class 0, one for class 1). MCC remains your go-to **global** metric when you want a single threshold-dependent summary that balances all four confusion counts in one go.

It can be useful as an initial exploratory step, but it has some limitations. Pearson correlation measures only the linear relationship between a predictor and the outcome. Here are some points to consider:

- **Exploratory Insight:**  
  Calculating Pearson correlations helps you quickly identify predictors that have a strong linear association with the outcome, which might be a good starting point.

- **Non-linear Relationships:**  
  If the relationship between a predictor and the outcome is non-linear, Pearson correlation might not capture it effectively. In such cases, a predictor could be very useful in a model even if its Pearson correlation is low.

- **Multivariate Context:**  
  In a multivariate regression setting, predictors can interact or correlate with each other (multicollinearity), so the univariate Pearson correlation might not reflect the true predictive power when combined with other variables.

- **Complementary Analyses:**  
  It’s best to use Pearson correlation as one tool among many—complement it with visualizations (like scatter plots), feature importance metrics from models, and other methods (e.g., Spearman’s rank correlation or mutual information) to get a fuller picture.

In summary, performing Pearson correlation is a reasonable first step for screening predictor candidates, but it shouldn’t be the sole basis for feature selection in your regression model.

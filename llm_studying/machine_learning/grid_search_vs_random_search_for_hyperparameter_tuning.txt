Grid Search

Definition: Exhaustively evaluates your model’s performance at every point in a predefined, Cartesian “grid” of hyperparameter values.

How it works: You specify a set of candidate values for each hyperparameter (e.g. learning_rate = [0.01, 0.1, 1.0], max_depth = [3, 5, 7]). Grid search trains and evaluates your model on every possible combination (here, 3 × 3 = 9 runs).

Pros:

Guarantees that you’ll test the exact combinations you care about.

Easy to parallelize since each combination is independent.


Cons:

Exponential blow‑up: with  hyperparameters and  choices each, you need  runs.

Wasted effort: if some hyperparameters matter little, you still spend time on them.

Random Search

Definition: Samples hyperparameter combinations at random from specified distributions or value lists, for a fixed number of iterations.

How it works: You specify ranges or distributions (e.g. learning_rate ∼ Uniform(0.001, 1.0), max_depth ∼ {3,5,…,15}) and a budget . Random search draws  independent samples, trains, and evaluates for each.

Pros:

Scales better with dimensionality: you decide your compute budget up front (e.g. 50 trials), regardless of how many hyperparameters you have.

More efficient when only a few hyperparameters really matter—Bergstra & Bengio (2012) showed that random search often finds better models in less time .

Can explore continuous spaces more naturally (no need to discretize finely).


Cons:

No guarantees you’ll hit a specific combination you might be curious about.

Performance depends on your chosen sampling distributions.

Key Takeaway

Grid search is best when your hyperparameter space is low‑dimensional and you want exhaustive coverage of a few discrete options.

Random search is generally more efficient in higher dimensions or when you have a fixed compute budget—random trials are likely to explore the most influential hyperparameters more thoroughly.

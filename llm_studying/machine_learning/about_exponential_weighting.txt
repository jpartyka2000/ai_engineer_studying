This formula calculates a weight that grows exponentially as you get closer to the maximum iteration. Let me break it down:

**The formula: 2^(iteration - max + 3)**

**Components:**
- `iteration` - the current iteration number
- `max` - the maximum iteration number
- The exponent is `(iteration - max + 3)`
- The base is 2

**How it works:**

When `iteration` is far from `max`, the exponent is a large negative number, giving a very small weight. As `iteration` approaches `max`, the exponent increases, and the weight grows exponentially.

**Example:** Let's say `max = 10`

- iteration = 1: exponent = 1 - 10 + 3 = -6 → weight = 2^(-6) = 1/64 ≈ 0.016
- iteration = 5: exponent = 5 - 10 + 3 = -2 → weight = 2^(-2) = 1/4 = 0.25
- iteration = 8: exponent = 8 - 10 + 3 = 1 → weight = 2^1 = 2
- iteration = 10: exponent = 10 - 10 + 3 = 3 → weight = 2^3 = 8

**Why the +3?**

The "+3" ensures that even at the maximum iteration, you get a reasonably large weight (2^3 = 8 in the example). Without it, the max iteration would only have weight 2^0 = 1. This constant shifts the entire curve upward, giving more emphasis to later iterations.

**Common use case:**

This type of weighting is often used when you want recent iterations to matter much more than early ones, such as in iterative optimization, temporal smoothing, or prioritizing recent data in a sequence.

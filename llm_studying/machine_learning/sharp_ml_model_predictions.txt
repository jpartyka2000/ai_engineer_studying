In machine learning, sharpness refers to how confident a model's predicted probabilities are. A model's predictions are considered sharp if they tend to be close to 0 or 1 rather than being spread out in the middle range (e.g., around 0.5).

Key Characteristics of Sharp Predictions:

The model assigns very high probabilities (close to 1) to instances it believes strongly belong to the positive class.

It assigns very low probabilities (close to 0) to instances it believes strongly belong to the negative class.

It avoids making uncertain predictions (e.g., in the 0.4–0.6 range).


When is Sharpness Good?

If the model is well-calibrated, meaning that its probabilities align with actual outcomes (e.g., when it predicts 90%, the event occurs about 90% of the time), sharpness is a sign of a strong, confident model.

In high-stakes decision-making (e.g., fraud detection, medical diagnosis), a sharp and well-calibrated model can be valuable.


When is Sharpness Bad?

If the model is overconfident but incorrect, it may assign extreme probabilities when the true uncertainty is high.

Poor calibration leads to unreliable confidence levels (e.g., a model predicts 95% probability, but the actual event happens only 60% of the time).

Sharp predictions that are not well-calibrated can hurt performance in downstream tasks, such as expected loss minimization.


Measuring Sharpness:

Reliability diagrams (calibration plots): Compare predicted probabilities to actual frequencies.

Brier Score: Measures both sharpness and calibration, penalizing incorrect confident predictions.

Expected Calibration Error (ECE): Quantifies misalignment between confidence and observed frequencies.

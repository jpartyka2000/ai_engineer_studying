Not quite! While the **Kaplan-Meier estimator** is **non-parametric**, that doesn’t mean it has **zero parameters**—it just means that it doesn’t assume a specific **underlying probability distribution** for the data (like exponential, Weibull, or normal distributions).  

### **What Does "Non-Parametric" Mean in Kaplan-Meier?**  
- **Non-parametric** methods make minimal assumptions about the data’s shape or distribution.
- Instead of estimating parameters like a mean or standard deviation, Kaplan-Meier **directly calculates survival probabilities from observed data**.

### **Does Kaplan-Meier Require Any Parameters?**  
Not in the sense of "tunable parameters" like in parametric models, but it does require:  
1. **Time-to-event data** (e.g., time since signup until churn or last observed time).  
2. **Censoring indicators** (to account for customers who haven’t churned yet).  

### **Key Idea of Kaplan-Meier:**  
- It **estimates the probability** that a subject (e.g., a customer) **survives beyond a given time** based on empirical data.
- It calculates survival as:  
  \[
  S(t) = \prod_{t_i \leq t} \left( 1 - \frac{d_i}{n_i} \right)
  \]
  where:  
  - \( d_i \) = number of events (e.g., churns) at time \( t_i \)  
  - \( n_i \) = number of customers at risk just before \( t_i \)  

Since it **does not estimate parameters like λ in an exponential model**, it’s considered **non-parametric**.

### **Summary:**  
✔ **Non-parametric ≠ No parameters**  
✔ It doesn’t assume a specific survival distribution.  
✔ It requires event times and censoring info but no tunable parameters.

**Great question! This is a subtle but important point.**

You're confusing two different things:
1. **What happens during TRAINING** (building the policy)
2. **What the FINAL POLICY looks like** (after training is done)

## **SMILe is Stationary AFTER Training**

**Stationary means:** The policy doesn't change based on what timestep you're at.

### **Let me clarify what happens:**

## **During Training (Building the Policy)**

**Yes, things change iteration by iteration:**

```
Iteration 1: Mix = 100% expert
Iteration 2: Mix = 90% expert + 10% π(2)
Iteration 3: Mix = 80% expert + 10% π(2) + 10% π(3)
...
```

**But this is TRAINING, not execution!**

## **After Training (Final Policy)**

Once training is done, you have a **final fixed mixture**, say:

```
π_final = 30% π(2) + 30% π(3) + 40% π(4)
```

**This mixture is the SAME at every timestep during execution!**

### **At Execution Time:**

```
Timestep 1: Use π_final (30% π(2), 30% π(3), 40% π(4))
Timestep 2: Use π_final (30% π(2), 30% π(3), 40% π(4))  ← SAME!
Timestep 3: Use π_final (30% π(2), 30% π(3), 40% π(4))  ← SAME!
...
Timestep T: Use π_final (30% π(2), 30% π(3), 40% π(4))  ← SAME!
```

**The policy doesn't depend on t (the timestep) → STATIONARY**

## **Contrast with Forward Training (Non-Stationary)**

**Forward Training:**
```
Timestep 1: Use π(1)  ← Different policy!
Timestep 2: Use π(2)  ← Different policy!
Timestep 3: Use π(3)  ← Different policy!
...
Timestep T: Use π(T) ← Different policy!
```

**The policy DEPENDS on t → NON-STATIONARY**

## **The Key Difference**

**Stationary:** π(state) - doesn't care what time it is
**Non-Stationary:** π(state, t) - depends on current timestep

## **SMILe at Execution**

**Every timestep:**
1. Look at current state
2. Flip weighted coin to pick a sub-policy
3. Use that sub-policy
4. **The weights are the same at every timestep**

**Example execution:**
```
t=1, state=A: Flip coin → pick π(3) → action = "go left"
t=2, state=B: Flip coin → pick π(4) → action = "go straight"
t=3, state=C: Flip coin → pick π(2) → action = "go right"
```

**Different sub-policies chosen, but same mixture weights!**

## **Your Confusion**

You might be thinking:

"But the policy changes from π(2) to π(3) to π(4) as I execute!"

**Answer:** That's just the **randomness** of which sub-policy gets selected. The **distribution** (the weights) stays the same.

### **Analogy: Dice Rolling**

**Stationary (SMILe):**
- Roll a standard die every turn
- Distribution: {1,2,3,4,5,6} each with 1/6 probability
- You get different numbers, but the **die itself doesn't change**

**Non-Stationary (Forward Training):**
- Turn 1: Roll a 6-sided die
- Turn 2: Roll an 8-sided die
- Turn 3: Roll a 12-sided die
- The **die itself changes** each turn

## **Mathematical Definition**

**Stationary:** π(a|s) - probability of action given state only
**Non-Stationary:** π(a|s,t) - probability depends on state AND time

**SMILe:** π(a|s) = Σ w(i) × π(i)(a|s)
- Weights w(i) don't depend on t
- Policy π(i) choices don't depend on t
- Therefore: **STATIONARY**

**Forward Training:** Use π(t)(a|s) at timestep t
- Explicitly depends on t
- Therefore: **NON-STATIONARY**

## **Summary**

**SMILe is stationary because:**
- The **final mixture weights are fixed** after training
- You use the **same mixture distribution** at every timestep
- Only the **random selection** from that mixture varies
- The policy function doesn't take timestep as input

**The changes during training iterations are building the policy, not executing it!**

You can test the Poisson process assumption by examining two main aspects of your event data:

1. **Inter-Arrival Times:**  
   A key property of a Poisson process is that the times between successive events (i.e., inter-arrival times) are exponentially distributed. You can:
   - **Plot the Empirical Distribution:** Calculate the differences between successive purchase dates (or renewals) for each customer, then plot the histogram of these inter-arrival times and overlay the corresponding exponential probability density function.
   - **Use a Q-Q Plot:** Compare the quantiles of your inter-arrival times against those of an exponential distribution.
   - **Statistical Tests:** Perform tests like the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) test to formally assess whether your inter-arrival times differ significantly from an exponential distribution.

2. **Counts in Fixed Intervals:**  
   Another property is that the number of events in a fixed time interval should follow a Poisson distribution, where the mean is equal to the variance. You can:
   - **Aggregate Counts:** Divide your timeline into equal intervals (e.g., days, weeks, or months) and count the number of purchases/renewals per interval.
   - **Compare Mean and Variance:** Check whether the variance of these counts is roughly equal to the mean.
   - **Fit a Poisson Model:** Fit a Poisson distribution to the counts and use goodness-of-fit tests or plots (like a chi-square goodness-of-fit test or Q-Q plot) to see if the observed data align well with the Poisson assumption.

If your inter-arrival times are approximately exponential and your event counts per interval closely follow a Poisson distribution (with mean ≈ variance), then your data support the Poisson process assumption. If you find significant deviations, you may need to consider alternative models or account for additional factors (like time-varying rates) that violate the constant-rate assumption of a Poisson process.

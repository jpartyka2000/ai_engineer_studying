Exactly—each SAG “iteration” works on just **one** (randomly chosen) training example. Here’s the micro-step:

1. **Pick sample** $i$.
2. **Compute** its gradient $\nabla \ell_i(w)$.
3. **Replace** the old stored gradient $g_i$ with this new one.
4. **Update** the **running average** gradient

   $$
     \bar g \;\;=\;\;\frac1n\sum_{j=1}^n g_j
   $$

   (which you can maintain in $O(1)$ time by adding the new $g_i$ and subtracting the old).
5. **Step** the weights

   $$
     w \;\leftarrow\; w - \alpha\,\bar g
   $$

So yes—unlike batch GD (which recomputes the full gradient over **all** samples each epoch), SAG only evaluates **one** sample’s gradient per update, but still uses the **average** of all stored gradients to compute the descent direction.

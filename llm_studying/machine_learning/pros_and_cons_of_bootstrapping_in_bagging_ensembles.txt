Pros of Using Bootstrapping in Bagging

Variance Reduction via Diversity
By training each base learner on a different bootstrap sample (drawn with replacement), bagging creates a diverse ensemble whose averaged (or voted) prediction has substantially lower variance than any single model .

Built‑in Validation (OOB Estimation)
Each bootstrap leaves out about 36.8 % of the data on average. Those “out‑of‑bag” samples can be used to estimate generalization error without a separate validation split or cross‑validation .

Parallelizable Training
Since each tree (or base learner) is fit independently on its own bootstrap sample, you can train them in parallel—making full use of multicore CPUs or distributed clusters .

Works Well with Unstable Learners
Models that are highly sensitive to small changes in the data (e.g. decision trees, neural networks) benefit most from bagging, because bootstrapping forces them to explore different regions of the feature space .



---

Cons of Using Bootstrapping in Bagging

No Bias Reduction for High‑Bias Learners
If your base learner is biased (e.g. a shallow decision tree), bagging will not reduce that bias—you’ll simply average many biased models .

Reduced Effective Sample Size per Model
Each bootstrap sample contains on average only about 63.2 % unique observations (the rest are duplicates), so each base learner sees less data than the full training set—potentially weakening individual learners .

Increased Computational Cost
You must fit m separate models (often hundreds), which multiplies both CPU time and memory usage relative to a single model.

Loss of Interpretability
Aggregating many models makes it much harder to understand why the ensemble makes a particular prediction .

No New Information
Bootstrapping resamples only from your existing dataset; it cannot introduce any new variability beyond what’s already present. As a nonparametric approach, its statistical guarantees are limited to the “population” represented by your sample .



---

Bottom Line
Bootstrapping in bagging is a powerful, data‑efficient way to reduce variance and obtain an internal validation metric, especially for unstable learners. However, it comes with extra computational cost, doesn’t help with bias, and may produce ensembles that are harder to interpret.

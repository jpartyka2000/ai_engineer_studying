Yes, you can use a machine learning regression model to predict customer lifetime value (CLV), but there are some important trade-offs to consider.

### Advantages of Using ML Regression

- **Flexibility and Feature Integration:**  
  ML regression models (e.g., linear regression, random forests, or gradient boosting machines) can incorporate a wide range of features beyond the standard recency, frequency, and monetary value. This can include demographic data, engagement metrics, or any other variables that may influence customer behavior.

- **Capturing Non-linear Relationships:**  
  Many ML regression models can capture complex, non-linear relationships in your data that might be missed by traditional probabilistic models.

- **Direct Outcome Prediction:**  
  With regression, you can model the CLV (or revenue over a set period) as a continuous outcome directly, without having to combine separate models for frequency and monetary value.

### Disadvantages of Using ML Regression

- **Handling Censored Data:**  
  In CLV analysis, many customers are still active (their “true” lifetime value isn’t fully observed). Standard regression models don’t naturally handle this censoring, which can lead to biased predictions if not addressed properly.

- **Interpretability:**  
  Models like gradient boosting machines or neural networks, while powerful, are often considered “black boxes.” In contrast, models such as BG/NBD come with explicit probabilistic assumptions that provide more insight into customer behavior.

- **Data Requirements and Overfitting:**  
  ML regression models, especially complex ones, typically require a lot of high-quality data and careful feature engineering. They also run a higher risk of overfitting if not properly validated, particularly when the dataset is not large enough relative to the number of features.

- **Loss of Domain-Specific Insights:**  
  Probabilistic models like BG/NBD or Pareto/NBD are designed specifically for customer behavior in subscription and repeat purchase contexts. They incorporate the nature of customer churn and purchase frequency into the model. Switching to a general-purpose regression model may ignore these underlying dynamics unless you explicitly model them.

### Conclusion

While ML regression is certainly a viable approach for CLV prediction—especially when you have a rich set of features—it might not handle the censoring inherent in CLV data as naturally as specialized models (like the BG/NBD model). The choice depends on your data, the importance of interpretability, and whether you need a model that directly leverages domain-specific behaviors versus a more flexible, feature-rich approach.

Both approaches have their place, so it might even be worth comparing them on your data to see which one performs better for your specific use case.

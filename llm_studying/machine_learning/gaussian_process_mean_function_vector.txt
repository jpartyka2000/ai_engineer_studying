When you see a **mean function** \(m(x)\) in the context of Gaussian Processes, you can think of it in the following ways:

1. **Discrete Domain (Finite or Countably Infinite):**  
   - If your domain \(\mathcal{X}\) is \(\{x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n\}\) (finite) or \(\{x_1, x_2, x_3, \ldots\}\) (countably infinite), then \(m(x_i)\) is essentially the *mean* of the random variable corresponding to the function value \(f(x_i)\).  
   - **In the finite case**, you could represent the entire mean function as a *vector* \(\mathbf{m}\) of length \(n\), where each entry corresponds to \(m(x_1), m(x_2), \ldots, m(x_n)\).  
   - **In the countably infinite case**, it becomes an *infinite sequence*: \(m(x_1), m(x_2), \ldots\).  

2. **Continuous Domain (Uncountably Infinite):**  
   - If your domain \(\mathcal{X}\) is a continuous space like \([0,1]\) or \(\mathbb{R}^d\), then \(m(x)\) is truly a *function* that assigns a mean value to *every* possible \(x\).  
   - We often write this as \(m: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}\), meaning for each \(x\in\mathcal{X}\), \(m(x)\) is the mean of the random variable \(f(x)\).  
   - In practice, we don’t store an “infinite vector” for all real values of \(x\). Instead, we keep a functional representation (often assumed to be constant, zero, or a simple parametric form) and work with it analytically.

---

### Finite (Discrete) Example

- **Domain**: \(\mathcal{X} = \{x_1, x_2, x_3\}\)  
- **Mean function**: 
  \[
  m(x_i) = \mu_i \quad\text{for each } i=1,2,3.
  \]  
- **Vector representation**:
  \[
  \mathbf{m} = 
    \begin{pmatrix}
      m(x_1) \\
      m(x_2) \\
      m(x_3)
    \end{pmatrix}
    = 
    \begin{pmatrix}
      \mu_1 \\
      \mu_2 \\
      \mu_3
    \end{pmatrix}.
  \]

In this scenario, the “mean function” is simply a finite vector of mean values, one for each domain element.

---

### Continuous Example

- **Domain**: \(\mathcal{X} = [0,1]\)  
- **Mean function**: 
  \[
  m(x) = \mu(x) \quad \text{for all } x \in [0,1].
  \]  
- **Functional representation**: You can’t enumerate all real numbers, so you store or define \(m(x)\) by a function. A simple assumption might be \(m(x) = 0\), or \(m(x) = c\) (a constant), or even \(m(x) = a + b\,x\).

Here, we no longer have a finite or countably infinite *list*, but a *continuous map*. Gaussian Process theory ensures we handle this as an *infinite-dimensional object*—though in practice, we only explicitly evaluate \(m(\cdot)\) at the **specific** points where we need it.

---

### Takeaways

- **Yes, in a discrete domain, you can literally store the mean function as a vector** where each element corresponds to \(m(x_i)\).  
- **In a continuous domain, \(m(x)\) is formally a function** that assigns a mean value to *every* point \(x\). This conceptually mirrors an *uncountably infinite dimensional* vector, which is why GPs are sometimes described as “infinite-dimensional Gaussians.”  

Hence, the short answer is:  
- For **each value of \(x\) in your domain**, \(m(x)\) is the mean of the random variable \(f(x)\).  
- In a **finite** domain, that can be handled as a literal vector of means.  
- In a **continuous** domain, it’s best described as a function, but the idea is the same: each domain point \(\to\) a mean value.

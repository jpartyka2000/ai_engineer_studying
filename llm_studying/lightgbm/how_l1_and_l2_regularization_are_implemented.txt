Even though LightGBM is a tree‑based method and doesn’t learn a global vector of coefficients like linear models do, it still learns a set of **leaf scores** $w_t^l$ (the output value at each leaf $l$ of each tree $t$).  Both L1 (lasso) and L2 (ridge) regularization in LightGBM are applied **directly** to those leaf scores via the `reg_alpha` and `reg_lambda` hyperparameters.

---

### 1. The regularized objective

LightGBM minimizes at each boosting iteration an approximate objective of the form

$$
  \mathcal{L}
  \;=\;
  \sum_{i=1}^n \Bigl[\ell\bigl(y_i, \hat y_i^{(t-1)} + f_t(x_i)\bigr)\Bigr]
  \;+\;
  \sum_{l=1}^{L_t} \Bigl[\tfrac12\,\lambda\,w_t^l{}^2 \;+\; \alpha\,|w_t^l|\Bigr]
$$

* $\ell$ is the per‑instance loss (e.g. logistic).
* $f_t$ is tree $t$’s prediction, which is constant $w_t^l$ on leaf $l$.
* The **second sum** is over all $L_t$ leaves in tree $t$:

  * **$\tfrac12\lambda\,w^2$** is an L2 penalty on each leaf weight ($\lambda=\mathtt{reg\_lambda}$).
  * **$\alpha\,|w|$** is an L1 penalty on each leaf weight ($\alpha=\mathtt{reg\_alpha}$).

---

### 2. How it changes split scoring

When LightGBM evaluates a candidate split, it computes for each side (left/right) the sums of gradients $G$ and Hessians $H$.  Without regularization, the **gain** from that split would be

$$
  \text{Gain}
  = \frac{1}{2}\Bigl(\tfrac{G_L^2}{H_L} + \tfrac{G_R^2}{H_R} - \tfrac{(G_L+G_R)^2}{H_L+H_R}\Bigr).
$$

With L2 (ridge), each $H$ is replaced by $H + \lambda$.  With L1 (lasso), you also subtract $\alpha$ from the gain if $|G| < \alpha$, and otherwise you shrink the optimal leaf weight by $\alpha$.  Concretely, the optimal weight for a leaf becomes

$$
  w^*
  = \begin{cases}
    \displaystyle
    \frac{-\bigl(\,|G|-\alpha\bigr)\,\mathrm{sign}(G)}{\,H + \lambda\,}
    & \text{if } |G| > \alpha,\\[6pt]
    0 & \text{otherwise.}
  \end{cases}
$$

That zeroing‐out when $|G|\le \alpha$ is exactly the lasso’s feature of driving small weights to zero.

---

### 3. Practical upshot

* **`reg_lambda`** (L2) stabilizes leaf weights—large $\lambda$ makes each $w^*$ smaller (more conservative splits).
* **`reg_alpha`** (L1) can force some leaf weights to **exactly zero**, effectively pruning small “bumps” in your tree’s predictions.
* You tune them exactly like you would in a linear model, but they operate on the **per‑leaf predictions** rather than on global feature coefficients.

---

So even though there’s no global “coefficient vector” in GBDTs, LightGBM implements ridge and lasso by **penalizing the size** (and in the L1 case the very existence) of each leaf’s output value.

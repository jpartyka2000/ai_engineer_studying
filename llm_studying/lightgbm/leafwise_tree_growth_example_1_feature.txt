Absolutely. Here's a simple example to illustrate leaf-wise (best-first) tree growth used in LightGBM, compared to the traditional level-wise growth used by XGBoost.


---

Scenario

Suppose we are building a decision tree on this tiny dataset:

Sample Feature X Target

A 2 10
B 3 12
C 8 25
D 9 27


Now imagine we're trying to predict Target from Feature X.


---

XGBoost: Level-wise Tree Growth

Level-wise adds splits evenly across all leaves at the same depth before going deeper.

Level 0 (root): Split on X < 6

Left child: A, B

Right child: C, D


Level 1:

Split left child (X < 2.5) -> A | B

Split right child (X < 8.5) -> C | D


This leads to a balanced tree, even if some branches don’t reduce loss much.


---

LightGBM: Leaf-wise Tree Growth

LightGBM evaluates all leaves and picks the one with the highest gain to split — not necessarily keeping tree levels balanced.

Step 1 (root): Same split, X < 6

Left: A, B

Right: C, D


Step 2: Let's say splitting the right leaf (C, D) gives higher gain (greater error reduction). LightGBM chooses that first:

Split right leaf: X < 8.5

C | D


Left (A, B) remains untouched


Step 3: Now, LightGBM might next split the left leaf if it finds high gain — or continue splitting the deeper leaf with highest remaining gain.

This leads to unbalanced but highly optimized trees.


---

Visualization

LightGBM Leaf-wise:

         Root
        /    \
     A,B      C,D
               \
                C | D

XGBoost Level-wise:

         Root
        /    \
     A,B      C,D
     /          \
   A | B        C | D


---

Takeaway

Leaf-wise: more aggressive, finds max gain, may overfit

Level-wise: more conservative, better generalization


You can control LightGBM's aggressiveness with parameters like num_leaves, min_data_in_leaf, and max_depth.

Yes—turning on `force_row_wise=True` **only** fixes which histogram‑building algorithm LightGBM uses; it does **not** turn off threading or distributed training. Here’s roughly how it works under the hood:

1. **Data partitioned by rows across threads (or workers)**

   * LightGBM will still split your training set’s rows into chunks—one chunk per thread (for `n_jobs > 1`) or per machine (in a multi‑node run).
   * Each thread/worker processes its own slice of rows *in row‑order*, building a **local** row‑wise histogram over its chunk.

2. **Local histograms are merged**

   * Once every thread has scanned its rows and updated its local bin counts, LightGBM performs an efficient reduction (all‑reduce) to sum up those partial histograms into a **global** histogram for each feature.
   * That global histogram is then used to find the best split, just as in column‑wise mode.

3. **Other stages remain parallel**

   * Computing gradients/hessians, finding leaf indexes, updating predictions, and so on—all still happen in parallel across threads or machines.
   * Only the method of accumulating the histogram (row‑wise rather than column‑wise) has been locked in.

4. **Why it stays reproducible**

   * Because each thread’s row‑chunk assignment and local histogram loop order are deterministic given a fixed seed, and because the final merge is a simple summation, you avoid the non‑determinism of thread‑raced floating‑point reductions in the column‑wise builder. ([LightGBM Documentation][1])
   * You still get full multicore (and multi‑machine) speedups everywhere else in training.

---

**In short:**

* **`force_row_wise=True`** → makes the histogram pass use a row‑wise algorithm.
* **`n_jobs=-1`** (or a distributed setup) → still splits rows across threads/machines, so your training remains parallel and scalable while staying bit‑for‑bit reproducible.

Telling LightGBM “these columns are categorical” does more than just flag them as integers—it fundamentally changes *how* the tree‐builder searches for splits on those features:

1. **No arbitrary ordering**

   * If you leave a categorical feature encoded as 0,1,2,3 and *don’t* mark it as categorical, LightGBM will treat it as a continuous variable.
   * It will only ever split on conditions like `code ≤ 1.5` or `code ≥ 2`, implicitly imposing an ordering that may be meaningless (why is category “3” “larger” than “1”?).

2. **Subset‐based splitting**

   * When you *do* mark it categorical, LightGBM uses a specialized algorithm that:

     1. Sorts the categories by their average gradient,
     2. Finds the optimal **subset** of categories to send left vs. right in *one* pass,
     3. Doesn’t restrict you to just `<` or `>` splits on the numeric codes.
   * This is effectively a fast way of finding the best one‐hot‑style grouping of levels without blowing up memory.

3. **Better statistical splits**

   * By treating each level as its own group, you let the algorithm decide, “Should categories {A, C, F} go left and {B, D, E} go right?”
   * Versus if you leave them as ints, you only get splits like `{0,1,2}` vs `{3,4}` which might never align with the real signal.

4. **Performance & memory**

   * Native categorical splits are implemented in O(k log k) time (k = #levels) instead of a potential O(k²) brute force.
   * You avoid having to one‑hot encode (which can massively increase dimensionality) or target‑encode (which can leak).

---

### In short

* **Without** `categorical_feature`: your encoded integers get treated as numeric thresholds—Fast, but often *suboptimal* or even *misleading* if the codes aren’t truly ordinal.
* **With** `categorical_feature`: LightGBM runs its built‑in category‐aware split routine, giving you richer, more appropriate partitions of the levels and no forced ordering.

That’s why you almost always want to tell LightGBM which features are truly categorical, even if they’re stored as `int64` under the hood.

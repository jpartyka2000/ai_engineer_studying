Just as $\theta$ are the parameters of your **actor** $\pi_\theta(a\mid s)$, the vector $w$ are the parameters of your **critic**, i.e. your value‐function approximator.  In other words:

* You posit a parametric function $V_w(s)$ (usually a small neural network or “value head”) that takes a state $s$ and outputs a scalar estimate of the expected return.
* The weights of that network are collectively denoted by $w$.

So whenever you write

```python
logits, value = actor_critic_net(obs)
```

under the hood

* `logits = π_θ(a|s)` comes from the **actor** weights $\theta$,
* `value = V_w(s)` comes from the **critic** weights $w$.

During training you

1. **Update $\theta$** to maximize expected return via the policy gradient, using advantages computed with $V_w$.
2. **Update $w$** to minimize the squared TD error (or MSE against Monte Carlo returns), so that $V_w(s)$ becomes a better predictor of true future reward.

In short:

> **$\theta$** parameterizes **“what actions should I take?”**
> **$w$** parameterizes **“how good is this state (or state–action)?”**

and $V_w(s)$ is simply the critic’s learned estimate of the discounted-future reward from state $s$.

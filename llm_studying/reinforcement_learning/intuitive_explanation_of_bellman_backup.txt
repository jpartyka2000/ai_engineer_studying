Think of a “backup” like this:

1. **In the Bellman equation**

   $$
     V(s) \;=\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}\bigl[r(s,a)+\gamma\,V(s')\bigr]}_{\text{one‑step return}}
   $$

   you’re using **values at the next state** $V(s')$ to compute a new estimate for the **current** state $V(s)$.

2. **Why “backup”?**

   * In **dynamic programming**, information (value) is propagated “backward” from future states to earlier ones.
   * You first look “forward” one step—what rewards and successor‑state values lie ahead—then you **pull** (or backup) that information into your estimate of $V(s)$.

3. **Analogy: water flowing upstream**

   * Imagine pouring water at the leaves of a tree (successor states).
   * Gravity pulls it down toward the root (earlier states).
   * A Bellman backup is like collecting (averaging/maxing) the flows from children to update the parent.

4. **Forward vs. backup**

   * **Forward**: simulating or sampling one step ahead (you go from $s$ to $s'$).
   * **Backup**: using what you learned at $s'$ to revise your belief about $s$.

So yes, you “step forward” to see rewards and next‐state values, but then you **step backward** to update the **current** state’s value—hence the term **“Bellman backup.”**

There’s no magic “right” count here—it really depends on the algorithm and your variance/budget trade-offs.  Broadly speaking you have two extremes:

---

## 1. Vanilla REINFORCE (episodic Monte Carlo)

* **Minimum**: you could literally use **one trajectory** per gradient update—i.e. run one episode under πₜ, compute

  $$
    \hat\nabla_\theta J
    = \sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\,G_t
  $$

  and do θ←θ+α Ĝ.

* **Downside**: that estimator has huge variance, so learning is very noisy.

* **Common practice**: collect **B** full episodes (e.g. B=10, 20 or more), average their gradients
  $\displaystyle\frac1B\sum_{i=1}^B\hat\nabla J^{(i)}$,
  then update.  More episodes ⇒ smoother gradient but more samples per update.

---

## 2. Actor–Critic / n-step methods

* Rather than waiting for full returns, you can update **every step** or every n steps using bootstrapped targets (the critic).
* Here you typically run your policy for a fixed number of **timesteps** (say 1,000 or 2,048) across all agents/environments, then compute advantage estimates for those transitions and do one (or several) SGD passes.

  * E.g. PPO often uses **2,048** steps per actor per update, split into mini-batches of 64–256.
  * A2C might use **20** steps of each of 16 workers (320 total) before updating.

---

## So: how many?

1. **As few as one** (vanilla MC), but variance will bite you.
2. **A small batch of full episodes** (tens) to stabilize Monte Carlo.
3. **A fixed number of timesteps** (hundreds to thousands) in actor-critic, with multiple minibatch passes.

---

### Bottom line

* **Episodes** are the natural unit for pure REINFORCE: you choose a batch size B of episodes per update.
* **Timesteps** are the unit for actor-critic/PPO: you choose a rollout horizon T (and then minibatch size).

You tune B or T (and your learning rate) until you get acceptable stability vs.\ sample efficiency on your problem.

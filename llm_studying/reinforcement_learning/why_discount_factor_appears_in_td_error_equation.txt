Because the value function $V(s)$ is defined as the **expected discounted sum of future rewards**, any reward you expect to get **after** the very next step must be **discounted** by $\gamma$.

---

### 1. Definition of the value function

By definition, for a given policy $\pi$,

$$
V^\pi(s_t) \;=\;\mathbb{E}\Bigl[\;r_{t+1} \;+\;\gamma\,r_{t+2}\;+\;\gamma^2\,r_{t+3}+\cdots
\;\Big|\;s_t\Bigr].
$$

Notice that $r_{t+1}$ appears with no discount, but $r_{t+2}$ is multiplied by $\gamma$, $r_{t+3}$ by $\gamma^2$, and so on.

---

### 2. One‐step TD target

When we form the **one‐step bootstrap** target for $V(s_t)$, we split that infinite sum into two pieces:

1. **Immediate reward:** $r_{t+1}$
2. **All later rewards:** $r_{t+2} + \gamma\,r_{t+3} + \cdots$

By definition of $V$, the second part is exactly $V(s_{t+1})$.  But recall in the original sum it was multiplied by $\gamma$.  Hence the target is

$$
r_{t+1} \;+\;\gamma\,V(s_{t+1}).
$$

---

### 3. Intuition

* **Immediate payoff** $r_{t+1}$ is “worth” itself right now.
* **Future payoff** starting from $s_{t+1}$ is inherently **uncertain** and **less valuable** today—so we multiply it by $\gamma<1$ to reflect time‐preference or risk.

If you didn’t include the $\gamma$, you’d be overvaluing all future rewards as if they occurred at the present moment.

---

### 4. Summary in the TD error

$$
\delta_t 
= \underbrace{r_{t+1} + \gamma\,V(s_{t+1})}_{\text{bootstrapped target}}
\;-\;V(s_t).
$$

That discount on $V(s_{t+1})$ is what keeps your updates **consistent** with the original definition of $V^\pi$ as a **discounted** sum of future rewards.

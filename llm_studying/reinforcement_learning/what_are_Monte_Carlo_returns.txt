They’re called “Monte Carlo” returns because you’re literally using **random sampling** of whole trajectories—just like Monte Carlo integration—to estimate the true expected return.  Concretely:

1. **Full‐episode rollouts**
   You run your policy from start to finish (until terminal) to collect one trajectory $\tau$.

2. **Compute the return**
   You sum (and discount) all rewards in that single sampled trajectory:

   $$
     G_t = r_{t+1} + \gamma\,r_{t+2} + \gamma^2\,r_{t+3} + \cdots.
   $$

3. **Average over many trajectories**
   To estimate the **expected** return $V^\pi(s)$ or the **policy gradient**, you repeat this tens, hundreds, or thousands of times and take the empirical average.

Because you’re approximating an expectation by **averaging independent random samples** of complete returns, it’s exactly the **Monte Carlo** method from numerical analysis.  That’s in contrast to **Temporal-Difference** methods, which bootstrap (mix sampling with existing value estimates) rather than sampling out to the end of the episode.

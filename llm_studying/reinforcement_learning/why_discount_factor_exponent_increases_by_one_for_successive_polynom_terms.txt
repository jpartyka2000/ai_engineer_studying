The reason the discount factor’s exponent grows by one with each future reward term is exactly to reflect **how “far” into the future** that reward occurs, and to **down‐weight** rewards the further out they are.  Concretely:

1. **Immediate reward**

   $$
   G_t = r_{t+1} + \cdots
   $$

   has no discount on the very next reward—$\gamma^0 = 1$.

2. **One step later**

   $$
   + \;\gamma\,r_{t+2}
   $$

   you multiply by one factor of $\gamma$, because it’s one step further in time.

3. **Two steps later**

   $$
   + \;\gamma^2\,r_{t+3}
   $$

   you multiply by $\gamma^2$, applying another “discount” for that extra lag.

---

### Intuitive analogies

* **Money today vs tomorrow**
  If you value \$1 today as \$1, you might value \$1 one day from now as only $\gamma$ dollars today, and \$1 two days out as $\gamma^2$ today—because of interest rates, inflation, or just “having to wait.”

* **Diminishing importance**
  You care most about what happens immediately, a bit less about what happens next step, and progressively less the farther into the future you look.  By raising $\gamma\in[0,1)$ to higher powers, you create a smooth geometric decay of weight.

---

### Mathematical rationale

The return is defined as

$$
G_t \;=\;\sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k\,r_{t+1+k}.
$$

* **$k=0$** ⇒ $\gamma^0 r_{t+1}$ (immediate reward)
* **$k=1$** ⇒ $\gamma^1 r_{t+2}$ (one-step future)
* **$k=2$** ⇒ $\gamma^2 r_{t+3}$ (two-step future)
* … and so on.

That exponent $k$ exactly counts “how many time‐steps ahead” each reward is, ensuring each successive reward is multiplied by one more factor of $\gamma$.

In the standard RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) pipeline, the **reward model** is the component that translates human judgments (“which of these two completions do you prefer?”) into a continuous numeric signal that can drive policy optimization. Concretely:

---

## 1. What it is

* A **neural network** (often based on the same architecture as your base LLM) that, given:

  1. A **prompt** $x$, and
  2. A **candidate completion** $y$,
     returns a scalar **reward score** $r(x,y)\in\mathbb{R}$.

* You train it on **comparison data**: human labelers see two completions $y^+$ and $y^-$ for the same prompt $x$ and indicate which they prefer.

---

## 2. How it’s trained

1. **Collect comparisons**

   * For each prompt $x$, gather a pair of model-generated responses $(y^+, y^-)$ with a human preference label (“+” or “–”).

2. **Formulate a loss**

   * Use a **pairwise ranking loss** (e.g. a Bradley–Terry or logistic loss) so that the reward model assigns higher scores to preferred completions:

     $$
     \mathcal{L}_{\text{RM}} = -\log \sigma\!\bigl(r(x,y^+) - r(x,y^-)\bigr)
     $$
   * Here $\sigma$ is the sigmoid, and you drive $r(x,y^+)\!) >\! r(x,y^-)$.

3. **Optimize**

   * Fine-tune your reward network’s parameters via gradient descent on this loss over many (prompt, better, worse) triples.

---

## 3. Why it exists

* **Bridges human feedback & RL**: Humans give **ordinal** feedback (A preferred over B), but RL algorithms need a **scalar reward** signal. The reward model converts one into the other.

* **Generalizes**: Once trained, it can score *any* new prompt-completion pair, not just those you explicitly compared.

* **Stabilizes RL**: By freezing the reward model when you run your policy optimization (e.g. PPO), you get a fixed surrogate objective that the policy can chase, avoiding direct reliance on noisy or expensive human labels.

---

## 4. How it fits in the pipeline

1. **Supervised pre-training** (optional)
2. **Collect comparisons** from labelers
3. **Train the reward model** on that data
4. **Run policy optimization** (e.g. PPO) on your policy model, using the reward model’s outputs $r(x,y)$ as the scalar reward signal
5. **Validate** with held-out comparisons and/or human checks

---

## 5. Alternatives

* **Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)**: bypasses a standalone reward model and policy optimization by directly fine-tuning the policy via a pairwise likelihood loss.
* **ReAct / chain-of-thought** scoring: some workflows prompt the model itself to self-score, but these are less robust than a learned reward model trained on human data.

---

### TL;DR

The **reward model** is a learned scoring function $r(x,y)$ that converts human A/B preferences into a continuous reward signal. You train it on human comparison data and then use its outputs to guide an RL-style fine-tuning of your policy (the actual LLM you’ll serve).

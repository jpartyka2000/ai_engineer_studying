Exactly—these terms refer to how many examples you process before each gradient‐update:

* **Batch** (or “full‐batch”) gradient descent means you use **the entire dataset** to compute your loss and take one update per epoch.
* **Mini-batch** gradient descent means you split your data into smaller chunks—**mini-batches**—and after computing the loss on each mini-batch you take an optimizer step.

So, if you have 10 000 training examples:

* **Batch size = 10 000** ⇒ 1 update per pass through the data.
* **Mini-batch size = 1 000** ⇒ 10 updates per pass (each on a different 1 000-example slice).
* **Mini-batch size = 1** (aka “stochastic” GD) ⇒ 10 000 updates per pass.

In PPO or A2C land, “batch” often means “all the rollout data we collected this iteration,” and you then sample many **mini-batches** from that rollout batch when doing your multiple‐epoch optimization. But the core idea is the same:

> **Batch = everything** you’ll use before updating;
> **Mini-batch = one subset** you actually compute a loss on and update with, repeating until you’ve covered the batch.

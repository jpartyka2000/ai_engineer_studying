Not at all—they don’t presuppose you already have $\pi^*$. Rather, they **define** what “optimal” means and give you a way to **compute** $\pi^*$ by solving those equations.

---

### What Eq. 3 & 4 actually are

1. **Bellman Optimality for $V^*$**

   $$
     V^*(s)
     = \max_{a}\,\mathbb{E}_{s'\sim P(\cdot\mid s,a)}\bigl[r(s,a) + \gamma\,V^*(s')\bigr].
   $$

   * This is a **self‑consistency** (fixed‑point) equation for the *optimal* state‑value function.
   * It doesn’t plug in some given $\pi^*$; it instead *characterizes* $V^*$ as the solution to a max‑plus‑expectation recursion.

2. **Bellman Optimality for $Q^*$**

   $$
     Q^*(s,a)
     = \mathbb{E}_{s'\sim P(\cdot\mid s,a)}\Bigl[r(s,a) + \gamma\,\max_{a'}Q^*(s',a')\Bigr].
   $$

   * Again, no $\pi^*$ on the right.  Instead the inner $\max_{a'}$ corresponds to “from the next state onward, you’ll behave optimally.”

---

### How you actually get $\pi^*$

Once you **solve** for $V^*$ or $Q^*$ (e.g. via value iteration, policy iteration, or Q‑learning), you simply extract:

* **From $V^*$:**
  $\displaystyle \pi^*(s) = \arg\max_a \mathbb{E}[\,r(s,a) + \gamma\,V^*(s')]$.
* **From $Q^*$:**
  $\displaystyle \pi^*(s) = \arg\max_a Q^*(s,a)$.

So the Bellman optimality equations are **not** assuming you know the best policy—they’re the **tool** you use to **discover** it.

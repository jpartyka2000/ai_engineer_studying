In reinforcement learning, the key distinction between **model‐based** and **model‐free** methods is whether (and how) the agent learns or uses an explicit model of the environment’s dynamics.

---

## 1. What’s a “model”?

A **model** in RL is a representation of the environment’s transition and reward functions:

* **Transition model** $P(s' \mid s,a)$: “If I’m in state $s$ and take action $a$, what’s the chance I’ll end up in each next state $s'$?”
* **Reward model** $r(s,a,s')$: “What immediate reward will I get when that transition occurs?”

---

## 2. Model‑Based Methods

### How they work

1. **Learn (or are given) a model** of $P$ and $r$.
2. **Plan** by simulating “inside your head”—you can do dynamic programming, lookahead search, or tree‐search to evaluate consequences of actions.
3. **Extract a policy** from that planning step (e.g. by value‑iteration, policy iteration, Monte Carlo tree search).

### Pros

* **Sample efficient:** once you’ve learned a good model, you can reuse it to evaluate many hypothetical trajectories without new environment interactions.
* **Adaptable:** if your reward or goal changes, you can re‑plan without re‑training from scratch.

### Cons

* **Model learning is hard:** approximating $P$ and $r$ well in high dimensions can be data‑hungry and error‐prone.
* **Planning can be expensive:** deep trees, high‐dimensional states, or long horizons blow up computationally.

### Examples

* **Dyna‑Q**: interleaves real‐world steps, model‐learning updates, and planning backups.
* **MuZero**: learns its own dynamics and reward models in latent space, then does Monte Carlo tree search.

---

## 3. Model‑Free Methods

### How they work

* **Directly learn** value functions ($V$, $Q$) or policies ($\pi$) **without** ever explicitly building $P$ or $r$.
* Use **experience** $(s,a,r,s')$ to update your estimates via temporal‑difference (TD) backups or policy gradients.

### Pros

* **Simplicity:** no separate model‐learning step.
* **Scales** to complex, high‑dimensional tasks (with deep function approximators).

### Cons

* **Sample inefficient:** every new policy change requires fresh interaction data—no “imagine if…” rollouts.
* **Rigid:** if you change the reward or goals, you often have to retrain your value/policy networks.

### Examples

* **Q‑learning, SARSA** (value‐based).
* **Policy gradient methods** (REINFORCE, PPO, A2C/A3C, SAC).

---

## 4. Trade‑Offs & Hybrids

* **Model‑based** is great when environment samples are expensive (e.g., robotics).
* **Model‑free** excels when you have massive simulation power but don’t know dynamics a priori.
* **Hybrid approaches** (e.g. Dyna, MuZero) seek the best of both: learn a model where possible, but fall back on model‐free updates when the model isn’t trusted.

---

### Bottom Line

> **Model‑based** methods “think ahead” by learning and using an internal simulator.
> **Model‑free** methods “learn by doing,” directly shaping value functions or policies from real interactions.
> Choosing between them (or blending them) depends on how cheaply you can collect data, how hard it is to learn a faithful model, and how much planning you can afford.

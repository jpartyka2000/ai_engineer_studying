Here’s a deeper look at that Markov‐property statement:

> $$
> P\bigl(s_{t+1},\,r_{t+1}\;\big|\;s_t,\,a_t\bigr)
> \;=\;
> P\bigl(s_{t+1},\,r_{t+1}\;\big|\;s_{1:t},\,a_{1:t}\bigr)
> $$

---

### What the subscripts mean

* **$s_{1:t}$** denotes the entire **history** of states up through time $t$:

  $$
    s_{1:t} = (s_1, s_2, \dots, s_t).
  $$
* **$a_{1:t}$** likewise is the full action sequence up through $a_t$:

  $$
    a_{1:t} = (a_1, a_2, \dots, a_t).
  $$
* On the **left**, you condition only on the **current** state–action pair $(s_t,a_t)$.
* On the **right**, you’re (in principle) allowed to condition on **every** past state and action.

---

### Why include the full history on the right?

* The equation is saying: “Even *if* you knew the entire trajectory so far, the probability of the next $(s_{t+1},r_{t+1})$ would be **identical** to knowing just the current $(s_t,a_t)$.”
* In other words, **no additional past information** beyond $(s_t,a_t)$ can improve your prediction of what happens next.

---

### Intuition

1. **Non‑Markovian world**:
   If the right‐hand side depended on $s_{1:t-1}$ or $a_{1:t-1}$, that would mean the history *does* matter (it’s non‐Markov).

2. **Markovian world**:
   Once you’re in state $s_t$ and take action $a_t$, everything you need to know about the past is “summarized” in that current state.

---

### Key takeaway

> **The subscripts $1:t$** are just a compact way to say “all states and actions from time 1 through time $t$.”
> The Markov property asserts those don’t add any predictive power over using only the **most recent** $(s_t,a_t)$.

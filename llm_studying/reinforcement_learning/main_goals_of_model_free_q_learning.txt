Q‑learning is a classic model‑free, off‑policy TD algorithm whose core aims are:

1. **Learn the optimal action‑value function $Q^*(s,a)$**

   * Use the Bellman optimality backup

     $$
       Q(s,a)\;\gets\;Q(s,a)\;+\;\alpha\Bigl[r + \gamma\,\max_{a'}Q(s',a') - Q(s,a)\Bigr]
     $$

     to iteratively push your estimates toward the true $Q^*(s,a)$.
   * Once $Q^*$ is known, you can extract the optimal policy via
     $\pi^*(s)=\arg\max_a Q^*(s,a)$.

2. **Bootstrapping for sample efficiency**

   * Rather than waiting for a full episode return, Q‑learning uses the one‑step TD target $r + \gamma \max_{a'}Q(s',a')$.
   * This “bootstrapping” leverages existing estimates to accelerate learning from each transition.

3. **Off‑policy learning**

   * You can learn about the greedy $Q^*$ policy while following any exploratory behavior policy (e.g. ε‑greedy).
   * This decoupling of data collection from the target policy improves flexibility and efficiency.

4. **Convergence to optimality**

   * Under standard conditions (sufficient exploration, decaying learning rate, bounded rewards), Q‑learning provably converges to $Q^*$.
   * That guarantee makes it a cornerstone of model‑free RL theory.

5. **Balance exploration vs. exploitation**

   * Typically paired with an exploration strategy (ε‑greedy, Boltzmann) to visit all $(s,a)$ pairs often enough.
   * Ensures the algorithm doesn’t get stuck with inaccurate estimates by overly favoring early high‑reward actions.

6. **Scalability with function approximation**

   * In large or continuous state spaces, Q‑learning extends to Deep Q‑Networks (DQN) and variants, combining TD updates with experience replay and target networks for stability.

---

**In essence:** Q‑learning’s goal is to **boot‑ strap** its way to the **optimal action‑value function** using **off‑policy** samples, so you can derive an optimal policy without ever modeling the environment’s transition or reward functions.

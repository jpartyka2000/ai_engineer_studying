Exactly—think of the Bellman equation as alternating between two types of “nodes” in a decision tree:

1. **Decision nodes** (the agent’s choice):
   $\displaystyle \max_a[\dots]$
   Here the agent gets to pick which action $a$ to take, so we look for the **best** one.

2. **Chance nodes** (the environment’s response):
   $\displaystyle \mathbb{E}_{s'\sim P(\cdot\mid s,a)}[\dots]$
   Once you pick an action, the next state $s'$ is determined by **stochastic** environment dynamics. You can’t choose or “optimize” over those outcomes—you can only take the **average** (expectation) across them.

---

### In short

* **Max over actions** because **you** control which action to take.
* **Expect over next states** because the environment randomly “rolls the dice” on what $s'$ you end up in.

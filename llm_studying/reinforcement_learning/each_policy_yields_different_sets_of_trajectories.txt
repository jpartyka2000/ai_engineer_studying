Exactly—different policies induce different **distributions** (and even **supports**) over trajectories. Concretely:

1. **Same universe, different weights**

   * The set of *all* theoretically possible trajectories is determined by your MDP’s states, actions, and transition dynamics.
   * **Which** of those you actually see—and **how often**—depends on your policy $\pi$.

2. **Zero‐probability trajectories**

   * If $\pi(a\mid s)=0$ for some action $a$ in state $s$, then *any* trajectory that uses $(s,a)$ has $P(\tau\mid\pi)=0$.
   * A more exploratory policy will assign nonzero probability to many trajectories; a greedy policy may collapse to a small subset.

3. **Implications for $J(\pi)$**

   $$
     J(\pi)=\sum_{\tau}P(\tau\mid\pi)\,R(\tau)
   $$

   only averages over the trajectories that $\pi$ actually “visits” (i.e., those with nonzero probability under $\pi$).

4. **Policy improvement = reshaping the distribution**

   * When you optimize $\pi$, you’re shifting probability mass **away** from low‑reward trajectories and **toward** high‑reward ones.
   * Over time, the set of *frequently* sampled rollouts narrows onto those that yield the best returns.

---

**Bottom line:**

> Every policy $\pi$ defines its own $P(\tau\mid\pi)$. Changing $\pi$ not only reweights trajectories but can entirely rule some out (set their probability to zero) and open others up.

**Distinguishing $\pi$ and $\mu$ in Reinforcement Learning**

---

### $\pi$ (Policy)

* **Definition**: A policy $\pi$ is a function or distribution that maps states (or beliefs) to actions.
* **Notation**:

  $$
    \pi(a \mid s) \quad\text{or}\quad \pi(a \mid b)
  $$

  means “the probability of taking action $a$ given state $s$” (in an MDP) or given belief $b$ (in a POMDP).
* **Role**:

  * Drives the agent’s decision making.
  * Can be deterministic ($\pi(s)=a$) or stochastic ($\pi(a\mid s)\in[0,1]$).
* **Examples**:

  * **$\pi^*$** often denotes the *optimal* policy.
  * In policy gradient methods, we parameterize $\pi_\theta(a\mid s)$ and update $\theta$ to maximize expected return.

---

### $\mu$ (Initial/State Distribution)

* **Definition**: $\mu$ is typically used to denote a probability distribution over initial states (or more generally the state distribution under some policy or dynamics).
* **Notation**:

  $$
    \mu(s) \quad\text{or}\quad \mu_0(s)
  $$

  means “the probability that the process starts in state $s$.”
* **Role**:

  * Specifies where episodes begin.
  * Influences the training objective (e.g., average return across $\mu$).
* **Examples**:

  * In policy evaluation, the value function may be defined as

    $$
      V^\pi = \mathbb{E}_{s\sim\mu}\Bigl[\;V^\pi(s)\Bigr]
    $$

    averaging state values according to $\mu$.
  * Sometimes $\mu^\pi$ denotes the *stationary* distribution over states when following policy $\pi$.

---

### Why Not Use $\pi$ or $\mu$ for “Observed” vs. “True” States?

* **$\pi$** is reserved for *policies*, not states or observations.
* **$\mu$** labels *distributions*, not concrete state values.
* To distinguish true vs. partial information, standard notation is:

  * $s_t$: true (latent) Markov state
  * $o_t$: observation
  * $b_t$: belief state in POMDPs

---

#### **Bottom Line**

> • Use **$\pi$** for policies—how the agent chooses actions.
> • Use **$\mu$** for (initial or stationary) state distributions.
> • Don’t repurpose these symbols to denote “observed” versus “fully known” states—stick to $s, o, b$ for clarity.

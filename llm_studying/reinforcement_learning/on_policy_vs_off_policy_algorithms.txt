In a nutshell, the difference comes down to **whose experience** you learn from:

---

### On-Policy

* **Learns from the very same policy it’s trying to improve.**
* Think: “I’ll try actions according to policy π, measure how well π does, then tweak π.”
* **Example algorithms:**

  * **SARSA** (“State–Action–Reward–State–Action”)
  * **Policy-gradient methods** like REINFORCE or PPO
* **Pros:** What you learn matches exactly how you act.
* **Cons:** You can’t reuse old data generated under an older policy—every time you change π, you need fresh rollouts.

---

### Off-Policy

* **Learns from experience generated by any policy** (not necessarily the one you’re improving).
* Think: “I’ll watch data collected by someone else (or an old policy), then learn the best policy π\* from it.”
* **Example algorithms:**

  * **Q-Learning**
  * **Deep Q-Networks (DQN)**
  * **Soft Actor-Critic (SAC)**
* **Pros:** You can replay and reuse past experiences (even random ones), which makes learning sample-efficient.
* **Cons:** Learning must correct for the fact that the data wasn’t collected by your current policy (you need importance-sampling or other tricks to avoid bias).

---

#### A Simple Analogy

* **On-policy** is like practicing moves in chess by playing *your current opening* over and over, then adjusting your opening based on those games.
* **Off-policy** is like studying *grandmaster games* (even if they played different openings), and using that to improve your own opening.

---

**Key takeaway:**

* **On-policy** = learn only from *your* current behavior.
* **Off-policy** = learn from *any* past behavior (yours or others’).

Not quite—what

$$
s' \sim P(\,\cdot\mid s,a)
$$

means is simply:

> “The next state $s'$ is drawn from the **entire** distribution over successor states conditioned on $(s,a)$.”

The “$\cdot$” is just a **placeholder** for “whatever value $s'$ might take.”  When you want the probability of *that particular* successor $s'$, you write

$$
P(s'\mid s,a).
$$

You do **not** sum over multiple “ways” to reach $s'$—all of that is already encapsulated in the single conditional probability $P(s'\mid s,a)$.  If the environment has, internally, multiple stochastic sub‑mechanisms that could produce the same $s'$, then $P(s'\mid s,a)$ itself is the (marginal) probability that *any* of those mechanisms lands you in $s'$.

---

### TL;DR

* **Dot notation**: $P(\,\cdot\mid s,a)$ denotes the full distribution over *all* next‑state values.
* **Specific state**: $P(s'\mid s,a)$ is the probability mass (or density) assigned to that *one* state $s'$.
* **No extra summation needed**: the environment’s transition kernel $P(s'\mid s,a)$ already integrates out whatever hidden randomness there is.

Yes—that’s exactly the idea. We define the **performance objective** of any policy $\pi$ as its expected return

$$
J(\pi) \;=\;\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi}\bigl[R(\tau)\bigr].
$$

The **optimal policy** $\pi^*$ is then simply the one that maximizes this objective:

$$
\pi^* \;=\;\underset{\pi}{\arg\max}\;J(\pi)
\;=\;\underset{\pi}{\arg\max}\;\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim\pi}\bigl[R(\tau)\bigr].
$$

In other words, among all possible ways of choosing actions—stochastic or deterministic—the optimal policy is the one whose rollouts, on average, collect the greatest (discounted) sum of rewards.

---

### A bit more formality

* **State‑value view:**

  $$
    V^{\pi}(s)=\mathbb{E}\bigl[\sum_{t=0}^\infty \gamma^t\,r_{t+1}\mid s_0=s,\pi\bigr],
  $$

  and we seek
  $\;V^*(s)=\max_\pi V^\pi(s)$
  for each state.

* **Policy extraction:**
  Once you know $V^*(s)$, any policy that in each state picks an action achieving the Bellman‐optimal value

  $$
    \max_a\;\mathbb{E}\bigl[r_{t+1}+\gamma\,V^*(s_{t+1})\mid s_t=s,a_t=a\bigr]
  $$

  is an optimal policy $\pi^*$.

---

### Key takeaway

> **“Optimal” means “maximizes the expected return.”**
> No matter how many trajectories are possible, $\pi^*$ is the policy under which the **average** (discounted) reward over those trajectories is as large as it can be.

**Parameterized Policies in Reinforcement Learning**

A **parameterized policy** is a policy $\pi_\theta$ whose behavior is controlled by a vector of parameters $\theta$. Instead of a lookup table of action probabilities for every state, you define a functional form—such as a neural network, linear model, or Gaussian distribution—and tune $\theta$ so that $\pi_\theta$ maximizes expected reward.

---

### Key Features

* **Compact representation**

  $$
    \pi_\theta(a \mid s) = f_\theta(s)_a
  $$

  where $f_\theta(s)$ might be, for example, the softmax output of a neural net over action logits or the mean of a Gaussian from which you sample.

* **Stochastic vs. Deterministic**

  * **Stochastic**: $\pi_\theta(a\mid s)$ gives a probability distribution (e.g., softmax, Gaussian).
  * **Deterministic**: $\mu_\theta(s)$ directly outputs an action (common in continuous control).

* **Learning the parameters**

  * **Policy gradient** methods estimate

    $$
      \nabla_\theta J(\theta)
      = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_\theta}\bigl[\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a\mid s)\,G_t\bigr]
    $$

    and perform gradient ascent on $\theta$.
  * **Actor–Critic** architectures maintain both $\pi_\theta$ (actor) and a value function $V_w$ (critic), updating $\theta$ using the critic’s feedback.

* **Advantages**

  * **Scalability** to large or continuous state/action spaces.
  * **Smooth generalization**: small changes in $\theta$ produce small changes in behavior.
  * **Flexibility** in incorporating function approximators (CNNs for images, RNNs for memory).

---

**In practice**, you choose a differentiable model for $\pi_\theta$, initialize $\theta$, collect rollouts under $\pi_\theta$, compute policy‑gradient estimates, and update $\theta$ until performance converges—yielding a policy that maps raw observations to actions in one smooth, trainable function.

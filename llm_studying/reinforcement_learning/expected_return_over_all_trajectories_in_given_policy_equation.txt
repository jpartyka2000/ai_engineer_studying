### Expected Return as an Expectation over Trajectories

In policy‐based RL, we measure a policy’s quality by its **expected return**, written

$$
J(\pi) \;=\;\int_{\tau} P(\tau\mid \pi)\,R(\tau)
\;=\;\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim \pi}\bigl[R(\tau)\bigr].
$$

Here’s what each piece means:

---

#### 1. Trajectory $\tau$

A trajectory (or rollout)

$$
\tau=(s_0,a_0,r_1,s_1,a_1,r_2,\dots,s_T)
$$

is one complete sample path of states, actions, and rewards generated by following $\pi$ in the environment.

---

#### 2. Probability $P(\tau\mid \pi)$

From our earlier discussion:

$$
P(\tau\mid \pi)
= \rho_0(s_0)\;\prod_{t=0}^{T-1}P(s_{t+1}\!\mid s_t,a_t)\,\pi(a_t\!\mid s_t).
$$

It’s the joint probability of seeing exactly those states and actions under policy $\pi$ and the environment dynamics.

---

#### 3. Return $R(\tau)$

The return of a trajectory is the **sum of (discounted) rewards** collected along it, e.g.

$$
R(\tau)
=\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}\gamma^t\,r_{t+1}.
$$

This scalar quantifies how “good” that particular rollout was.

---

#### 4. Why the integral (or sum)

* If trajectories are continuous or infinite in number, we write an **integral** over all possible $\tau$.
* In a discrete or countable setting, you’d use a **sum**:
  $\displaystyle J(\pi)=\sum_{\tau}P(\tau\!\mid\!\pi)\,R(\tau).$

Either way, you’re averaging the return over every way the world could unfold under $\pi$.

---

#### 5. The expectation notation

$$
\mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim \pi}\bigl[R(\tau)\bigr]
$$

is just a compact way to say “the mean of $R(\tau)$ when $\tau$ is sampled according to $P(\tau\mid\pi)$.”

---

### Putting it all together

> **$J(\pi)$** is your **objective**: the average (discounted) reward you expect to get if you repeatedly run episodes using policy $\pi$.
>
> Policy‐gradient and actor‐critic methods work by estimating or differentiating this expectation with respect to the policy parameters, then improving $\pi$ to increase $J(\pi)$.

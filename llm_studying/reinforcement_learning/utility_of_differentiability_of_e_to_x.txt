Exactly—expressing $e^x$ as a power series makes its **infinite differentiability** completely transparent, because:

1. **Term‑wise differentiation**

   $$
     \frac{d}{dx}\Bigl(\tfrac{x^n}{n!}\Bigr)
     = \tfrac{n\,x^{n-1}}{n!}
     = \tfrac{x^{n-1}}{(n-1)!}\,,
   $$

   so you can differentiate the series
   $\sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n/n!$
   term by term under the usual convergence theorems, and you immediately recover the same series.

2. **Infinite smoothness**
   Because every term is a polynomial, and you can differentiate an infinite number of times by the same argument, $e^x$ is $C^\infty$ (infinitely differentiable).

3. **Why that matters in ML/RL**

   * **Backpropagation & gradients**: Policy gradients, value‑function gradients, actor–critic updates—all rely on the fact that $\exp(z)$ and hence softmax/log‑softmax are smooth with well‑defined derivatives at every point.
   * **Stable implementations**: Even though libraries use optimized approximations, they ensure the underlying function remains smoothly differentiable so gradient descent (and its variants) works reliably.

So while the limit definition of $e^x$ also defines the same function, the power‐series form is what makes its smooth, gradient‑friendly nature most obvious in theory—and underpins the fact that every ML framework can backpropagate through `exp` (and softmax) without hiccups.

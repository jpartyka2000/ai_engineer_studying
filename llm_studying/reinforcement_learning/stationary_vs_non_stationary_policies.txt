In the context of this paper, a **non-stationary policy** is a policy (decision-making rule) that **changes over time** rather than staying fixed.

## **Stationary vs. Non-Stationary Policies**

**Stationary Policy:**
- **Same rule at all time steps**: π(state) → action
- The policy function doesn't change as time progresses
- If you encounter the same state at t=1 and t=100, you take the same action
- **Example**: "Always turn left when you see a red sign" - this rule never changes

**Non-Stationary Policy:**
- **Different rules at different time steps**: π_t(state) → action (notice the subscript _t)
- The policy changes based on when you're making the decision
- Same state at different times → potentially different actions
- **Example**: "Turn left at red signs for the first 10 steps, then turn right after that"

## **Why This Paper Cares**

In **imitation learning**, you're trying to learn a policy by watching an expert. The problem is:

1. **Dataset Aggregation (DAgger)** and similar methods create policies that depend on "which iteration of training are we in?"
2. This means the policy is **non-stationary** - it behaves differently depending on when it was learned
3. **Deployment problem**: When you actually use the policy, which version do you use?

**The paper's contribution:**
They propose an algorithm that learns a **stationary deterministic policy** - one fixed rule that works well regardless of when you apply it. This is more practical because:
- **Simpler to deploy** - just one policy to ship
- **Easier to reason about** - consistent behavior
- **Better theoretical guarantees** - no need to track "what iteration am I on?"

## **Analogy**

Think of learning to drive:

**Non-stationary approach (like DAgger):**
- "In lesson 1, brake gently"
- "In lesson 2, brake medium hard"  
- "In lesson 3, brake hard"
- When you get your license: "Which lesson's braking should I use?"

**Stationary approach (this paper):**
- Learn one consistent braking rule: "Brake proportional to distance to obstacle"
- Use the same rule always, regardless of how many lessons you've had

## **Technical Detail**

The paper critiques methods that train policies like:
- π₁ for iteration 1
- π₂ for iteration 2
- π₃ for iteration 3

And instead proposes learning a single π that works well throughout training and deployment.

This makes the learned behavior **stationary** - unchanging across time steps.

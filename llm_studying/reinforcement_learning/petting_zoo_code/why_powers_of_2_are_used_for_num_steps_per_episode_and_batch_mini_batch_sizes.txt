Using a power of two for your rollout length (and often for your minibatch size) is almost entirely a matter of convenience and hardware efficiency rather than a strict algorithmic requirement. Here’s why you’ll see it everywhere:

1. **Evenly divisible into minibatches**

   * If your rollout is $N$ steps and you use a minibatch size of $M$, you want $N \bmod M = 0$.
   * Picking both $N$ and $M$ as powers of two (e.g. 2048 rollout / 64 minibatch) guarantees that $\tfrac{N}{M}$ is an integer without having to think through odd factors.

2. **Memory & compute alignment**

   * GPUs and CPUs often perform best when working on blocks of data whose size is a power of two.
   * Under the hood, memory allocators, cache lines, and vectorized math routines are optimized for power-of-two strides and buffer sizes.

3. **Warp and thread‐block sizing on GPUs**

   * On NVIDIA hardware, a “warp” is 32 threads.  A power-of-two batch lets you tile your data cleanly into warps and blocks, maximizing throughput and minimizing idle threads.

4. **Bit‐level simplicity**

   * Dividing by two is just a bit‐shift in binary, so loops that halve or double sizes (e.g. coarse-to-fine scheduling, multi-GPU splits) become trivial and branch-free.

5. **Conventional defaults ≫ less mental arithmetic**

   * Everybody uses 256, 512, 1024, 2048, etc., so it becomes the de-facto standard.  When you pick one of those, you immediately know how many minibatches, epochs, or worker splits you’ll get—no trial-and-error needed.

---

### Can you pick a non-power-of-two?

Absolutely. The only real requirements are:

* **Divisibility:** make sure `n_steps_per_update % minibatch_size == 0`.
* **Hardware fit:** very large arbitrary sizes might fragment GPU/CPU memory or slightly underutilize vector units.

In practice, if you need a very specific number of steps (say, 3000 for your experiment), go for it—just check that it divides cleanly into your minibatches and fits in your memory budget. Powers of two just make all those checks disappear and tend to give you a tiny performance edge on modern hardware.

### Why We Use the Power‑Series Definition of $e^x$ (Even in RL)

In reinforcement learning (and machine learning generally), you often see $e^x$ introduced via its **power‑series** expansion:

$$
e^x \;=\;\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}.
$$

Here’s why that series pops up everywhere—even in RL—rather than, say, computing it by an infinite limit or some other “normal” definition:

---

#### 1. **Analytic Convenience**

* **Differentiation**: Term‑by‑term,
  $\tfrac{d}{dx} x^n / n! = x^{n-1}/(n-1)!$,
  so $\tfrac{d}{dx}e^x = e^x$ follows immediately.
* **Integration**: Similarly trivial to integrate, which underpins many proofs in dynamics and optimization.

#### 2. **Universal Validity**

* The series converges for **all** real (and even complex) $x$.
* There’s no “domain restriction” or worry about dividing by zero as in limit definitions.

#### 3. **Numerical Approximation**

* **Practical libraries** (NumPy, PyTorch, etc.) implement `exp(x)` using highly optimized polynomial or rational approximations—essentially truncated Taylor/Pade series behind the scenes.
* This gives both **speed** and **accuracy** across the full range of inputs you encounter in RL (from large negative log‑probabilities to big positive advantages).

#### 4. **Conceptual Clarity in Softmax & Policy Gradients**

* When you derive the softmax gradient $\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a\mid s)$, the series form of $e^x$ keeps the math clean, since you work with sums and products of exponentials directly.
* It makes it clear why $\exp(z)$ is always positive—an essential property for probabilities.

---

### The “Normal” vs. “Series” View

* **Limit definition**: $\displaystyle e^x = \lim_{N\to\infty} (1+\tfrac{x}{N})^N$ is elegant but **impractical** to compute or differentiate in code.
* **Series definition**: gives you a clear, term‑by‑term recipe that underlies every efficient implementation of `exp(x)` and facilitates analytic work like deriving gradients.

---

**Bottom Line:**
Even though you never literally sum an infinite series in your RL code, understanding $e^x$ via its power series is what lets libraries provide a fast, accurate `exp()` function—and why all the theoretical derivations of softmax, policy gradients, and value‐iteration are so clean.

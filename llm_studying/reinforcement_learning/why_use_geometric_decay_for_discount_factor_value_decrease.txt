A geometric (exponential) decay—that is, multiplying by γᵏ for the reward k steps in the future—shows up everywhere in RL for a few tightly-interwoven reasons:

---

### 1. **Convergence of the Infinite Sum**

If you have an unbounded (infinite-horizon) problem, you need

$$
G_t = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k\,r_{t+1+k}
$$

to converge.  A geometric series with 0≤γ<1 guarantees that distant rewards get vanishingly small weights and the sum stays finite.

---

### 2. **Time-Consistent, “Markovian” Preferences**

Exponential discounting is **memoryless**: the relative weight between “one step ahead” and “two steps ahead” is always γ, no matter when you start.  This means:

* Your preference ordering over sequences of rewards doesn’t change with absolute time.
* It meshes perfectly with the Markov assumption (future depends only on current state, not on how long you’ve been in the process).

If you used some other decay (like hyperbolic), you’d break that stationarity and complicate your Bellman recursion.

---

### 3. **Simple, Recursive Bellman Equation**

Because

$$
G_t = r_{t+1} + \gamma \underbrace{\sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k\,r_{t+2+k}}_{G_{t+1}}
     = r_{t+1} + \gamma\,G_{t+1},
$$

you get the classic one-step Bellman backup

$$
V(s) = \mathbb{E}[\,r + \gamma V(s')\,],
$$

which is **linear** in V and yields a **contraction mapping**.  That contraction is the heart of DP convergence guarantees (value iteration, TD, Q-learning).

---

### 4. **Models Real-World Uncertainty & Time-Value**

* In many real problems—finance, control systems, even animal behavior—you really do value immediate rewards more than later ones.
* Exponential decay gives you a single “half-life” parameter (via γ) to tune how quickly you “forget” the future.

---

### 5. **Algorithmic Convenience**

* Exponentially decayed returns are easy to compute on the fly with a running accumulator.
* They mesh seamlessly with streaming data and online updates (no need to revisit every past reward when a new one arrives).

---

**Bottom line:** geometric decay isn’t just tradition—it’s what lets you define a **stationary**, **time-consistent**, **recursive**, and **computationally stable** notion of “total future reward” that both models sensible time preferences and gives you the mathematical hooks (contraction, Bellman backups) to actually learn in infinite-horizon settings.

In reinforcement learning (RL), the distinction between a **state** and an **observation** comes down to **what the agent truly needs to know** versus **what the agent actually sees**:

---

## 1. True State (Markov state)

* **Definition:** The minimal complete description of the environment at a time $t$ that, together with the agent’s action $a_t$, fully determines the next state and reward.
* **Markov property:**

  $$
    P(s_{t+1}, r_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t)
    = P(s_{t+1}, r_{t+1} \mid s_{1:t}, a_{1:t})
  $$

  In other words, once you know the current **state** $s_t$, the future is independent of the past.
* **Examples:**

  * In a chess game, the state is the full board configuration (including whose turn it is).
  * In a cart‐pole, the state might be $(\theta,\dot{\theta},x,\dot{x})$: pole angle, pole angular velocity, cart position, cart velocity.

---

## 2. Observation

* **Definition:** The data the agent actually receives at time $t$, which may be a noisy, partial, or transformed view of the true state.
* **Partial observability:** If the environment is a **Partially Observable Markov Decision Process** (POMDP), the observation $o_t$ does **not** satisfy the Markov property on its own. You often need a history of observations (or an internal memory) to infer the true state.
* **Examples:**

  * A self‑driving car’s camera image (you don’t directly observe the exact positions and velocities of all nearby vehicles—just pixels).
  * In a card game, you only observe your own hand and public cards, not the opponents’ hands.

---

## 3. Why the Distinction Matters

1. **Algorithm choice:**

   * **Fully observable (MDP):** algorithms like Q‑learning or vanilla policy gradients assume $s_t$ is known.
   * **Partially observable (POMDP):** you need memory‑based methods (e.g., Recurrent Neural Networks, belief‑state tracking, or Bayesian filters) to aggregate past observations into an internal state estimate.

2. **State estimation:**

   * In POMDPs, you often maintain a **belief** $b_t(s)$, a probability distribution over possible states given past observations $o_{1:t}$ and actions $a_{1:t-1}$.

3. **Architectural differences:**

   * **MDP agent:**

     ```text
     o_t = s_t   →  policy π(a_t|s_t)
     ```
   * **POMDP agent:**

     ```text
     o_t  →  update hidden memory h_t = f(h_{t-1}, o_t, a_{t-1})
     h_t  →  policy π(a_t|h_t)
     ```

---

### Bottom Line

* **State** = the underlying “ground‑truth” Markovian descriptor of the environment.
* **Observation** = what your agent actually sees, which may be incomplete or noisy.

Understanding this distinction is critical for choosing the right RL algorithm and for handling partial observability effectively.

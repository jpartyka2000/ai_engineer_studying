We call the Bellman equations **self‑consistent** because each one defines a value (state‑value $V$ or action‑value $Q$) in terms of **itself**—but at the “next” state—so that the true value function must satisfy the same recursive relationship over and over again.

---

### What “self‑consistency” means here

* The Bellman expectation equation for a fixed policy $\pi$ says

  $$
    V^\pi(s)
    = \mathbb{E}_{a\sim\pi}\bigl[r(s,a) + \gamma\,V^\pi(s')\bigr].
  $$

  Notice that **the left‑hand side** is $V^\pi(s)$, and **on the right** you also see $V^\pi\bigl(s'\bigr)$.  In other words, $V^\pi$ must equal its own one‑step look‑ahead expectation.

* The Bellman optimality equation similarly demands

  $$
    V^*(s)
    = \max_a \,\mathbb{E}\bigl[r(s,a)+\gamma\,V^*(s')\bigr],
  $$

  so the optimal value function must be **consistent with itself** under the best‑action recursion.

---

### Why that matters

1. **Fixed‑point view**

   * Each Bellman operator (expectation or optimality) is a mapping $T$ on the space of candidate value functions:

     $$
       T(V)(s) = \mathbb{E}[r + \gamma V(s')]
     \quad\text{or}\quad
       T^*(V)(s) = \max_a \mathbb{E}[r + \gamma V(s')].
     $$
   * A truly correct value function $V$ is a **fixed point** of that operator: $T(V) = V$.  That fixed‑point property is exactly what “self‑consistent” means.

2. **Iterative convergence**

   * Because these operators are **contractions** (they shrink differences between functions), repeated application—“backups”—converges to the unique fixed point $V^\pi$ or $V^*$.

3. **Principled backups**

   * Every Bellman backup update moves your estimate closer to satisfying its own self‑consistency condition.
   * When you’ve reached consistency (no change on further backups), you’ve found the true value function.

---

### In a nutshell

> Bellman equations are “self‑consistent” because they require that the value you assign to a state (or state–action) **matches** the combination of immediate reward and discounted value you’d compute using that very same value function at successor states.  The correct $V$ (or $Q$) is the one that makes that recursive definition hold *everywhere*, i.e. is its own fixed point under the Bellman operator.

**REINFORCE** is basically the simplest “vanilla” policy‐gradient (PG) algorithm—it directly applies the policy‐gradient theorem using full-episode returns. Here’s what that means:

1. **Parameterized stochastic policy**
   You have $\pi_\theta(a\mid s)$, e.g. a neural network that given state $s$ outputs a distribution over actions.

2. **Monte-Carlo returns**
   You run a complete episode (trajectory) under $\pi_\theta$, collect rewards $\{r_{t+1}\}$, and compute the **discounted return**

   $$
     G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{T-t-1}\gamma^k\,r_{t+1+k}.
   $$

3. **Score-function (log-prob) estimator**
   The policy‐gradient theorem tells us

   $$
     \nabla_\theta J(\theta)
     = \mathbb{E}\Bigl[\sum_{t=0}^{T-1}G_t\,\nabla_\theta\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)\Bigr].
   $$

   You approximate this expectation with your single (or small batch of) episode(s).

4. **Update**
   You form the loss

   $$
     \mathcal{L}(\theta) \;=\; -\sum_{t}G_t\,\log\pi_\theta(a_t\mid s_t)
   $$

   and run a standard gradient‐descent step on $\mathcal{L}$.  Because of the minus sign, you’re actually doing **gradient ascent** on $J(\theta)$.

---

### So: is it “vanilla”?

* **Yes**—REINFORCE is often called the **vanilla policy gradient**.
* It uses **full-episode** Monte Carlo (no bootstrapping), so it’s unbiased but can have very high variance.
* All later PG methods (A2C, PPO, TRPO, etc.) build on REINFORCE by adding variance reduction (baselines, critics), clipping, multiple epochs, or trust regions for **more stable** and **sample‐efficient** learning.

In short, REINFORCE is the straightforward, no-frills policy‐gradient algorithm—it *is* the “vanilla” PG.

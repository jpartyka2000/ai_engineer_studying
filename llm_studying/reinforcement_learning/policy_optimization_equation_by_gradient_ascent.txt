That update rule is just **gradient ascent** on your policy’s performance surface $J(\pi_\theta)$:

$$
\theta_{k+1} = \theta_k \;+\;\alpha\,\nabla_\theta J(\pi_\theta)\bigl|_{\theta_k}
$$

---

### Intuitive picture

1. **Parameters as coordinates**
   Think of $\theta$ as a point in a high-dimensional space where each direction tweaks some aspect of your policy (e.g. “how much I turn left when I see an enemy”).

2. **Objective surface**
   Above every $\theta$ you have a “height” given by $J(\pi_\theta)$, the **expected return** (how well you play on average with that policy).

3. **Gradient = uphill direction**
   $\nabla_\theta J$ is like the slope or compass that points you in the steepest uphill direction on this surface.  Moving that way will **increase** your expected score fastest.

4. **Step size $\alpha$**
   The scalar $\alpha$ (learning rate) is **how big** a step you take in that uphill direction.  Too small ⇒ slow learning; too large ⇒ you might overshoot or bounce around.

5. **Iterate**
   Each iteration $k$:

   * You estimate $\nabla_\theta J$ (via rollouts and log-prob tricks).
   * You move $\theta$ a bit uphill.
   * Your policy improves, so your next gradient points you toward even better performance.

---

### In plain English

> “At each update, look at how changing each policy parameter would affect your expected return, then nudge those parameters a little bit in the directions that boost your return most—repeating until you can’t climb any higher.”

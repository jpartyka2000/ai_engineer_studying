In the Transformer architecture, **dot product + normalization (scaling)** is primarily used in two key places to calculate similarity:

### 1. **Scaled Dot-Product Attention (Self-Attention & Cross-Attention)**
   - In the attention mechanism, similarity between query (\(Q\)) and key (\(K\)) vectors is computed using the **dot product**, followed by **scaling**:
     \[
     \text{Attention Scores} = \frac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}
     \]
   - This scaling factor (\(\sqrt{d_k}\)) is crucial because dot products tend to grow in magnitude as the dimensionality (\(d_k\)) increases, leading to sharper softmax distributions.

### 2. **Query-Key-Value Attention Across Multiple Heads**
   - In **multi-head attention**, each head independently computes similarity using **scaled dot-product attention** before concatenating the results.

---

While the **dot product itself** appears in other places (e.g., final classification layers in BERT or similarity-based retrieval), **explicit dot product + scaling for similarity computation** happens **inside attention mechanisms** in the transformer.

Let's break it down step by step with an example to see exactly where **dot product + norm scaling** is used to compute similarity in a Transformer.

---

### **Step 1: Self-Attention – Computing Similarity Between Tokens**
In self-attention, we compute how similar each token is to every other token in a sequence. This is done by:

1. **Projecting input embeddings** into three vectors:  
   - **Query (Q)**: Represents the current token asking for information.
   - **Key (K)**: Represents all tokens that might provide useful information.
   - **Value (V)**: Contains actual content to be aggregated.

   If our input consists of \( n \) tokens, and each token is represented as a vector of dimension \( d_{\text{model}} \), then:
   - \( Q, K, V \) are each of shape \( (n, d_k) \) where \( d_k = d_{\text{model}} / h \) (for \( h \) attention heads).

---

### **Step 2: Computing Similarity (Dot Product + Scaling)**
We compute **attention scores** by taking the **dot product** of queries and keys:

\[
\text{Attention Scores} = Q K^T
\]

- This results in an \( n \times n \) matrix where each entry represents the similarity between two tokens.
- A larger dot product means the tokens are more similar.

Next, we **scale** these scores to prevent large values from dominating the softmax function:

\[
\frac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}
\]

- The division by \( \sqrt{d_k} \) prevents large dot products from making the softmax distribution overly sharp, which would lead to vanishing gradients.

---

### **Step 3: Applying Softmax & Weighting Values**
- We apply the **softmax** function to transform the similarity scores into attention weights:

\[
\text{Attention Weights} = \text{softmax} \left(\frac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_k}} \right)
\]

- These weights determine how much information each token should "attend" to.
- We use these weights to compute a weighted sum of the **value (V) vectors**:

\[
\text{Output} = \text{Attention Weights} \times V
\]

---

### **Step 4: Multi-Head Attention**
- In **multi-head attention**, multiple attention heads perform these calculations in parallel.
- Each head has its own set of \( Q, K, V \) projections and applies **dot product + scaling** separately.
- The outputs from different heads are concatenated and passed through a linear transformation.

---

### **Final Answer: Where Dot Product + Scaling is Used**
1. **Self-Attention & Cross-Attention (Inside Multi-Head Attention)**
   - Used to compute similarity between tokens in a sequence.
   - Formula: \( \frac{Q K^T}{\sqrt{d_k}} \).

Since this happens **in each attention head**, the operation is repeated **h times per layer** in **each attention block** (self-attention for encoders, cross-attention for decoders).

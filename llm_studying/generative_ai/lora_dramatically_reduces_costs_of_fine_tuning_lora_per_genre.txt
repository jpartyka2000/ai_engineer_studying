Yes, dramatically. LoRA is perfect for this kind of specialization.

A full fine-tune of a model like MusicGen-large (1.5B params) updates all parameters and might cost $5K-$50K in compute. A LoRA fine-tune updates maybe 0.1-2% of the parameters (a low-rank adapter layered on top of the frozen base weights), so you're looking at:

- **Single A100 or H100 GPU** for hours to a few days, not weeks
- **$50-$500** in cloud compute for a genre-specific LoRA
- Even feasible on a single consumer GPU (RTX 4090) for smaller models

The tradeoff is that LoRA adapters are narrower in what they learn — but that's actually an advantage for your use case. A "blues LoRA" trained on a curated dataset of blues recordings would learn the specific tonal qualities, chord progressions, instrumentation patterns, and rhythmic feel of blues without catastrophically forgetting the base model's general music understanding.

You could even build a collection of swappable LoRAs — blues, jazz, metal, country — all sharing the same base model, switching at inference time. Each one might need only a few hundred to a few thousand high-quality training tracks in that genre.

The practical bottleneck isn't compute at that point, it's **data quality**. A LoRA trained on 500 carefully curated, well-labeled blues tracks will outperform one trained on 10,000 random tracks tagged "blues" on Spotify. And you still face the licensing question — where do those training tracks come from?

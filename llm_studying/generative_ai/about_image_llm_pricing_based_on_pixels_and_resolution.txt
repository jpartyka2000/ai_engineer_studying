The pricing for AI image generation is moving toward resolution-aware models, though most providers today still use simplified tiers rather than a true linear per-pixel formula.

**How compute cost actually scales with pixels:** The GPU work required to generate an image scales roughly proportionally with pixel count. A 1024×1024 image (1MP) takes meaningfully less compute than a 2048×2048 image (4MP) — roughly 4x the pixels means roughly 4x the processing. This is because diffusion models operate on a latent space whose size is derived from the target resolution, so more pixels means a larger tensor to denoise at every step.

**How providers handle this today:** Most APIs use resolution buckets or tiers rather than a continuous per-pixel rate. For example, Black Forest Labs charges one rate for standard FLUX output and a higher rate for their 4MP "Ultra" mode. Google's Imagen 4 ranges from $0.02 to $0.06 depending on quality and resolution settings. Providers like fal.ai charge by compute-second, which indirectly reflects pixel count since larger images take longer. Together.ai charges a flat per-image rate but limits the maximum resolution you can request.

**Why flat per-image pricing persists:** It's simpler for developers to budget around. Nobody wants to do math on megapixels every time they make an API call. And since most use cases cluster around a few standard resolutions (1024×1024, 1024×768, 1536×1536), buckets work well enough.

**Where it's likely heading:** As models like FLUX.2 support continuous resolution scaling up to 4MP and beyond, and as upscaling and outpainting become single-API-call features, you'll probably see pricing converge on something like a base rate plus a megapixel multiplier — conceptually similar to how cloud storage charges per GB. The base rate covers the fixed overhead (prompt encoding, model loading), and the variable component reflects the actual pixel work. Some providers may also factor in step count, since a 50-step generation at 4MP costs roughly 10x the compute of a 4-step generation at 1MP.

The economic pressure is real — providers who charge a flat $0.04 for every image regardless of resolution are effectively subsidizing high-res users at the expense of low-res ones, which isn't sustainable at scale.

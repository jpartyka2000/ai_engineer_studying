A common rule of thumb for setting aside a validation (or “dev”) set when you have a fixed number of training examples is to reserve **10–20 %** of your total labeled data.  Applied to your case:

* **10 % split:**

  * Training: 500 examples
  * Validation:  50 examples

* **20 % split:**

  * Training: 400 examples
  * Validation: 100 examples

Most practitioners fall somewhere in that range.  Here are a few considerations to help you choose:

1. **Representativeness:**
   You want your validation set to cover the same variety of inputs, styles, or edge cases as your training set.  If your data is highly heterogeneous, leaning toward the higher end (e.g. 15–20 %) can help ensure you catch performance issues on less common patterns.

2. **Statistical stability:**
   A too‐small validation set (e.g. < 50 examples) can lead to noisy estimates of your model’s true generalization performance.  If you’re concerned about high variance in your evaluation metrics, err on the side of more examples.

3. **Resource constraints:**
   If labeling or curating examples is expensive, you might cap your validation at 10 %.  Conversely, if you have plenty of unlabeled or lightly reviewed examples you can repurpose, taking 20 % can give you a more robust early‐warning signal of overfitting.

4. **Empirical tuning:**
   It’s not uncommon to start with a 90/10 split (450 train / 50 val) and then experiment—if you see wildly fluctuating validation losses or metrics, increase to 80/20 (400/100) and compare.

---

**Recommendation for your 500-example file:**

* Start with **50 examples** (10 %) in your validation file.
* If you notice high variance in validation metrics, bump up to **75–100 examples** (15–20 %).

This balance should give you both sufficient training signal and a reliable check on generalization.

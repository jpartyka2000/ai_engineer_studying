Great question — you’ll often hear “pick a batch size like 8, 16, 32, 64…” when training or fine-tuning large models. The reason isn’t anything mystical about machine learning itself, but about how the hardware and software underneath work:

---

## 1. GPU & Memory Alignment

* GPUs work with memory in **blocks (cache lines, warps, vector widths)** that are almost always powers of 2.
* When the batch dimension is also a power of 2, tensors align nicely in memory, so fewer padding or remainder operations are needed.
* This reduces wasted cycles and improves throughput.

---

## 2. CUDA / cuDNN Optimizations

* NVIDIA’s CUDA libraries (and AMD equivalents) contain many hand-tuned kernels for matrix multiplies, convolutions, etc.
* These kernels are usually optimized for dimensions that are multiples of 8, 16, 32, etc.
* If your batch size isn’t aligned, the libraries may internally pad to the next multiple, which wastes memory and time.

---

## 3. Parallelization Efficiency

* GPUs schedule threads in groups (NVIDIA calls them **warps**, typically 32 threads).
* If your batch size divides evenly into these thread groups, you minimize idle threads.
* Example: a batch size of 30 on a warp of 32 leaves 2 threads idle per warp, reducing efficiency.

---

## 4. Historical Convention

* Early deep learning frameworks and hardware strongly encouraged power-of-2 sizes for stability.
* Although modern hardware is more flexible, the habit stuck, and powers of 2 remain a safe default.

---

## 5. Practical Reality Today

* **It’s not strictly required.** Many models train fine with odd batch sizes, especially if constrained by GPU memory.
* But if you can choose freely, powers of 2 tend to give **slightly better throughput** and **more predictable GPU utilization**.
* Some people even go for “mixed” rules of thumb: multiples of 8 are usually enough (because tensor cores like dimensions divisible by 8 or 16).

---

✅ **In short:** Powers of 2 (or at least multiples of 8/16) make GPUs and their libraries happiest — better memory alignment, better thread utilization, and slightly faster training. That’s why they’re recommended as batch sizes when fine-tuning LLMs.

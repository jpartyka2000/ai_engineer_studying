Based on the research I already gathered, here's the comparison:

**Pricing**

FLUX1.1 [pro] is flat-rate at about $0.04 per image.

FLUX.2 [flex] is more expensive in the FLUX.2 family. On fal.ai, Flex is priced at $0.06 per megapixel, and Together.ai follows a similar megapixel-based structure. For a standard 1024×1024 image (~1 MP), that's roughly $0.06 — actually **more expensive** than FLUX1.1 [pro]. Flex also charges per megapixel on both input and output, which affects cost for editing workflows with multiple reference images. So for multi-reference generation, costs can climb quickly.

FLUX1.1 [pro] is the cheaper option here, which is a reversal from the [dev] comparison.

**Image Quality**

Same generational leap as with [dev] — FLUX.2 [flex] sits on the 32-billion parameter architecture with the rewritten VAE, so it inherits all the FLUX.2 improvements in lighting, skin textures, fabric, materials, and depth of field. FLUX.2 supports native 4MP generation vs FLUX1.1 [pro]'s ~1MP ceiling.

The key difference between [flex] and [dev] is that [flex] gives developers full control over generation parameters like step count and guidance scale, allowing fine-grained balancing of quality, prompt alignment, and performance. This means you can push quality higher than [dev] by cranking up steps, at the cost of more time and money. Or you can dial steps down for faster drafts. FLUX1.1 [pro] doesn't expose these controls.

For default settings, both FLUX.2 [flex] and FLUX1.1 [pro] produce strong photorealistic images, but [flex] has the edge due to the architectural generation gap.

**Text Rendering**

This is where [flex] really shines — it's the text specialist in the FLUX.2 lineup. [Flex] is particularly strong at producing crisp text and fine-detail imagery. Together.ai's own description positions it as the model to choose for text-heavy content. [Flex] excels at typography and text rendering specifically.

FLUX1.1 [pro] handles short text reasonably well but struggles with longer strings, repeated characters, and complex layouts. [Flex] is in a different league — it's designed for posters, UI mockups, packaging, and infographics where legibility matters. The adjustable step count lets you allocate more compute specifically to refine text details, since text is typically the last thing to converge during diffusion.

**Bottom Line**

| | FLUX1.1 [pro] | FLUX.2 [flex] |
|---|---|---|
| **Price (1MP image)** | ~$0.04 | ~$0.06 |
| **Max resolution** | ~1MP | Up to 4MP |
| **Architecture** | 12B params | 32B params |
| **Photorealism** | Very good | Better (gen leap) |
| **Text rendering** | Decent | Best in FLUX.2 family |
| **Parameter control** | None (black box) | Full (steps, guidance) |
| **Multi-reference** | No | Yes (up to 10 images, 14MP total input) |
| **LoRA/fine-tuning** | No | No (API-only) |

The tradeoff here is straightforward: FLUX.2 [flex] is better at everything *except* price. If text rendering quality is your priority (signage, packaging, UI comps), [flex] is worth the premium. If you're doing general photorealistic generation and cost matters, FLUX1.1 [pro] remains competitive — or you could use FLUX.2 [dev] at $0.012/MP to get most of the FLUX.2 quality gains at a third of the cost of FLUX1.1 [pro].

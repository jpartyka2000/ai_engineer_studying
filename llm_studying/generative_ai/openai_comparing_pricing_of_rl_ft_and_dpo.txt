No – Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) on OpenAI is almost always much cheaper than Reinforcement Fine-Tuning (RFT). Here’s why:

* **DPO is token-based**
  You pay only for the tokens in your training file (and the number of epochs), at the standard fine-tuning per-token rates. For example, if you use GPT-4.1 mini, training costs are as low as \$5 per 1 M training tokens (input), plus \$0.20 per 1 M cached tokens and \$3.20 per 1 M output tokens.

  > “You’re charged based on the number of tokens in your training file, and the number of epochs…
  > price = # training tokens × # epochs × price per token” ([Microsoft Learn][1])

* **RFT is time-based**
  RFT jobs are billed at \$100 per training hour for the o4-mini model, *plus* any grader token usage (e.g. \$4 / 1 M input tokens, \$16 / 1 M output tokens).

  > “RFT price for o4-mini: … Training: \$100.00 / training hour
  > Input: \$4.00 / 1 M tokens; Output: \$16.00 / 1 M tokens” ([OpenAI][2])

---

### Rough comparison

| Method  | Pricing Model                             | Typical Cost Drivers                               |
| ------- | ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- |
| **DPO** | Token × Epoch                             | E.g. \$5 / 1 M tokens (GPT-4.1 mini)               |
| **RFT** | \$100 / training hour + grader token fees | Often tens of hours × \$100 = thousands of dollars |

Even if you run a DPO job for multiple epochs on millions of tokens, you’re likely spending on the order of tens to hundreds of dollars, whereas RFT jobs routinely rack up thousands of dollars in compute time alone. So if your alignment task can be expressed as pairwise preferences, DPO is by far the more cost-effective route.

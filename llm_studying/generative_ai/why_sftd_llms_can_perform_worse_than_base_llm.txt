When you fine-tune a powerful base model like GPT-4.1 on a very small dataset (e.g. \~100 examples), it isn’t at all uncommon to see **worse** performance on that same data than simply using the base model “zero-shot” or with prompt-engineering. Here are the main culprits:

---

## 1. Overfitting on a tiny dataset

* **High capacity**: GPT-4.1 has billions of parameters. With only \~100 examples, the fine-tune can “memorize” idiosyncrasies of those examples rather than learning the underlying pattern you really care about.
* **Poor generalization**: On held-out or slightly different prompts, an overfit model will mis-apply whatever narrow mappings it saw during training.

## 2. Catastrophic forgetting

* During SFT, you update the model’s weights to fit your 100 examples. In doing so, you can inadvertently **erase** or weaken the broader reasoning skills and world knowledge it had pre-training—so it sometimes performs worse even on very similar prompts.

## 3. Data distribution shift

* If your 100 examples aren’t perfectly representative of the range of real-world inputs you later test on, the SFT’d model can get “confused” by anything outside that tiny slice. The base model, in contrast, knows how to handle arbitrary language.

## 4. Suboptimal fine-tuning hyperparameters

* **Learning rate too high** → the model “jumps” too far from its pretrained weights.
* **Too few epochs** → the model never actually learned your examples.
* **No regularization** or **no validation early stopping** → you either under- or over-fit without noticing.

## 5. Loss mismatch

* SFT optimizes for **maximum likelihood** on your examples, but your true metric (e.g. correctness of a tool call sequence) may not align perfectly with likelihood. The base model’s unsupervised objective can sometimes yield higher “naturalness” or coherence on unseen examples.

---

### What to do about it

1. **Increase and diversify your SFT data**

   * Move from 100 to at least a few thousand high-quality examples that cover the full range of your use cases.

2. **Use a held-out validation set + early stopping**

   * Monitor your true metric on held-out prompts and stop fine-tuning the moment validation performance peaks.

3. **Lower your learning rate and adjust batch size**

   * Try LR in the 1e-5 to 5e-6 range, and batch sizes that fit your GPU/endpoint.

4. **Mix in some base-model examples (“replay”)**

   * When you fine-tune, include a fraction of the original base-model’s pretraining data (or generic “instruction tuning” prompts) so you don’t forget the general capabilities.

5. **Consider parameter-efficient tuning**

   * Methods like LoRA or adapters let you tweak far fewer parameters, reducing catastrophic forgetting.

6. **Evaluate with the *same* prompts**

   * Make sure you’re measuring performance on held-out examples that truly reflect your intended deployment, not just the 100 you trained on.

---

By expanding and diversifying your fine-tuning set, carefully tuning hyperparameters, and using techniques to preserve the base model’s strengths, you’ll almost always see SFT eventually **surpass** the vanilla model on your specific task. But with only 100 examples and naïve tuning, it’s completely expected that the base GPT-4.1 can outperform your custom endpoint.

Here’s a concise summary of **MLE-Bench Lite**, the “lite’’ variant of OpenAI’s MLE-Bench for evaluating LLM-based ML engineering agents:

### 1. What is MLE-Bench?

MLE-Bench is an **offline Kaggle-competition environment** designed to measure how well AI agents perform real-world ML engineering tasks—everything from data prep to model training and experimentation. It comprises **75 curated Kaggle competitions** (spanning NLP, vision, signal processing, etc.), each with its own description, dataset split, and grading code. Agents submit CSVs that are graded locally against the private leaderboard, and performance is reported in terms of Kaggle-style medals (bronze/silver/gold) and “Any-Medal %” (the fraction of competitions where an agent achieves at least bronze) .

### 2. Complexity splits & the “Lite’’ variant

Each competition is hand-annotated by estimated engineering effort:

* **Low**: sensible solution in < 2 h
* **Medium**: 2–10 h
* **High**: > 10 h
  Out of 75 tasks, **22 are Low-complexity** (≈ 30 %) .
  The **Lite evaluation** simply runs on this **Low-complexity split** of 22 competitions, cutting the number of tasks (and thus compute) by ≈ 3× while still allowing direct comparison on the “Any-Medal %” metric ([GitHub][1]).

### 3. How to run Lite

In the `openai/mle-bench` codebase, the README’s **“Lite Evaluation”** section recommends:

```bash
# prepare only the low-complexity tasks
mlebench prepare --complexity low

# then evaluate your agent on that split
mlebench evaluate --split low 
```

(This reduces storage, data-prep time, and grading runtime.) ([GitHub][1])

### 4. Resources & metrics

* **Full benchmark**: 75 competitions × 24 h each ≈ 1 800 GPU-hours (plus heavy token usage)
* **Lite benchmark**: 22 competitions × 24 h ≈ 528 GPU-hours
* **Metric**: **Any-Medal %** (mean ± SEM over ≥ 3 seeds)&#x20;

### 5. Why use Lite?

* **Cost & speed**: roughly one-third the compute of full MLE-Bench
* **Lower variance**: fewer tasks → tighter standard errors on “Any-Medal %”
* **Accessibility**: makes benchmarking feasible for smaller labs or for rapid prototyping before scaling up

All code and data splits (including the Low-complexity “lite” split) are open-source at **github.com/openai/mle-bench** .

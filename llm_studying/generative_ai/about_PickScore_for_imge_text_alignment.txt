Here's the overview:

## What PickScore Is

PickScore is a CLIP-based scoring function trained on the Pick-a-Pic dataset — a large, open dataset of over a million text-to-image prompts with real users' preference annotations. It was developed by researchers at Tel Aviv University and the Technion, with support from Stability AI.

## How It Works

The architecture is simple — it's fine-tuned from CLIP-ViT-H-14 (the "huge" variant of CLIP trained by LAION). Like CLIPScore, it uses a dual-encoder approach: encode the image and text separately, normalize the embeddings, then compute a scaled cosine similarity. The key difference is that instead of using vanilla CLIP weights, PickScore's weights have been fine-tuned on hundreds of thousands of real user preference pairs — cases where a human looked at two images for the same prompt and picked which one they preferred.

So it inherits CLIP's speed (dual-encoder, dot product) but learns a much better notion of "what humans actually like."

## Key Stats

- **Architecture:** CLIP ViT-H/14 (~1B params — larger than base CLIP but same inference pattern)
- **Training data:** Pick-a-Pic v1/v2, over 1M preference examples from real users
- **Max text length:** 77 tokens (inherited CLIP limitation)
- **Score output:** Unbounded (like CLIPScore), but higher = better alignment/preference
- **GPU memory:** ~4-6 GB — very manageable
- **Latency:** Similar to CLIPScore, roughly 10-20ms per image on GPU
- **Your M3 Pro:** Will run comfortably, probably sub-second on MPS

## Quality

PickScore aligns better with human judgments than any other publicly-available automatic metric at the time of release, and together with Pick-a-Pic's natural distribution prompts, enables much more relevant text-to-image model evaluation than existing evaluation standards.

From the `imscore` benchmarks, PickScore scores 0.754 average pairwise accuracy across preference benchmarks — beating ImageReward (0.669), HPSv2 (0.734), and CLIPScore (0.601).

## Limitations

The main one is the 77-token limit inherited from CLIP — long, detailed prompts get truncated. It also shares CLIP's compositional weakness: it's better than vanilla CLIP at preference prediction but still struggles with complex spatial/relational prompts compared to VQAScore. And PickScore is trained on prompts from real users often describing artwork, so it may be less well-calibrated for natural photographs or domain-specific imagery.

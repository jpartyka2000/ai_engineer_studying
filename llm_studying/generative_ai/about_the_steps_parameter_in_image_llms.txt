Sure. AI image models like FLUX and Stable Diffusion work by starting with pure random noise and gradually refining it into an image over multiple iterations — each iteration is a "step." At each step, the model looks at the current noisy image, predicts what noise is present, removes some of it, and nudges the image closer to what your prompt describes.

**Fewer steps** (say 4–8) means the model takes bigger, rougher jumps. You get a result fast, but it may look soft, lack fine detail, or have artifacts. Think of it like sketching with broad strokes.

**More steps** (20–50) means smaller, more precise refinements. The model has more chances to clean up details like skin texture, sharp edges, text rendering, and lighting. But there are diminishing returns — going from 30 to 50 steps usually improves things far less than going from 4 to 20.

**Beyond ~50 steps** you typically see no visible improvement, and sometimes quality can even degrade slightly as the model starts "over-cooking" the image.

In practice, the sweet spot depends on the model. FLUX schnell is designed to produce good results in just 1–4 steps (it's "distilled" for speed). FLUX dev/pro typically works best around 20–28 steps. Stable Diffusion models usually land around 20–30 steps.

The steps parameter is essentially a quality-vs-speed dial — more steps means better quality up to a point, at the cost of proportionally longer generation time.

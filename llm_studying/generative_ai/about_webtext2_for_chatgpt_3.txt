WebText2 is a large web text dataset that OpenAI used as part of GPT-3’s pretraining corpus. It can be thought of as an updated successor to the original WebText (used for GPT-2), containing a curated selection of internet content. Below is an overview of WebText2, including its scale, content sources, collection process, how it differs from WebText1, notable features/limitations, and its role in GPT-3’s capabilities.

Scale and Size of WebText2

WebText2 is massive in scale, comprising on the order of tens of billions of tokens. In the GPT-3 training mix, WebText2 accounted for about 19 billion tokens of text. To put this in perspective, those 19B tokens made up roughly 4% of the raw tokens in GPT-3’s training data, yet were given a disproportionately high weight (about 22% of the effective training dataset) during training. This means GPT-3 saw WebText2 examples multiple times (nearly 3 epochs over 19B tokens) while sampling other sources less frequently. By comparison, the original WebText (used for GPT-2) contained about **40 GB of text (8 million documents)**, which was on the order of ~8–10 billion tokens. Thus, WebText2 is of a similar order of magnitude but somewhat larger, reflecting the inclusion of more recent data and an expanded crawl. Overall, WebText2 provided a very large high-quality text source for GPT-3, significantly contributing to the 570 GB of filtered text used to train the model.

Content Types and Sources Included

WebText2 consists of web pages that were shared on Reddit and received moderate upvotes, meaning it spans a broad range of content types and domains. Specifically, it contains the text of web pages from all outbound Reddit links in posts with ≥3 upvotes. This Reddit-driven curation leads to a diverse set of sources: news articles, blog posts, informational websites, forums, academic writings, code snippets, etc. In fact, OpenAI noted that WebText (and by extension WebText2) can be seen as “web pages which have been curated/filtered by humans… all outbound links from Reddit… which received at least 3 karma,” a heuristic indicator that other users found the content interesting, educational, or funny.

Because Reddit users share content from many websites, WebText2 draws from a wide variety of domains. For example, the original WebText’s top domains included popular platforms and publishers such as Google (e.g. Google Docs/usercontent), Archive.org, Blogspot blogs, GitHub repositories, major news outlets (like NYTimes, Washington Post, BBC, The Guardian, CNN, HuffPost), community sites like Wikia, e-commerce/forums like eBay, paste sites like Pastebin, and many others. We can expect WebText2 to have a similar profile of content sources, covering everything from news journalism and Wikipedia-like pages to programming code and social commentary. Notably, programming and tech content is well represented (due to links to GitHub, Pastebin, etc.), which means the dataset includes source code and technical discussions alongside prose. All content is in natural text form (extracted from HTML), and although mainly English, it could include other languages if Reddit linked to them (the OpenWebText project focusing on WebText2 has aimed to include all languages with metadata, though English is by far the dominant language in these Reddit-shared links).

One important exclusion is Wikipedia: the creators intentionally omitted direct Wikipedia articles from WebText2. This follows the precedent of WebText1, where all Wikipedia pages were removed to avoid overly easy or duplicative content. (For GPT-3, English Wikipedia was included as a separate dataset of 3B tokens rather than being mixed into WebText2.) Similarly, extremely short or low-information pages were filtered out. Overall, the content of WebText2 can be characterized as high-quality web text curated by Reddit’s community tastes, spanning a rich assortment of topics and writing styles.

Data Collection and Filtering Process

Collection: WebText2 was assembled by scraping web pages that Reddit users had linked to, using Reddit as a filtering mechanism. In practice, OpenAI (and later replication efforts) gathered all URLs from Reddit submissions meeting a threshold of at least 3 upvotes (or “karma”), over a certain period. The original WebText collection covered links up to December 2017. WebText2 extended this to include later years (covering posts through 2018–2019 and possibly up to 2020), thereby incorporating more recent content than WebText1. Using Reddit’s pushshift data and APIs, millions of outbound links were retrieved. This approach leverages Reddit as a form of crowd-sourced curation: links that have a few upvotes are presumably better content than random web pages. As Radford et al. (2019) noted, using Reddit upvotes as a filter is a way to “decentralize curation” of a web corpus. In effect, Reddit users act as filters, surfacing interesting content that then goes into the dataset.

Filtering: After collecting raw HTML from these URLs, the data was cleaned and filtered extensively. First, any duplicate URLs were removed, and further deduplication was done on the text content itself to avoid repeated passages. OpenAI applied heuristic-based cleaning and used techniques like MinHash Locality-Sensitive Hashing to perform fuzzy deduplication at the document level. This ensured that near-duplicate pages or re-posted content did not appear multiple times in the training data. They also excluded non-English content in the filtering process (at least for the original WebText, non-English pages were removed, and likely a similar English-focus was retained for GPT-3’s WebText2 given the model was primarily trained for English). Additionally, a blocklist of certain subreddits was used to avoid scraping content from domains that are pornographic or extremely offensive. OpenAI explicitly did *“not sample from a variety of subreddits which contain sexually explicit or otherwise offensive content.”* This helped limit the amount of overt hate speech or pornographic text in WebText2, though it would not remove all toxic language since even generally benign sources can contain some. Another filtering step was to remove very short texts or pages with little actual content; for example, the open replication enforced a minimum length (e.g. discarding documents under 128 tokens).

After these steps, the resulting WebText2 corpus was a cleaned collection of millions of high-quality web documents. Importantly, OpenAI also deduplicated across datasets – meaning if a given text appeared in Common Crawl and WebText2, for instance, it would be removed from one to prevent the model from overweighting it. The end product was a corpus of web pages that had passed both human curation (via Reddit) and algorithmic cleaning. According to community reconstructions, the original WebText ended up with ~8,013,000 documents totaling 40 GB; WebText2, with extended timeframe, would have even more documents (on the order of tens of millions) to reach its 19B-token size. All of this was done offline and prior to training – GPT-3 did not crawl the web live, but learned from this pre-collected WebText2 data.

Differences Between WebText2 and WebText1 (Original WebText)

WebText2 builds upon WebText1, the dataset used to train GPT-2, with a similar collection philosophy but several notable differences in scope:

Timeframe and Freshness: The original WebText (WebText1) only included Reddit-linked pages up to about late 2017. WebText2 expanded this, pulling in links from 2018 and 2019 as well (since GPT-3’s training data covers up to around 2019). This means WebText2 contains more recent events, memes, and knowledge. Essentially, it’s a larger and more up-to-date crawl following the same Reddit-upvote heuristic. The “OpenWebText2” project confirms that extending coverage beyond 2017 to gather more data was a primary goal for the second version.

Size: Thanks to the extended range and continuous accumulation of content, WebText2 is considerably larger than its predecessor. WebText1 was ~40 GB of text (8 million documents). WebText2’s token count (≈19 billion) suggests roughly double the content (for comparison, 8 million documents of WebText1 likely contained on the order of 8–10 billion tokens). In GPT-3’s training, WebText2 provided about 6.3% of the total training tokens (19B out of ~300B), whereas WebText1 was the entirety of GPT-2’s training corpus. In practice, this larger size means WebText2 covers more diverse content simply by virtue of having more webpages and presumably includes new domains that became popular post-2017.

Inclusion in Training Mix: Another difference is how the dataset was used. For GPT-2, WebText was the main (only) pretraining data. For GPT-3, WebText2 was one of five components. OpenAI chose to give WebText2 a much higher relative weight in training than its raw size would suggest (22% weight for 19B tokens). This contrasts with WebText1 which wasn’t mixed with other sources at all. The heavy weighting indicates OpenAI’s confidence in the quality of WebText2. It also means GPT-3 effectively “relied” on WebText2 more than on some larger-but-noisier sources like raw Common Crawl. In summary, WebText2 played a more strategically weighted role in GPT-3’s training, whereas WebText1 was simply the bulk dataset for GPT-2.

Overlap with Other Data: In WebText1, Wikipedia was left out entirely. In WebText2, Wikipedia was still excluded from the scraped pages, but GPT-3 incorporated a separate Wikipedia dataset. So GPT-3 had the benefit of both: curated Reddit-sourced web pages and a full copy of Wikipedia on the side. This is a slight methodological difference: GPT-2 avoided Wikipedia in its data; GPT-3 included it as a small portion but isolated. Additionally, OpenAI took care to deduplicate overlaps between WebText2 and CommonCrawl or Books corpora, an issue less relevant when WebText1 stood alone. (They did, however, discover a bug in their filtering that caused some overlap leak – meaning a few evaluation texts were unintentionally present in the training data – but they could not retrain due to cost. This bug was general and not specific to WebText2, but it underscores that the engineering of WebText2 wasn’t perfect.)

Public Availability: Like WebText1, the official WebText2 corpus used by OpenAI has not been released publicly (likely due to licensing and safety concerns). However, communities have attempted to replicate it (e.g. OpenWebText for the original, and OpenWebText2 for the extended version). These efforts confirm many of the above details (e.g., the original WebText’s statistics and the plan to gather more data for WebText2). In terms of content, any qualitative differences (such as shifts in popular sites or topics between 2017 and 2019) are incidental to the time period. The fundamental construction method remained the same between WebText1 and WebText2 – leveraging Reddit as a filter for quality web content.


In essence, WebText2 is WebText1 “plus more” – more data, more recent links, and integrated slightly differently (as part of a larger training set). It retained the core idea of Reddit-guided scraping while addressing the need for greater scale to train a 175B-parameter model.

Notable Features and Limitations of WebText2

High-Quality, Human-Curated Content: A key feature of WebText2 is its relatively high quality compared to randomly scraped web data. By using Reddit upvotes as a signal, the dataset is enriched with pages that people found worthwhile. This tends to include well-written articles, engaging discussions, and useful resources. OpenAI viewed WebText2 as a way to “counterbalance the low-quality of the Common Crawl” data. Common Crawl, while huge, contains a lot of noisy or boilerplate text, whereas WebText2’s content has passed through human interest filters. Indeed, WebText2 was given the highest sampling priority during GPT-3 training because OpenAI considered it one of the most valuable sources. Each token from WebText2 was effectively counted ~5.5 times as much as a token from the raw crawl in the training mix. This oversampling reflects its quality: the model was steered to learn disproportionately from this curated subset of the internet. As a result, WebText2 is often credited with making GPT-3’s outputs more coherent and “human-like,” since the model saw so many examples of human-curated writing.

Diversity of Topics and Styles: Thanks to Reddit’s broad user base and interests, WebText2 covers an extremely wide array of topics. From scientific blog posts to news commentary, DIY guides, historical essays, and programming tutorials, the dataset has a bit of everything. This diversity is a strength, as it exposes the model to multiple domains and writing styles. It is not limited to any single genre or type of text. Many niche or specialized topics that have online communities (e.g. discussions on subreddits for medicine, law, gaming, etc.) would indirectly contribute content via shared links. That said, the diversity is constrained by what Reddit users tend to share. For example, programming and tech content is richly represented (since those communities are active on Reddit), while highly scholarly content might be less present unless it was popularized in a blog or news piece. Still, compared to a more homogenized source like Wikipedia or a single-book corpus, WebText2 brings a heterogeneity that helps a model like GPT-3 generalize across many tasks.

Biases and Representativeness: A notable limitation of WebText2 stems from the biases of Reddit’s community. Because the selection of content is driven by what Reddit users upvote, it inherently reflects the interests and viewpoints of that demographic (largely English-speaking, Western, and male-dominated in many subreddits). This has led observers to describe GPT-3 as “monocultural” despite being trained on multilingual text, meaning the cultural lens is skewed toward that of the English-speaking internet forum user. In effect, Reddit acts as a cultural filter. Content that never gets traction on Reddit would be absent from WebText2. For example, regional news or perspectives outside the Anglophone internet or content from communities underrepresented on Reddit might be missing or underweighted. One analysis pointed out that by boosting WebText2 ~5× in training, *“Reddit, with all the biases of its various communities, was artificially elevated in importance.”* This can manifest in the model’s behavior: GPT-3 might mirror opinions or assumptions common in Reddit-shared content. Additionally, although explicit hate speech was filtered at the subreddit level, toxic or biased language can still appear in mainstream content and thus in WebText2. The dataset likely contains internet biases such as gender or racial stereotypes and moderate levels of profanity or hostility, simply because those exist in news comments, blogs, etc. The GPT-3 authors noted that “toxic language” does occur in the training data and GPT-3 sometimes reproduces it. WebText2 is certainly not free from that issue. It also may over-represent internet pop culture and tech-centric world knowledge relative to, say, formal literature or offline information. These biases are important caveats when considering GPT-3’s outputs – the model’s knowledge and tone are influenced by what Reddit users curate, which is not an unbiased sample of all possible text.

Lack of Transparency and Copyright: Another limitation is that WebText2, as an internal dataset, isn’t publicly inspectable. Researchers and the public cannot easily see exactly which documents are in it, since OpenAI did not release it (to avoid legal and ethical complications). This opacity means analysis of its contents is indirect. We infer its composition from methods and partial statistics, but we don’t know, for instance, the exact proportion of news vs. blogs, or which specific articles appear. Moreover, the dataset almost certainly includes copyrighted material (news articles, blog posts, etc. that are not public domain), raising ethical questions. OpenAI’s vague description of Books1 and Books2 corpora (and reluctance to detail them) has been noted as possibly due to copyright concerns, and similar issues apply to WebText2 (web content is often copyrighted). Thus, while WebText2 was valuable for training, it sits in a gray area regarding data usage rights – a common issue for large-scale AI datasets.

Unbalanced Tokenization Effects: One minor technical note – since WebText2 was stored in byte-pair encoded (BPE) tokens like the rest of GPT-3’s data, certain content might be tokenized sub-optimally. For example, code from GitHub or unusual unicode characters might break into many tokens. This isn’t a limitation of the dataset per se, but it affects how the model sees the data. Large amounts of code in WebText2 could inflate the token count (since code can be character-dense), though GPT-3 still learned from it. It’s worth noting that WebText2’s 19B “tokens” are BPE tokens, not words – so the actual word count is smaller (perhaps ~15 billion words). The use of BPE means the dataset includes a variety of token types, from whole words to subword fragments, which GPT-3 had to learn to handle.

In summary, WebText2’s strengths lie in being high-quality and varied, thanks to Reddit curation, while its weaknesses include embedded biases from that curation and limited transparency. It significantly improved training data quality, but one should be aware of the perspective it represents.

Contribution of WebText2 to GPT-3’s Capabilities

WebText2 played a critical role in shaping GPT-3’s performance. By providing a large volume of curated web text, it contributed in several ways:

Improved Coherence and Fluency: Because WebText2 is full of well-written, human-approved content (as opposed to garbled or random text), it likely helped GPT-3 learn to generate more coherent and fluent language. Models trained on higher-quality prose tend to produce higher-quality outputs. OpenAI’s weighting strategy explicitly prioritized WebText2 for this reason – it helped the model “see” good writing more often during training. This is reflected in GPT-3’s ability to generate paragraphs of natural-sounding English that often read as if written by a person. The diversity in writing styles (news vs. blogs vs. forum comments) also means GPT-3 can adapt its tone or register in responses, a capability bolstered by having seen those variations in WebText2.

Broad Knowledge and Domain Coverage: The wide range of topics in WebText2 enriched GPT-3’s knowledge base. GPT-3 absorbed facts and terminology from countless subjects present in the web pages. For example, WebText2 likely contained articles about science, technology, sports, politics, arts, and almost any topic Reddit discussed. This broad coverage helps GPT-3 respond to a variety of prompts with relevant information. It complements the breadth provided by Common Crawl, but with generally more context per document. Where Common Crawl might have many fragmented pages, WebText2’s pages are typically more content-heavy (since trivial or low-effort pages wouldn’t get upvoted). This means GPT-3 had the benefit of reading full articles and posts that delve into topics, helping it learn how to handle complex or long-form content. Many of the few-shot tasks GPT-3 excels at (like answering questions or summarizing) presumably benefited from the model having ingested so many detailed web articles via WebText2.

Internet and Pop Culture Savvy: GPT-3 displays a notable adeptness with internet culture, memes, and colloquial language. WebText2 is a big reason why. Since its content comes from what Redditors share, GPT-3 saw plenty of contemporary internet slang, cultural references, and popular knowledge. This makes the model more “savvy” about things like memes or trending discussions (at least up to 2019). In contrast, a model trained only on books might miss those informal nuances. Thus, WebText2 helped ground GPT-3 in the style of modern internet communication. This contributes to GPT-3’s skill in casual dialogue or comedic prompts, for example.

Coding and Structured Text Abilities: Interestingly, GPT-3 shows some ability to generate code (in languages like Python, JavaScript/JSX, CSS, etc.) even though it was not explicitly a code-specialized model. WebText2 was a likely contributor here. As noted, WebText2 includes content from sites like GitHub and Pastebin, which contain source code and configuration files, as well as discussions about programming. By learning from these, GPT-3 picked up patterns of coding languages and markup. Observers have found GPT-3 can produce syntactically correct code snippets or understand code-like input to a surprising extent. This capability was later harnessed in OpenAI’s Codex model. While Common Crawl also has some code, the curated nature of WebText2 might mean it had more useful, commented code examples (like code from tutorials or Q&A posts). Therefore, WebText2 directly expanded GPT-3’s versatility, enabling it to handle not just natural language but also semi-structured text like code and HTML. In general, any niche domain that was sufficiently represented in Reddit-shared content (be it coding, recipes, poems, etc.) would give GPT-3 a foothold in that domain knowledge.

Better Few-Shot Learning: The GPT-3 paper’s core finding was strong few-shot learning performance. WebText2 likely contributed to this in subtle ways. Few-shot prompts often resemble short dialogues or Q&A pairs which could appear in web forums or comment threads. Since WebText2 contains lots of interactive or instructive content (e.g., FAQ pages, how-to articles, user discussions), GPT-3 had exposure to those formats. Moreover, the high-quality context in WebText2 examples may have taught GPT-3 to internalize how to follow an instruction or continue a pattern with very little explicit guidance. While it’s hard to quantify, the authors did note that without using these curated sets, purely scaling up Common Crawl data did not yield as good performance. It was the blend (with heavy WebText2, Books, Wiki) that produced GPT-3’s impressive general skill. WebText2, being the largest curated slice, was probably a major factor in GPT-3’s ability to generalize from a few examples – essentially because it had seen many analogues of few-shot scenarios in its training texts.

Reduced Noise and Training Stability: Another practical contribution is that including WebText2 made GPT-3’s training more stable and effective. Large-scale language model training can be sensitive to noise in the data. By injecting a substantial amount of cleaner text (and repeating it a few times), OpenAI improved the signal-to-noise ratio of the training process. This likely helped the model converge to a better functional state (fewer inconsistencies that might come from ingesting garbled web pages). In essence, WebText2 acted as a foundation of reliable text that the model could rely on, which complemented the vast but messier Common Crawl portion. Researchers have noted that “a smaller amount of high quality data” can often outperform an uncurated massive dataset when paired with a big model. GPT-3’s design seems to embrace that principle, and WebText2 is the embodiment of high-quality subset selection.


In conclusion, WebText2 significantly enhanced GPT-3’s training data. By providing a rich, curated sample of the internet, it enabled GPT-3 to learn language patterns, factual knowledge, and stylistic nuances that might have been diluted or lost in a purely random crawl. The dataset’s scale ensured the model still saw tremendous variety, while its quality ensured the model learned from the best the web has to offer (relatively speaking). This combination helped GPT-3 become a more capable and versatile language model. However, as noted, it also meant GPT-3 inherited the biases and viewpoint of Reddit-curated content. Understanding WebText2 gives insight into why GPT-3 behaves the way it does — fluent and knowledgeable across many subjects (thanks to a wealth of quality data), but with subtle tilts reflecting the internet culture that curated its knowledge base.

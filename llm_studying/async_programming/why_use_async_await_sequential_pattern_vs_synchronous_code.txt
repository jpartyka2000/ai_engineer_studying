Even when your logic feels “step 1 → step 2 → step 3,” wrapping it in `async`/`await` still gives you important advantages over pure blocking calls:

1. **Non-blocking I/O**
   Even though you wait for each step to finish before moving on, during that wait your event loop can service other coroutines—timers, network sockets, background tasks, or even other parts of your application. In a synchronous script, a blocking `sleep` or HTTP call would freeze everything.

2. **Uniform API**
   If any one of your steps ever needs to do something asynchronously (e.g. fetch from a database, read from disk, or call another async library), you don’t have to rip out and rewrite your orchestration code. Everything already lives in the async world, so swapping in a new `await something_async()` is painless.

3. **Graceful Shutdown & Cancellation**
   Async code lets you attach timeouts, cancel tasks cleanly, or respond to signals without your entire script hanging mid-operation. You gain a lot more control over error handling and cleanup than you do with bare blocking calls.

4. **Future Concurrency**
   Today your three steps run one after the other, but tomorrow you might want to fire off a heartbeat task, poll multiple services in parallel, or batch up lookups. With `asyncio`, you can introduce `gather()`, `create_task()`, or even third-party schedulers without rewriting your whole flow.

In short, even a “sequential” coroutine lets you plug into Python’s async ecosystem—making your code more flexible, responsive, and ready to scale beyond purely blocking operations.

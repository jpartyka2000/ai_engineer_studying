Imagine you have a bunch of little glasses on a table—one glass for each student in a class. You want to pour exactly a certain amount of water into all those glasses combined (say, exactly 10 cups), but each glass has two rules:

1. **It can’t go below empty** (no “negative” water).
2. **It can’t spill over** (no more than 1 cup per glass).

You also start with a guess for how much water should go into each glass (maybe based on some other measure). The goal is to adjust those guesses so that:

* No glass goes below empty or overflows.
* The total water in all the glasses is exactly 10 cups.
* And you change each guess as little as possible.

Here’s how you “magically” do it in one smooth motion:

1. **Imagine a big flat lid** hovering way above the glasses.
2. **Slowly lower the lid** straight down. Wherever the lid is above a glass’s rim, that glass is full up to its rim. Wherever the lid is below the rim, the glass only fills up to the lid’s level.
3. **Keep lowering** until the total water trapped in all the glasses exactly equals 10 cups.

At that moment, each glass’s water level is your new, adjusted number:

* If the lid is higher than a glass’s old guess, that glass just stays at its old (smaller) level.
* If the lid is lower than a glass’s old guess, you’ve shaved off just enough from that guess so it fits under the lid.
* Glasses that would have ended up below zero or above one automatically get “capped” at those limits by the lid touching floor or ceiling.

Because you lowered the lid uniformly for all glasses, you’ve shifted everyone’s guess by the same (small) amount—so overall you’ve stayed as close as possible to the original guesses while exactly meeting the total‑water goal and the min/max rules.

In real code, that “lid height” is just a single number δ that you subtract from each original guess, then clip to \[0,1]. You pick δ so that, after shifting and clipping, the sum of the new values is exactly what you want.

**Why it feels fair and optimal:**

* Everyone’s guess is adjusted by the same amount (that’s the fairest way to spread the change).
* Nobody gets pushed outside the allowed range (the clip takes care of that).
* The total ends up exactly right (because you stop lowering the lid at the perfect moment).

So, you can tell your friends: “We pour exactly the right total amount by sliding a lid over all the glasses until the volume left under it is perfect—no spills, no empty dips.”  That simple “water‑plus‑lid” picture is literally what the algorithm does under the hood.

Adding a small random offset (the “jitter”)—even if it’s just 0–1 second—helps break up synchronization between concurrent callers trying the same operation. Here’s why it matters:

1. **Prevent thundering herds**
   If many coroutines (or even different processes/clients) all hit a failure at the same time and then sleep for exactly the same delays (1 s, 2 s, 4 s, 8 s, …), they’ll retry in lockstep and potentially overwhelm the service again. Even a tiny bit of randomness makes each client’s retry schedule drift apart, smoothing out the overall retry traffic.

2. **Smoothing burstiness**
   At early retries, a ±1 s jitter is a large fraction of the base delay (e.g. sleep = 1 s → 1–2 s). At later retries it’s relatively small (e.g. sleep = 32 s → 32–33 s), but by then the load on the service has hopefully decreased enough that precise staggering is less critical. Early randomness is most important.

3. **Simple and effective**
   Adding `random.random()` is one of the simplest “jitter” strategies (sometimes called “full jitter” when you randomize between 0 and the full backoff period). Even using a small additive jitter is better than none, and it’s easy to reason about:

   ```python
   delay = min(delay * 2, 60.0)       # exponential growth
   sleep_time = delay + random.random()  # add 0–1 s of jitter
   await asyncio.sleep(sleep_time)
   ```

---

### Alternative jitter strategies

* **Full jitter** (random between 0 and `delay`):

  ```python
  sleep_time = random.uniform(0, delay)
  ```

  Provides maximum randomness, but can make retries very bunched up at low delays.

* **Decorrelated jitter** (AWS recommendation):

  ```python
  sleep_time = min(max(base, random.uniform(0, sleep_time * 3)), cap)
  ```

  Mixes exponential backoff with randomized scaling for even smoother spreads.

* **Proportional jitter** (e.g. ±20%):

  ```python
  jitter = delay * 0.2
  sleep_time = random.uniform(delay - jitter, delay + jitter)
  ```

All of these aim for the same goal: avoid synchronized retries. Even a “small” `random.random()` does meaningful work at the point when your delays are shortest—which is often when the system is most stressed.

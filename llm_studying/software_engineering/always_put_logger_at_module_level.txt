It’s almost always better to keep your logger at the **module** level rather than burying it inside a class. The idiomatic pattern in Python is:

```python
# top of your module
import logging

LOGGER = logging.getLogger(__name__)
LOGGER.setLevel(logging.INFO)
# …add handlers/formatters once…
```

Then anywhere in that file—even inside your class methods—you just refer to that `LOGGER`. The benefits are:

* **Consistent naming & hierarchy:**  You get a logger named after your module’s import path (e.g. `"my_package.my_module"`), which plays nicely with the rest of your application’s logging configuration.
* **Single point of configuration:**  You configure handlers, levels, and formatters in one place, not every time the class is instantiated.
* **Simplicity & clarity:**  There’s no hidden “magic” class state to manage or re-initialize; it’s just a module-level constant everyone re-uses.

If you really need per-class granularity, you can still do:

```python
class Foo:
    LOGGER = logging.getLogger(f"{__name__}.{__qualname__}")
```

—but in practice you almost never gain much over the simple module-level approach.

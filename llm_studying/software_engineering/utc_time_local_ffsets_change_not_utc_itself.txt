What’s changing with DST is **your local offset**, not UTC itself. UTC is a continuous, un‑adjusted time scale; when your region “springs forward” or “falls back,” you’re simply changing how many hours you add or subtract from UTC to get **local** time.

---

### How it works in practice

1. **UTC stays fixed**

   * Think of UTC as a river flowing steadily downstream—its water level never jumps up or down for daylight‑saving.
2. **Local clocks jump**

   * Your local clock is like a weir on that river: twice a year you raise or lower the gate, shifting the local water level relative to the river, but **not** the river itself.
3. **Offsets before vs. after DST**

   * **Eastern Standard Time (EST)** is UTC – 5.
   * **Eastern Daylight Time (EDT)** is UTC – 4.
   * If it’s **08:00 EST**, the UTC time is **13:00 UTC** (08:00 + 5 hours).
   * Once DST kicks in, **08:00 EDT** is **12:00 UTC** (08:00 + 4 hours).
4. **What “falls back” actually affects**

   * You “fall back” from EDT to EST by moving your **local** clock one hour earlier—so the **offset** flips from –4 to –5.
   * But UTC continues ticking at its steady pace; only your conversion formula changes.

---

### Key takeaway

> **UTC never springs forward or falls back.**
> **Your local UTC offset** does, so the same local clock time can map to different UTC values depending on whether DST is in effect.

The classical $O(n\log n)$ “capped‑simplex” algorithm sorts your vector **once up front**, then does a single linear pass (or two) to find the exact water‑filling threshold. Concretely:

1. **Sort** the entries of $p$ in **descending** order:

   $$
     p_{(1)} \ge p_{(2)} \ge \cdots \ge p_{(n)}.
   $$

   This is the $O(n\log n)$ step.

2. **Scan** down the sorted list, maintaining the prefix sums

   $$
     S_k = \sum_{i=1}^k p_{(i)}
   $$

   and checking at which $k$ the “lid” would sit between $p_{(k)}$ and $p_{(k+1)}$ to match your target total $S$. Finding that bracket and solving a small linear equation for $\delta$ is $O(n)$.

3. **Apply** the projection in another $O(n)$ pass:

   $$
     x_i = \min\bigl(1,\;\max(0,\;p_i - \delta)\bigr).
   $$

Because the sort dominates, the overall cost is $O(n\log n)$.

---

In our bisection‑based Python version we skipped that sort and instead repeatedly clipped and summed in $O(n)$ per iteration. That gives you $O(n \times \#\mathrm{iterations})$, which in practice converges fast but isn’t the textbook $O(n\log n)$ approach. If you want guaranteed $O(n\log n)$ worst‑case, swap in the “sort + single scan” method above.

**Wirth's Law** is a famous adage in computing that states:

> **"Software is getting slower more rapidly than hardware becomes faster."**
> — *Niklaus Wirth*, Swiss computer scientist

---

### 💡 What does it mean?

Even though computer hardware (like CPUs and memory) gets faster over time:

* **Software performance doesn't keep up** — in fact,
* Software often becomes **more bloated**, **inefficient**, or **complex**
* So the **end-user experience** can feel **slower or unchanged**, despite faster machines

---

### 🧠 Why does it happen?

* Developers may **rely too much on faster hardware** and write less efficient code
* Feature creep and abstraction layers (e.g., frameworks, libraries) add **computational overhead**
* Many applications prioritize **developer productivity** or **time to market** over raw performance

---

### 📉 Example:

> A word processor in 1990 could run on a 25 MHz machine.
> Today, a far faster computer might run a word processor that **starts slower** and **uses more RAM** — yet offers mostly the same features.

---

### 🔁 Related Concepts:

* **Moore’s Law**: Hardware improves exponentially (e.g., transistor counts double every \~2 years)
* **Jevons Paradox**: Efficiency gains lead to more consumption — similarly, faster hardware invites software bloat

---

### ✅ TL;DR

> **Wirth's Law** warns that **software inefficiency grows faster than hardware performance** — so even with better computers, users may not see speed gains.

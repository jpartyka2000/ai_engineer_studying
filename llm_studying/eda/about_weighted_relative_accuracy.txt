**Weighted Relative Accuracy (WRAcc)** is a popular quality measure in subgroup‐discovery and rule‐mining algorithms. It balances two competing goals:

1. **High precision**: the subgroup should have a higher target‐class proportion than the overall dataset.
2. **Sufficient coverage**: the subgroup shouldn’t be trivially small.

---

## 1. Definitions

* Let $N$ = total number of records in your dataset.
* Let $n_s$ = number of records covered by the subgroup (i.e.\ its support).
* Let $p$ = overall prevalence of the positive class:

  $$
    p \;=\; \frac{\text{# positive records in the whole dataset}}{N}.
  $$
* Let $p_s$ = prevalence of the positive class *within* the subgroup:

  $$
    p_s \;=\; \frac{\text{# positive records in the subgroup}}{n_s}.
  $$

---

## 2. Formula

Weighted Relative Accuracy is defined as:

$$
\mathrm{WRAcc}(S)
\;=\;
\frac{n_s}{N}\;\times\;(p_s - p)
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\mathrm{WRAcc}(S)
\;=\;
\underbrace{\frac{n_s}{N}}_{\text{coverage}}
\;\times\;
\underbrace{\Bigl(p_s - p\Bigr)}_{\substack{\text{precision lift}\\\text{over baseline}}}.
$$

---

## 3. Intuition

1. **Coverage term** $\tfrac{n_s}{N}$

   * Rewards larger subgroups (more examples covered).
   * Prevents tiny “one‐off” rules from dominating (even if they’re 100% precise).

2. **Lift term** $(p_s - p)$

   * Measures how much better the subgroup’s precision is compared to the global average.
   * A subgroup that has the same positive‐rate as the dataset ($p_s = p$) scores zero, no matter its size.

By multiplying them, WRAcc favors subgroups that are both **accurate** (better than baseline) and **useful** (cover a non‐negligible share).

---

## 4. Range and Interpretation

* **Minimum**:

  * Can be negative if the subgroup’s positive rate is *below* the global rate ($p_s < p$).
  * E.g.\ a large subgroup with unusually low target rate yields a negative WRAcc.

* **Maximum**:

  * The absolute upper bound occurs when $p_s = 1$ and $n_s = N$, giving
    $\mathrm{WRAcc} = 1 \times (1 - p) = 1 - p.$
  * For a rare target ($p\ll1$), you can approach nearly 1.

* **Zero**:

  * Any subgroup whose precision matches the global baseline ($p_s = p$).

---

## 5. Why Use WRAcc?

* **Balances precision vs. coverage**

  * Unlike *raw* precision, it penalizes very small rules.
  * Unlike *support*, it penalizes large but low‐precision rules.

* **Smooth trade-off**

  * A rule can compensate a small coverage by having a large lift, or compensate lower precision by covering more records.

* **Easy to compute**

  * Only needs simple counts and proportions.

---

## 6. Example

Suppose:

* Dataset has $N=1000$ records, of which $p=0.10$ (100 positives).
* You find a subgroup with $n_s=200$ records and $p_s=0.25$ (50 of them positive).

Then

$$
\mathrm{WRAcc} 
= \frac{200}{1000} \times (0.25 - 0.10) 
= 0.20 \times 0.15 
= 0.03.
$$

Compare that to a tiny subgroup of 10 records all positive ($n_s=10,\,p_s=1$):

$$
\mathrm{WRAcc} 
= \frac{10}{1000} \times (1 - 0.10) 
= 0.01 \times 0.90 
= 0.009.
$$

Even though its precision is perfect, its WRAcc (0.009) is lower because it covers so few records.

---

## 7. Alternatives & Extensions

* **Relative Accuracy** = $p_s - p$ (ignores support).
* **Weighted Likelihood Ratio** or **Lift** = $p_s / p$.
* **Growth Rate** = $\frac{p_s(1-p)}{p(1-p_s)}$ (odds ratio style).

WRAcc remains a good default for subgroup discovery when you want **interpretable**, balanced rules that generalize beyond tiny pockets.

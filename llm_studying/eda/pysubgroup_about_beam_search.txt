The `ps.BeamSearch(beam_width=50)` call tells **pysubgroup** to use a **beam‐search** strategy when exploring the space of possible feature‐combinations. Here’s what that means in practice:

---

## 1. What is Beam Search in Subgroup Discovery?

* **Breadth-first with pruning**
  Classic breadth-first search would expand *every* partial rule at depth \$d`to all possible single‐selector extensions at depth`d+1\`. Beam search instead:

  1. **Generates** all one-step extensions of the current “beam” of partial rules.
  2. **Scores** those extensions by your quality function (here, WRAcc).
  3. **Keeps only the top `beam_width`** of them.
  4. Discards the rest.

* **Iterates** until you’ve reached your maximum rule length (`depth=5` in your example) or cannot generate any new extensions.

---

## 2. Role of `beam_width`

* **Beam width = 50** means “at each level of the search tree, only keep the 50 highest-scoring partial rules and discard the rest.”
* **Larger beam\_width** → more coverage (explore more combinations), but **higher** memory/time cost.
* **Smaller beam\_width** → faster & less memory, but risk missing some high-quality subgroups because their prefixes didn’t make it into the beam.

---

## 3. How It Works in Your Code

```python
search = ps.BeamSearch(beam_width=50)
result = search.execute(task)
```

1. **Level 1 (depth = 1)**

   * Start with all single‐selector rules (e.g. `age > 30`, `country == 'US'`), score each, keep the top 50.

2. **Level 2 (depth = 2)**

   * For each of those 50, generate all possible one‐more‐selector extensions (e.g. `age>30 AND country=='US'`, `age>30 AND gender=='F'`, …).
   * Score every extension by WRAcc.
   * Keep the 50 best.

3. **Repeat** for depths 3, 4, and 5.

4. **Result**

   * Among all the partial rules you kept at depth 5, pick the overall top 25 (your `result_set_size`) to return.

---

## 4. Trade‐Offs and Tuning

| Beam Width  | Pros                                              | Cons                       |
| ----------- | ------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------- |
| Small (10)  | Very fast, low memory use                         | May miss interesting rules |
| Medium (50) | Good balance of speed vs. coverage                | Moderate memory/time cost  |
| Large (200) | Higher chance to find “rare” or complex subgroups | Much slower, more RAM      |

* **If you see too few diverse rules** or they’re too similar, try **increasing** `beam_width`.
* **If the search is too slow** or you’re running out of memory, **decrease** it.

---

## 5. Why Beam Search vs. DFS or Exhaustive?

* **DFS** (Depth‐First Search) can drill very deep into one branch and may find highly specific rules quickly, but it can also get “stuck” exploring low-quality branches.
* **Exhaustive** search (evaluating *every* possible rule up to depth 5) is usually intractable once you have dozens of selectors.
* **Beam** search strikes a middle ground:

  * Systematically expands breadth‐first (so you explore many different prefixes)
  * Prunes aggressively to keep compute feasible
  * Still guarantees you’ll find the best rules *among* those whose prefixes always stayed in the top `beam_width`.

---

### In Summary

* `ps.BeamSearch(beam_width=50)` means **“at each depth of rule length, hold onto only the 50 best partial rules”**
* It balances **exploration** (trying many different selector combinations) against **efficiency** (never letting the search tree blow up exponentially).
* You can tune `beam_width` up or down depending on your dataset size, feature count, and how deep/complex you expect your best subgroups to be.

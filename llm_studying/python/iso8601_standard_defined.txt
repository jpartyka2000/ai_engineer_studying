**ISO 8601** is indeed a **standard for representing dates and times as *strings*** — not for how they’re stored in memory.

Let’s unpack that properly:

---

## 🧠 What ISO 8601 *is*

**ISO 8601** is an **international standard** (from the International Organization for Standardization) that defines a consistent **string-based representation** of:

* Dates
* Times
* Time zones
* Date-time combinations
* Durations and intervals

It’s purely about **textual / human-readable interchange formats**, not binary storage.

---

## 🧩 Examples of Valid ISO 8601 Date/Time Strings

| Type              | Example                     | Meaning                         |
| ----------------- | --------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Date              | `2025-10-20`                | October 20, 2025                |
| Datetime (local)  | `2025-10-20T14:33:05`       | Local time, no timezone         |
| Datetime (UTC)    | `2025-10-20T14:33:05Z`      | UTC (the “Z” means *Zulu time*) |
| Datetime (offset) | `2025-10-20T14:33:05+02:00` | UTC+2                           |
| Week date         | `2025-W43-1`                | Monday of week 43 of 2025       |
| Ordinal date      | `2025-293`                  | 293rd day of 2025               |
| Duration          | `P3DT4H`                    | 3 days and 4 hours              |

---

## 🚫 What ISO 8601 *is not*

* It doesn’t define how timestamps are **stored** in RAM, on disk, or in databases.
* It doesn’t specify **time arithmetic** or calendars.
* It doesn’t cover **binary formats** (like UNIX epoch, NumPy’s `datetime64`, or Arrow timestamps).

---

## ⚙️ Why it matters for Pandas (and most software)

Because ISO 8601 is standardized and unambiguous:

* Pandas can automatically **parse** it with `pd.to_datetime()`.
* Pandas and most APIs **output** datetimes as ISO 8601 strings (e.g. `to_json()`, `to_csv()`, REST APIs, etc.).
* It’s interoperable across languages and databases — Python, JavaScript, SQL, etc.

---

### ✅ In one line:

> **ISO 8601 defines how to represent dates and times *as strings* for consistent exchange between systems.**

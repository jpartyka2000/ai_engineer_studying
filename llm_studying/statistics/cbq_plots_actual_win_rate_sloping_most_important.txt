Because you’re *binning* by predicted probability, you’ll *always* see a rising “mean predicted win-rate” across quartiles—that’s literally how the bins are defined. What really matters is:

1. **Observed (actual) win-rate slope**  
   - You want the **actual** win-rate in each bin to rise monotonically from Quartile 1 → Quartile 4.  
   - If it *doesn’t*, your model isn’t even ranking customers correctly (poor discrimination).  
   - Perfect ordering would give a strictly increasing sequence of actual rates.

2. **Calibration gap per bin**  
   - For each quartile, compare  
     \[
      \text{mean(predicted prob)}\;-\;\text{actual win-rate}.
     \]
   - Small gaps across all four bins ⇒ good calibration.  
   - Systematic over- or under-prediction in certain bins shows miscalibration.

---

### TL;DR

- **Predicted slope** is guaranteed by construction (qcut on probability).  
- **Actual slope** tells you if higher scores really correspond to higher real win-rates.  
- **Bin-wise gap** (predicted − actual) measures calibration quality.

That combination—**rising actual win-rate** *and* **small pred-vs-obs gaps**—is what you’re really after.

The `np.random.dirichlet` function requires a vector of concentration parameters (often called α) as its argument. By passing `np.ones(n_campaigns)`, you're creating a vector where each concentration parameter is 1. Here’s why this is useful:

- **Uniform Distribution Over the Simplex:**  
  When all concentration parameters are 1, the Dirichlet distribution becomes a uniform distribution over the probability simplex. This means all valid combinations of weights (that add up to 1) are equally likely. You aren’t biasing the simulation towards any particular allocation pattern.

- **No Prior Preference:**  
  Using `np.ones(n_campaigns)` indicates that you have no prior reason to favor one campaign over another when generating random weights. Each campaign gets an equal "prior weight" in the simulation.

- **Flexibility for Simulation:**  
  This uniform sampling is often desirable in Monte Carlo simulations where you want to explore a wide variety of scenarios without imposing additional assumptions.

In summary, passing `np.ones(n_campaigns)` as the argument for `np.random.dirichlet` tells Python to draw a random probability vector with no inherent bias toward any specific allocation pattern—exactly the behavior you want when simulating random budget splits.

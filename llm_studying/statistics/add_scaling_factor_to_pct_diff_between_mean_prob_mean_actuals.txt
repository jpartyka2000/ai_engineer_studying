Yes, scaling or multiplying the delta is absolutely a valid and flexible approach for calibration. In fact, what you're describing is a simple form of post-hoc calibration, and introducing a multiplicative factor for the delta gives you a way to control how aggressively you adjust the predicted probabilities.


---

📐 Current Calibration Formula (Additive Delta)

You're currently doing:

adjusted_prob = predicted_prob + delta

Where:

delta = actual_rate - predicted_rate


---

🔧 Modified Calibration with Scaling Factor (λ)

You can generalize this to:

adjusted_prob = predicted_prob + λ * delta

Where:

λ (lambda) is a hyperparameter (e.g., between 0 and 1) controlling how much of the delta you apply.

When λ = 1, it's your current method.

When λ = 0, no calibration is applied.

When λ < 1, you’re performing partial adjustment (e.g., smoothing).

When λ > 1, you’re over-correcting the calibration, which may be risky unless validated.



---

🧠 Why Use a Scaling Factor?

1. Prevent overfitting on validation set: Especially if you have small groups or noise.


2. Smooth transitions across groups: Avoid large jumps in probability shifts between neighboring bins/groups.


3. Control calibration strength: Useful in pipelines where you want to experiment with conservative or aggressive calibration.




---

⚠️ Practical Notes

Ensure that adjusted_prob stays within [0, 1] after the update.

adjusted_prob = np.clip(predicted_prob + λ * delta, 0, 1)

You can optimize λ using a validation set by minimizing a loss function like Brier score or log loss.

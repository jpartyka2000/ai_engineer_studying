No—the **formula** for the standard error of a **sample mean**,

$$
\mathrm{SE}(\bar Y)
\;=\;
\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}},
$$

does **not** change just because the underlying data aren’t Normal.  That expression,

* $\sigma$ = the true standard deviation of your observations,
* $n$ = your sample size,

is purely a consequence of how variances add for independent draws, and holds for **any** distribution with finite variance.

---

### So why do we always talk about Normality?

* **CLT → Normal sampling distribution**
  The Central Limit Theorem says that, regardless of the underlying distribution of your $Y_i$, the **distribution of**
  $\bar Y = \tfrac1n\sum Y_i$
  becomes approximately Normal as $n$ grows.  This is what lets us turn “SE” into

  $$
    \bar Y \;\pm\; z_{0.975}\,\mathrm{SE}
  $$

  intervals (using the Normal quantile $z_{0.975}\approx1.96$).

* **No Normality needed to compute SE**
  Even if the true $Y_i$’s come from a heavy‑tailed or skewed distribution, you still compute

  $$
    \hat\sigma
    = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n-1}\sum_i (Y_i - \bar Y)^2},
    \quad
    \widehat{\mathrm{SE}}
    = \frac{\hat\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}.
  $$

  That $\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}$ is your best estimate of the sampling‑distribution spread of $\bar Y$.

---

### When things do change

1. **Very small $n$**

   * If $n$ is tiny (say $<30$), the CLT may not have kicked in yet.  The sampling distribution of $\bar Y$ could be noticeably non‑Normal.  In that case you’d use the **Student’s t** distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom for CIs/tests, but you still plug in the same $\hat\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ for the “standard error” term.

2. **Other statistics**

   * If you’re estimating the **median**, **quantiles**, or some complex functional of the data, the SE formula is **different** (often derived by the delta method or by bootstrap).
   * E.g. the SE of the sample median is roughly
     $\displaystyle \frac{1}{2f(m)\,\sqrt{n}}$
     where $f(m)$ is the density at the true median $m$.

3. **Bootstrap paths**

   * For highly skewed or heavy‑tailed data (or small $n$), you can estimate the SE of any statistic—mean or otherwise—by resampling (nonparametric bootstrap) rather than relying on a closed‑form formula.

---

**Bottom line:**

* For **mean-based** summaries (like the MeanAbsSHAP), the standard error formula $\hat\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ is **universal** and does not depend on Normality.
* Normality (via the CLT) is what justifies turning that SE into Normal (or t‑based) confidence intervals, but the SE itself remains the same.

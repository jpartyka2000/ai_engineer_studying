A Q-Q (quantile-quantile) plot is a visual way to check if your data follows a particular distribution — usually a normal distribution.

The idea is simple: if your data is truly normal, then when you sort your values from smallest to largest, they should line up with where you'd *expect* values to fall in a perfect normal distribution. A Q-Q plot puts the expected values on the X axis and your actual values on the Y axis, plotting one point per data point.

If the points fall roughly along a straight diagonal line, your data is approximately normal. If the points curve away from the line, that tells you how your data deviates — for example, points curving upward at the right end means you have a heavier right tail than a normal distribution would (more extreme high values than expected).

It's often more informative than a formal test like Shapiro-Wilk because it shows you *how* your data departs from normality, not just whether it does. A histogram tells you the overall shape, but a Q-Q plot makes subtle deviations from normality much easier to spot.

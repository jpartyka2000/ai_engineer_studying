When we talk about applying linear regression, we’re usually aiming at one of two distinct goals:

---

## 1. **Inference**: Understanding relationships

**Objective:** Answer questions like “How much does a one‑unit change in $X_j$ change $Y$, holding everything else constant?”

* **Key focus**

  * **Coefficient estimates** $\hat\beta_j$, their **standard errors**, **$t$‑tests** and **$p$‑values**.
  * **Confidence intervals** around each $\hat\beta_j$.
  * Model **assumptions** (linearity, homoskedasticity, no omitted‑variable bias, etc.) must be checked carefully.
* **Typical workflow**

  1. Specify a simple, **parsimonious** model: pick a few covariates you believe to be causal drivers.
  2. Fit with **ordinary least squares (OLS)**.
  3. Examine residual plots, run **heteroskedasticity** and **multicollinearity** diagnostics.
  4. Interpret each $\hat\beta_j$ as an estimate of the partial effect $\partial E[Y]/\partial X_j$.
* **When to use**

  * You want **explanations** or to test hypotheses (e.g. “Is income significantly associated with credit‑limit utilization?”).
  * You need **policy guidance** or scientific understanding, not just “what predicts best.”

---

## 2. **Prediction**: Forecasting new outcomes

**Objective:** Build a model that **minimizes out‑of‑sample error** on $Y$, regardless of whether the individual $\beta$s match their “true” causal values.

* **Key focus**

  * **Predictive performance** measures: MSE, MAE, $R^2$ on a **validation** or **test** set.
  * **Generalization**: avoid overfitting via cross‑validation, regularization (Ridge, Lasso), or feature selection.
  * Assumptions like homoskedasticity or unbiasedness of $\hat\beta$ matter far less—what matters is **accurate forecasts**.
* **Typical workflow**

  1. **Engineer** a rich set of features (polynomials, interactions, external data).
  2. Split into **train/validation/test**.
  3. Possibly fit **Ridge**/ **Lasso** (shrinkage) or use cross‑validated feature selection.
  4. Tune hyperparameters (regularization strength) to minimize validation error.
  5. Evaluate final predictive error on the hold‑out test set.
* **When to use**

  * You need to **forecast** or **score** future observations (e.g. predict next month’s sales, default risk).
  * You’re less concerned with “why” or “which driver” and more with “how well” you can predict.

---

### Summary of differences

|                    | **Inference**                           | **Prediction**                              |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| **Goal**           | Estimate & test **relationships**       | Minimize **out‑of‑sample error**            |
| **Modeling**       | Simple, interpretable<br>Few covariates | Rich feature set<br>May use regularization  |
| **Metrics**        | $t$‑stats, $p$‑values, CIs              | MSE, MAE, $R^2$ on validation/test          |
| **Assumptions**    | Critical (linearity, exogeneity, etc.)  | Less critical—focus on predictive power     |
| **Interpretation** | $\hat\beta_j$ ≈ causal effect           | $\hat y$ accurate; $\hat\beta$s perhaps not |

---

In practice, **choose your approach** based on your question:

* **“Why did our default rate rise?”** → use **inference**.
* **“What will our default rate be next quarter?”** → use **prediction**.

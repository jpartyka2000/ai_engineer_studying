Here's the interesting thing: they're mathematically equivalent.

## The equivalence

$$I(X;Y) = H(Y) - H(Y|X) = IG(Y|X)$$

Mutual information *is* information gain. The difference is primarily about framing and context.

## A concrete example

Suppose we're predicting whether someone exercises (Y) based on gym membership (X):

| | Member | Non-member | Total |
|--|--------|------------|-------|
| **Exercises** | 40 | 10 | 50 |
| **Doesn't exercise** | 10 | 40 | 50 |
| **Total** | 50 | 50 | 100 |

**Step 1: Calculate H(Y)**
- P(exercises) = 0.5, P(doesn't) = 0.5
- H(Y) = -0.5 log₂(0.5) - 0.5 log₂(0.5) = 1 bit

**Step 2: Calculate H(Y|X)**
- Given member: P(exercises|member) = 0.8, P(doesn't|member) = 0.2
  - H(Y|member) = -0.8 log₂(0.8) - 0.2 log₂(0.2) ≈ 0.72 bits
- Given non-member: P(exercises|non) = 0.2, P(doesn't|non) = 0.8
  - H(Y|non-member) ≈ 0.72 bits
- H(Y|X) = 0.5 × 0.72 + 0.5 × 0.72 = 0.72 bits

**Step 3: Information gain**
$$IG(Y|X) = H(Y) - H(Y|X) = 1 - 0.72 = 0.28 \text{ bits}$$

**Step 4: Mutual information (alternative calculation)**
$$I(X;Y) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y)$$

- H(X) = 1 bit (same logic as H(Y))
- H(X,Y) = joint entropy over the four cells = 1.72 bits
- I(X;Y) = 1 + 1 - 1.72 = 0.28 bits

Same answer.

## So why two terms?

| Term | Typical context | Emphasis |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| **Information gain** | Decision trees, feature selection | Directional: "how much does X help predict Y?" |
| **Mutual information** | Information theory, general statistics | Symmetric: "how much information do X and Y share?" |

**Information gain** language is common when you have a clear target variable and you're evaluating features. It emphasizes the predictive direction.

**Mutual information** language emphasizes that the relationship is symmetric—I(X;Y) = I(Y;X). Knowing exercise habits tells you just as much about membership as knowing membership tells you about exercise habits.

## When the distinction matters

In practice, the terms are often used interchangeably. But the framing can matter:

- Building a decision tree? You'll hear "information gain"
- Measuring general dependency between two variables without a clear target? "Mutual information" fits better
- Comparing how much two variables "know" about each other? Mutual information's symmetry is the natural framing

The math is identical—it's just which perspective you're taking.

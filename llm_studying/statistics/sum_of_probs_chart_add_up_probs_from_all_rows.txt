Great question — the answer depends on **what exactly you’re trying to evaluate**, but here's the key principle:

---

### ✅ General best practice (and statistically sound approach):

> **Include all rows when summing predicted probabilities**, **regardless of the model’s prediction (0 or 1).**

---

### 💡 Why?

Predicted probabilities are continuous estimates of the **likelihood** of a positive class (e.g., a submit). So:

* If you sum **all predicted probabilities**, you get the **expected number of positive outcomes** across the whole group
* If you filter to only `prediction == 1`, you're conditioning on a thresholding decision, which **biases** the sum

---

### 📊 Example:

| Row | prob | prediction | actual |
| --- | ---- | ---------- | ------ |
| 1   | 0.92 | 1          | 1      |
| 2   | 0.58 | 1          | 0      |
| 3   | 0.45 | 0          | 0      |
| 4   | 0.23 | 0          | 1      |

* **Sum of all probs**: 0.92 + 0.58 + 0.45 + 0.23 = **2.18** → your model expects \~2.18 submits
* **Sum of actuals**: 1 + 0 + 0 + 1 = **2**
* ✅ Comparing these gives a calibration-like view

But if you only include predicted wins:

* Sum of probs = 0.92 + 0.58 = **1.50**
* Sum of actuals = 1
* ❌ Now you’re ignoring part of the probability mass and introducing selection bias

---

### ✅ Summary

| Approach                             | Recommended? | Why                                           |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------ | --------------------------------------------- |
| Sum of probs on all rows             | ✅ Yes        | Reflects total **expected** positive outcomes |
| Sum of probs where `prediction == 1` | ❌ No         | Filters based on threshold; biases the result |

---

### ✅ Rule of thumb:

> **Probabilities live in a continuous world; predictions live in a binary one.**
> To evaluate expectations, **sum probabilities**.
> To evaluate decision-making, **count predictions**.

In regression and other linear-model settings, the **design matrix** (often denoted **X**) is simply the matrix that collects all of your input (predictor) variables in a form that lets you write your model as

$$
y \;=\; X\,\beta \;+\;\varepsilon
$$

where

* **$y$** is the vector of observed outcomes,
* **$\beta$** is the vector of parameters (coefficients) you want to estimate,
* **$\varepsilon$** is the residual noise.

---

### 1. Structure

* **Rows** correspond to individual observations (data points).
* **Columns** correspond to features (predictors), plus usually an initial column of 1’s to capture the intercept.

For example, if you have three data points and two predictors $x^{(1)}, x^{(2)}$, your design matrix might look like:

$$
X
=
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & x^{(1)}_1 & x^{(2)}_1\\
1 & x^{(1)}_2 & x^{(2)}_2\\
1 & x^{(1)}_3 & x^{(2)}_3
\end{bmatrix}
$$

so that

$$
\begin{pmatrix}y_1\\y_2\\y_3\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & x_{1}^{(1)} & x_{1}^{(2)}\\
1 & x_{2}^{(1)} & x_{2}^{(2)}\\
1 & x_{3}^{(1)} & x_{3}^{(2)}
\end{pmatrix}
\,
\begin{pmatrix}\beta_0\\\beta_1\\\beta_2\end{pmatrix}
\;+\;\varepsilon.
$$

---

### 2. Why it’s useful

* **Compact notation**: lets you leverage matrix algebra to solve for $\beta$ in one go (e.g.\ via the normal equations $\beta=(X^TX)^{-1}X^Ty$).
* **Extensibility**: you can augment it with polynomial terms ($x^2$, $x^3$), interactions ($x_1 \times x_2$), or dummy-encoded categorical variables, all just by adding columns.

---

### 3. In practice

* **Intercept**: prepend a column of all 1’s if you want a free intercept $\beta_0$.
* **Centering/Scaling**: you might standardize each column (zero mean, unit variance) so that your coefficients have comparable scales.
* **Dummy coding**: a categorical feature with $k$ levels becomes $k-1$ binary columns.

---

### 4. Connection to weighted least squares

Even in **weighted** least squares, the **design matrix** stays the same—you introduce a diagonal **weight matrix** $W$, but the model is still

$$
y \;=\; X\,\beta\;+\;\varepsilon,
$$

and you solve

$$
(X^T W X)\,\beta = X^T W y.
$$

---

#### TL;DR

A **design matrix** is just a way to line up your inputs into a single matrix $X$ so that fitting a linear model (ordinary or weighted) becomes a matter of straightforward matrix algebra.

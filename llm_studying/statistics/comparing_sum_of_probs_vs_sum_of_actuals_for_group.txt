### ✅ What you're being asked to do:

For each `degree_offering` and `year_quarter` group:

* Compare:

  * **Sum of predicted probabilities**

    * e.g., `sum(probability)` → represents **expected number of submits**
  * **Sum of actual submits**

    * e.g., `sum(SUBMITTED_APPLICATION)` → represents **actual number of submits**

---

### 📊 Why this makes sense statistically:

* **Predicted probability** of submit = 1 for each row is a number between 0 and 1 (e.g., `0.85`, `0.30`, etc.)

* When you sum these over a group, you're computing the **expected count of positive cases**:

  $$
  \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{P}(\text{submit}=1 \mid x_i)
  $$

* Meanwhile, the actual sum of the `SUBMITTED_APPLICATION` column (with 0/1 values) gives you the **observed number of submits** in that group.

---

### ✅ Interpretation

This comparison tells you, for each group:

* Whether your model is **overpredicting** or **underpredicting** submits
* Whether it's **well-calibrated in aggregate** for that segment

You can then compute a simple metric:

```python
error = sum(predicted_probs) - sum(actuals)
```

Or percent difference:

```python
pct_diff = 100 * (sum(predicted_probs) - sum(actuals)) / sum(actuals)
```

| degree\_offering | year\_quarter | probability | SUBMITTED\_APPLICATION |

* Sum of probs: `0.6 + 0.8 + 0.3 = 1.7`
* Sum of actuals: `1 + 1 + 0 = 2`
* Error: `1.7 - 2 = -0.3` → model slightly underpredicts in this group

### 🚨 One Caution

This analysis assumes the model's probabilities are **well-calibrated** (i.e., probabilities are meaningful expectations). If your model is poorly calibrated, this comparison may be misleading.

Here’s a quick comparison for a few example boost-factors $f\in[1,2]$, showing

* the **reciprocal** $1/f$
* the **symmetric reflection** $2 - f$
* and the absolute difference between them:

\| $f$   | $1/f$ (reciprocal) | $2 - f$ (symmetric) | $\bigl|1/f - (2-f)\bigr|$ |
\|:-------:|:--------------------:|:---------------------:|:---------------------------:|
\| 1.00    | 1.0000               | 1.0000                | 0.0000                      |
\| 1.05    | 0.9524               | 0.9500                | 0.0024                      |
\| 1.10    | 0.9091               | 0.9000                | 0.0091                      |
\| 1.20    | 0.8333               | 0.8000                | 0.0333                      |
\| 1.50    | 0.6667               | 0.5000                | 0.1667                      |
\| 1.80    | 0.5556               | 0.2000                | 0.3556                      |
\| 2.00    | 0.5000               | 0.0000                | 0.5000                      |

* **For $f$ just above 1**, the two methods are quite similar (within a few percent).
* **As $f$ grows toward 2**, the **symmetric** approach $(2-f)$ plunges linearly to 0, while the **reciprocal** $1/f$ decays more slowly—creating increasingly large differences.

This shows that even in $[1,2]$, $1/f$ and $2-f$ are **not** equivalent unless $f=1$.

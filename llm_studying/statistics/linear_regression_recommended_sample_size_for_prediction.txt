There really isn’t a magic “N” that works for every linear regression—it depends on how many predictors you have, how big the effects are, how noisy the data are, and what you want to do (prediction vs inference).  That said, here are some useful rules of thumb and considerations:

---

## 1. Minimum for identifiability

* **Absolute bare minimum** is **$p+1$** observations if you have $p$ predictors (so you can invert the $X^TX$ matrix).
* In practice, you almost never work at that boundary—your estimates would be wildly unstable.

## 2. Rule‑of‑thumb: observations‑per‑variable

* A common heuristic is **10–20 observations** per predictor.

  * e.g. if you have 5 regressors, aim for at least 50–100 rows.
* If you’re doing hypothesis tests on coefficients, some texts recommend **20–30 observations** per variable to get decent power.

## 3. Detecting small effects

* If the true relationship is weak (small $R^2$ or small coefficients), you’ll need a **much larger** $N$ to detect it with confidence.
* You can plug your expected effect size, noise variance, and desired power into a **regression sample‑size calculator** or use software (e.g. G\*Power) to get a more precise target.

## 4. Prediction‑focused

* If your goal is **prediction** (not inference), you care about out‑of‑sample error rather than coefficient p‑values.
* In that case you can:
  1. Fit your model on progressively larger subsets of data,
  2. Plot training vs. validation error (a “learning curve”),
  3. Watch where the validation error plateaus—once it levels off, additional data add little.

## 5. Practical guidance

* **Small $p$ (< 10):** Hundreds of rows will often suffice for reasonable estimates.
* **Moderate $p$ (10–50):** You’ll want at least a few hundred—ideally 500–1,000.
* **Large $p$ (> 50):** Thousands of observations, or consider dimensionality reduction (PCA, regularization) first.

---

### Bottom line

* **To “properly” train** a regression for **inference**, aim for **20–30 observations per predictor**, and use power analysis if you need rigour.
* **To “properly” train** for **prediction**, use learning curves and cross‑validation: once out‑of‑sample error stops improving with more data, you’ve likely got enough.

So start with the 10–20 obs/var rule, validate with learning curves, and adjust based on your model’s noise and the effect sizes you care about.

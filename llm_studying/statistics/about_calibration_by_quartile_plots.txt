A **calibration-by-quartiles** plot (sometimes called a “binned calibration” or “coarse calibration” plot) is a simple, intuitive way to check whether your model’s predicted probabilities line up with the actual outcome rates—on a coarse, quartile‐level scale rather than point-by-point.  

---

## 1. What It Is  
- **Binning** your predicted probabilities into four equally‐sized groups (quartiles).  
- **Summarizing** in each quartile:  
  - The **mean predicted probability** (what the model thinks the win-rate is).  
  - The **observed win-rate** (the fraction of actual successes).  
- **Plotting** those two numbers side by side (or as a line) for each quartile bin.

---

## 2. Why Use It  
- **Quick sanity check** of calibration without needing dozens of bins or a smooth curve.  
- Highlights **systematic biases**:  
  - If the model **over-predicts** in low bins, the “predicted” bars will be noticeably higher than the “observed” bars in quartile 1.  
  - If it **under-predicts** in high bins, the observed win-rate will exceed the mean probability in quartile 4.  
- Easy for non-technical stakeholders to grasp: “In the top 25% of scores, we actually win 80% of the time, yet the model only predicts 70%.”

---

## 3. How to Construct  

1. **Compute quartile codes**  
   ```python
   df['quartile'] = pd.qcut(df['probability'], 4, labels=False)
   ```  
2. **Aggregate per quartile**  
   ```python
   summary = (
     df.groupby('quartile')
       .agg(
         mean_pred = ('probability','mean'),
         obs_rate   = ('outcome',    'mean')
       )
       .reset_index()
   )
   ```  
3. **Plot**  
   - **Grouped bars**: one bar for `mean_pred`, one for `obs_rate` in each quartile.  
   - **Line plot**: two lines (predicted vs. observed) connected across quartile centers.

---

## 4. How to Read It  
- **Perfect calibration** ⇒ both bars (or lines) coincide in all four bins.  
- **Slope** ⇒ you expect both predicted and observed to **increase** monotonically from quartile 1 → 4 if the model has any discriminative power.  
- **Gaps** ⇒ vertical distance between predicted vs. observed in each bin shows **calibration error**.  

---

## 5. Best Practices & Variations  

- **More bins**: use deciles (10 bins) for finer-grained calibration at the cost of higher variance per bin.  
- **Confidence intervals**: add error bars showing uncertainty in each observed rate (e.g., Wilson intervals).  
- **Combined plot**: overlay the “perfectly calibrated” diagonal line (45°) and draw your observed vs. predicted points connected, yielding a simplified “calibration curve.”  
- **Display counts**: annotate each bin with the number of samples it contains (should be roughly equal if true quartiles).

---

### In a Nutshell  
A calibration-by-quartiles plot gives you a **high-level snapshot** of how well your model’s probability estimates reflect reality—easy to compute, easy to explain, and powerful enough to catch major mis-calibration or ranking issues at a glance.

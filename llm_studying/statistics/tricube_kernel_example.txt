The **tricube kernel** is a smooth, compactly-supported weighting function often used in LOESS.  For a distance $d$ (usually normalized so that the farthest neighbor in your span has $d=1$), the weight is

$$
w(d) \;=\;
\begin{cases}
\bigl(1 - |d|^3\bigr)^{3}, & |d| \le 1,\\
0, & |d| > 1.
\end{cases}
$$

* **Compact support**: once $d>1$ the weight is exactly zero.
* **Smooth drop-off**: starts at $w(0)=1$, then decays gently, reaching $w(1)=0$.

Here are some sample values:

| Distance $d$ |  $d^3$ | $1 - d^3$ | $\bigl(1 - d^3\bigr)^{3}$ |
| :----------: | :----: | :-------: | :-----------------------: |
|     0.00     | 0.0000 |   1.0000  |           1.0000          |
|     0.25     | 0.0156 |   0.9844  |           0.9537          |
|     0.50     | 0.1250 |   0.8750  |           0.6699          |
|     0.75     | 0.4219 |   0.5781  |           0.1934          |
|     1.00     | 1.0000 |   0.0000  |           0.0000          |
|     1.20     | 1.7280 |     —     |  0.0000 (out of support)  |

* At **$d=0$** you get full weight $1$.
* By **$d=0.5$** you’re down to \~0.67.
* At **$d=1$** and beyond, weight is **0**.

In LOESS local fits, you normalize each point’s distance by the maximum in your neighborhood, compute these tricube weights, and then do a weighted regression so that closer points influence the fit much more than those at the edge.

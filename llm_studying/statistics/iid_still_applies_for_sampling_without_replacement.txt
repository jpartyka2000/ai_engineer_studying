Almost—there’s one subtlety around “independent” in the strict sense, but for large populations it boils down to the same conclusion you reached:

1. **Sampling without replacement isn’t strictly independent**, because once you pick one row, the chance of picking another specific row changes slightly.  In full generality your drawn values $\{Y_i\}$ are **exchangeable** but not independent.

2. **Finite‑population CLT (with correction)**
   There is a version of the Central Limit Theorem that covers sampling **without** replacement: it says

   $$
     \bar Y = \frac1n\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i
     \;\xrightarrow{d}\;
     N\!\Bigl(\mu,\;\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\times\frac{N-n}{\,N-1\,}\Bigr)
   $$

   as long as $n\to\infty$ and $n/N\to\alpha<1$.  The extra factor
   $\sqrt{\frac{N-n}{\,N-1\,}}$
   is the **finite‑population correction** (FPC) that slightly shrinks the variance because of the negative dependence.

3. **Why we treat it as i.i.d. for large $N$**
   When your dataset is huge (e.g.\ $N=2.5$ million or $2.5$ billion) and your sample is relatively small (e.g.\ $n=25{,}000$),

   $$
     \sqrt{\frac{N-n}{\,N-1\,}} 
     \approx 1.0000
   $$

   (the FPC is essentially 1), so the dependence is negligible.  In practice we **approximate** the draws as i.i.d. and use

   $$
     \mathrm{SE}(\bar Y) \approx \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}
   $$

   and invoke the usual CLT to argue $\bar Y$ is approximately Normal.

—

### So in summary

* **Strictly:** sampling without replacement → hypergeometric CLT with a tiny correction factor.
* **Practically:** when $n\ll N$, you can treat your $|\phi_j|$ draws as “effectively i.i.d.” with finite variance, apply the standard CLT, and use $\text{SE}=\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ and Normal confidence intervals just as if you’d sampled with replacement.

“i.i.d.” stands for **independent and identically distributed**.  It means:

1. **Independent**
   Each random variable (or data point) doesn’t influence any other. Formally, for variables $X_1, X_2, \dots, X_n$:

   $$
     P(X_i \in A,\;X_j \in B)
     = P(X_i \in A)\,P(X_j \in B)
     \quad\text{for all }i\neq j,\;A,B.
   $$

2. **Identically distributed**
   Every $X_i$ follows the **same** probability distribution $F$. That is,

   $$
     P(X_i \le x) = F(x)
     \quad\text{for all }i.
   $$

Together, the i.i.d. assumption says your sample $\{X_1, \ldots, X_n\}$ is drawn **with replacement** from one fixed distribution, so you can apply tools like the Central Limit Theorem, compute standard errors simply as $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, and guarantee properties like unbiasedness and consistency of sample averages.

---

**Example:**
Drawing $n$ cards **with replacement** from a well‑shuffled deck: each draw is independent, and every draw has the same distribution over the 52 cards—hence the outcomes are i.i.d.

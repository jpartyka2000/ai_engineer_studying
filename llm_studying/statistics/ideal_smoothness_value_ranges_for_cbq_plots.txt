Because we defined

\[
\text{smoothness} \;=\;\frac{1}{1 + \operatorname{std}(\Delta)},
\]

where \(\Delta\) is your array of first‐differences (i.e. \(\Delta_i = obs_{i+1}-obs_i\)), the **ideal** perfectly linear, equal‐step slope (all \(\Delta_i\) identical ⇒ \(\operatorname{std}(\Delta)=0\)) gives

\[
\text{smoothness} = \frac{1}{1+0} = 1.0.
\]

As the variation in step‐sizes grows, \(\operatorname{std}(\Delta)\) increases, so:

- **Smoothness → 1** means an almost perfectly straight, evenly‐climbed slope.  
- **Smoothness ≈ 0.8–0.9** indicates fairly uniform rises with small kinks.  
- **Smoothness < 0.5** suggests large, erratic jumps (poor sloping).

---

### Concrete examples

| std(diff) | smoothness = 1/(1+std) | Interpretation             |
|----------:|-----------------------:|----------------------------|
|     0.000 |                  1.000 | Perfectly linear slope     |
|     0.050 |                  0.952 | Very smooth                |
|     0.100 |                  0.909 | Mild variation             |
|     0.200 |                  0.833 | Noticeable unevenness      |
|     0.500 |                  0.667 | Quite bumpy                |
|     1.000 |                  0.500 | Large step‐size swings     |
|     2.000 |                  0.333 | Very erratic               |

---

### Rule of thumb

- **`smoothness > 0.9`** → you have an almost textbook‐smooth rise across quartiles.  
- **`0.8 < smoothness ≤ 0.9`** → good, some small irregularities.  
- **`smoothness ≤ 0.8`** → consider inspecting for bins where the observed rate dips or jumps too much.

Use whichever cutoffs make sense for your business context, but in general **higher** smoothness (closer to 1) is **better** sloping.

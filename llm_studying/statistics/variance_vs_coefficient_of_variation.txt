## Variance vs. Coefficient of Variation

**Variance** measures the average squared deviation from the mean:

$$\text{Variance} = \sigma^2 = \frac{\sum(X_i - \mu)^2}{N}$$

**Coefficient of Variation** is the standard deviation divided by the mean:

$$CV = \frac{\sigma}{\mu}$$

## Key differences

| Aspect | Variance | Coefficient of Variation |
|--------|----------|--------------------------|
| **Units** | Squared units of the original data | Dimensionless (often expressed as %) |
| **Scale dependence** | Absolute measure—changes with scale | Relative measure—independent of scale |
| **Comparability** | Hard to compare across different features | Easy to compare across features |
| **Mean dependence** | Independent of the mean | Explicitly normalized by the mean |

## Why this matters

**Variance is scale-dependent.** If you measure height in centimeters, the variance is in cm². Switch to meters, and the variance shrinks by a factor of 10,000. This makes it difficult to compare variability across features measured in different units.

**CV solves this.** By dividing by the mean, CV produces a unitless ratio that allows apples-to-apples comparisons.

## Example

Suppose you have two features:
- **Salary:** mean = $50,000, std dev = $10,000
- **Age:** mean = 40 years, std dev = 10 years

Variance comparison:
- Salary variance: 100,000,000 (dollars²)
- Age variance: 100 (years²)

This makes salary look wildly more variable, but that's just because dollars are smaller units than years.

CV comparison:
- Salary CV: 10,000 / 50,000 = 0.20 (20%)
- Age CV: 10 / 40 = 0.25 (25%)

Now we see that age actually has *more* relative variability than salary. CV strips away the unit problem and reveals the underlying proportional spread.

Yes, that's a fair characterization—with one important nuance.

## Entropy captures two things

1. **How many distinct values exist** (variety)
2. **How evenly distributed those values are** (balance)

Both contribute to entropy. High entropy requires diversity *and* balance.

## Examples

**Scenario A: High diversity, high balance**
- Feature has 4 categories, each with 25% of observations
- H = 2 bits (maximum for 4 categories)
- High entropy ✓

**Scenario B: High diversity, low balance**
- Feature has 4 categories, but one has 97% of observations
- H ≈ 0.24 bits
- Low entropy, despite many categories

**Scenario C: Low diversity, perfect balance**
- Feature has 2 categories, each with 50%
- H = 1 bit
- Moderate entropy

**Scenario D: No diversity**
- Feature has only 1 value (constant)
- H = 0 bits
- Zero entropy

## So the refined statement would be

High entropy indicates that values are spread across many categories *in a relatively even way*. Low entropy indicates either few distinct values, or many values with heavy concentration in just a few.

For practical feature analysis, this makes entropy more informative than just counting unique values. A feature with 1000 unique values sounds diverse, but if 99% of observations fall into one value, it's effectively near-constant—and entropy will reflect that by being low.

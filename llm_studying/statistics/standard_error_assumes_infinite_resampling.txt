The standard error (SE) of an estimator (like the sample mean) is defined as the **standard deviation** of its **sampling distribution**—i.e. the distribution you’d get if you could repeat your entire sampling‑and‑estimation procedure many times under identical conditions.

* **Conceptually**, it assumes an infinite number of hypothetical repeats of the sampling process.
* **Formally**, for the sample mean $\bar Y$:

  $$
    \mathrm{SE}(\bar Y)
    = \sqrt{\mathrm{Var}(\bar Y)}
    = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}},
  $$

  where

  * $\sigma$ is the true standard deviation of the underlying data,
  * $n$ is the size of *each* sample,
  * and you imagine drawing many independent samples of size $n$.

In practice, you only have **one** real sample, so you:

1. Compute the **sample standard deviation** $s$.
2. Estimate SE as $\;s/\sqrt{n}$.

You **do not** need to actually collect multiple samples—the formula describes what would happen **on average** over infinitely many repeats.

---

### Key takeaway

* **SE doesn’t “assume” you actually rerun the experiment** a fixed number of times; it **characterizes** the variability you’d see if you did.
* When you report “$\bar Y \pm 1.96\,\mathrm{SE}$,” you’re saying:

  > “If I could repeat this sampling-and-estimation process over and over, about 95% of those intervals would contain the true mean.”

So it’s a **theoretical** construct tied to the idea of repeated sampling, not a count of how many times you actually estimated it.

## Breaking it down

- **H(Y)** = total uncertainty about Y before knowing anything
- **H(Y|X)** = remaining uncertainty about Y *after* knowing X
- **IG(Y|X) = H(Y) - H(Y|X)** = uncertainty that was *eliminated* by knowing X

So when H(Y) is high and H(Y|X) is low, the difference is large. That large difference represents all the uncertainty that "went away" once you learned X. In other words, X explained most of what was unpredictable about Y.

## A concrete example

Imagine predicting whether a student passes an exam (Y):

**Before knowing anything:**
- 50% of students pass, 50% fail
- H(Y) = 1 bit (maximum uncertainty for binary outcome)

**After knowing their attendance (X):**
- Among high attenders: 95% pass, 5% fail → H(Y|X=high) ≈ 0.29 bits
- Among low attenders: 10% pass, 90% fail → H(Y|X=low) ≈ 0.47 bits
- Weighted average: H(Y|X) ≈ 0.38 bits

**Information gain:**
- IG = 1 - 0.38 = 0.62 bits

Knowing attendance eliminated 62% of our uncertainty about passing. Attendance "contains" most of the information we need to predict the outcome.

## The flip side

If H(Y|X) ≈ H(Y), then knowing X didn't help at all—Y remains just as uncertain as before. Information gain is near zero, and X is useless for predicting Y.

Your framing is spot on: information gain quantifies how much of Y's information is "contained in" or "explained by" X.

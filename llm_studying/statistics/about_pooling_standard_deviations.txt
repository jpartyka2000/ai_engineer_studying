Sure. Let's walk through it with a concrete example.

Say you have one scorer and two fixed outputs from different question types. You run the scorer 25 times on each.

**Output A (factual question):** 25 scores, σ_A = 0.06
**Output B (creative question):** 25 scores, σ_B = 0.14

You want a single number that represents the overall wobbliness of this scorer. You could just average the two standard deviations: (0.06 + 0.14) / 2 = 0.10. But that's not quite right because standard deviations don't combine linearly — variances do.

So pooling means:

1. Convert to variances: σ_A² = 0.0036, σ_B² = 0.0196
2. Take a weighted average of the variances (weighted by degrees of freedom, which is n-1 for each group): ((24 × 0.0036) + (24 × 0.0196)) / (24 + 24) = 0.0116
3. Take the square root to get back to a standard deviation: √0.0116 = 0.108

Compare that to the naive average of the SDs which was 0.10. Not a huge difference here, but pooling through variances is the mathematically correct way to combine spread measurements because variance is the quantity that actually adds and averages cleanly, while standard deviation is not.

The weighting by n-1 matters more when your group sizes are unequal. If you had 5 repeats on Output A but 25 on Output B, you'd want Output B's variance to count more heavily since you measured it more precisely. That's what the pooling formula handles automatically.

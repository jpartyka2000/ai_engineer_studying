Both—they're two sides of the same coin, viewed from different moments in time.

## Before observation: uncertainty

When you haven't yet observed the variable, entropy measures how unpredictable it is. A fair coin has high entropy because you genuinely don't know what's coming.

## After observation: information

Once you observe the outcome, entropy tells you how much information you gained. The more uncertain something was beforehand, the more informative it is to learn the actual value.

## The connection

These aren't competing definitions—they're the same quantity viewed from different perspectives:

| Timing | Interpretation |
|--------|----------------|
| Before observing | "How uncertain am I?" |
| After observing | "How much did I learn?" |

A constant variable has zero entropy both ways: you have no uncertainty beforehand, and you gain no information afterward. A fair coin has maximum entropy both ways: maximum uncertainty beforehand, maximum information gained afterward.

## A helpful intuition

Think of uncertainty as "potential information." The more uncertain a variable is, the more information is *available* to be gained by observing it. Entropy quantifies that potential.

So when someone says "high entropy means high information," they mean high *information content*—the variable has a lot to tell you. When someone says "high entropy means high uncertainty," they mean the outcome is hard to predict. Same quantity, different framing.

For feature analysis, either framing works. A high-entropy feature has diverse, balanced values (uncertain/informative). A low-entropy feature is predictable and won't tell you much.

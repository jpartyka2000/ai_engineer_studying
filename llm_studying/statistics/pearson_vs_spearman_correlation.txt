## Pearson Correlation

**Pearson correlation** measures the linear relationship between two variables:

$$r = \frac{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})(Y_i - \bar{Y})}{\sqrt{\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2 \sum(Y_i - \bar{Y})^2}}$$

It answers: "How well does a straight line describe the relationship between X and Y?"

## Spearman Correlation

**Spearman correlation** measures the monotonic relationship between two variables. Instead of using raw values, it first converts them to ranks, then computes Pearson correlation on those ranks:

$$\rho = \frac{\sum(R(X_i) - \bar{R_X})(R(Y_i) - \bar{R_Y})}{\sqrt{\sum(R(X_i) - \bar{R_X})^2 \sum(R(Y_i) - \bar{R_Y})^2}}$$

It answers: "When X increases, does Y consistently increase (or decrease), regardless of whether the relationship is linear?"

## Key differences

| Aspect | Pearson | Spearman |
|--------|---------|----------|
| **Relationship type** | Linear only | Any monotonic relationship |
| **Based on** | Raw values | Ranks |
| **Sensitivity to outliers** | High | Low |
| **Data requirements** | Continuous, ideally normal | Ordinal or continuous |
| **Perfect score meaning** | Points fall exactly on a line | Ranks are perfectly consistent |

## Visual intuition

Consider these scenarios:

**Scenario 1: Exponential relationship**
- X = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]
- Y = [2, 4, 8, 16, 32]

Pearson ≈ 0.86 (not a straight line, so not perfect)
Spearman = 1.0 (perfectly monotonic—every increase in X corresponds to an increase in Y)

**Scenario 2: Outlier present**
- X = [1, 2, 3, 4, 100]
- Y = [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]

Pearson is heavily distorted by that outlier (100, 5).
Spearman sees ranks [1,2,3,4,5] vs [1,2,3,4,5] = perfect correlation of 1.0.

## When to use each

**Use Pearson when:**
- You expect a linear relationship
- Your data is continuous and roughly normally distributed
- Outliers are not a concern

**Use Spearman when:**
- The relationship might be monotonic but non-linear
- You have ordinal data (rankings, ratings, etc.)
- Outliers are present or the data is skewed
- You want a more robust measure

## Relevance to ML feature selection

Spearman correlation is often more practical for initial feature screening because:

1. **Robustness:** Real-world data frequently has outliers and skewed distributions. Spearman won't be thrown off by a few extreme values.

2. **Catches non-linear relationships:** If a feature has a logarithmic or exponential relationship with the target, Pearson might underestimate its usefulness while Spearman will capture it.

3. **Works with ordinal features:** Survey responses, rankings, or binned categories can be evaluated directly.

A common approach is to compute both: if Pearson and Spearman are similar, the relationship is roughly linear; if Spearman is much higher, there's a monotonic but non-linear relationship worth exploring with appropriate transformations.

Yes—when you say “quartiles,” you’re literally talking about dividing your data into four groups of (roughly) equal size. That equal‐counts property is what makes the comparison of predicted vs. observed rates in each bin fair:

- **Equal sample size → comparable variance.**  
  If one bin had twice as many points as another, its observed rate would be much more stable (lower sampling noise), and the visual gaps could just reflect different error bars rather than real miscalibration.

- **Coarse calibration vs. density calibration.**  
  Quartiles are a form of **fixed-frequency** (equal counts) binning. You could instead choose **fixed-width** bins in probability (e.g. [0–0.2), [0.2–0.4), …), but then your bin sizes (in terms of number of examples) will vary with how your scores are distributed—and your observed rates will have wildly different confidence intervals.

So for a “calibration-by-quartiles” plot the **essence** is equal-sized groups. If you end up with slight imbalances (because of tied scores exactly at the cut-points), that’s usually OK, but the goal is to keep each bin’s count similar so the comparison of mean predicted probability vs. mean actual rate isn’t confounded by differing sample precision.

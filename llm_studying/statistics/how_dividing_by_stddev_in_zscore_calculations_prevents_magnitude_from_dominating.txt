Absolutely — this is a **great follow-up question** because that phrase (“prevents magnitude from dominating”) is subtle but *hugely* important in machine learning, reinforcement learning, and statistics.

Let’s walk through a clear example 👇

---

## 🧮 Example setup

Let’s say we have **two groups of rewards** from different tasks or environments:

| Group | Rewards                 | Mean (μ) | Std. Dev. (σ) |
| ----- | ----------------------- | -------- | ------------- |
| **A** | [10, 12, 8, 9, 11]      | 10       | **1.4**       |
| **B** | [100, 120, 80, 90, 110] | 100      | **14.1**      |

---

## ⚖️ Step 1: Raw (unscaled) differences

Now, suppose we get two new reward values:

* From Group A: **r = 13**
* From Group B: **r = 130**

Both are **+3 units above the mean**, *in their own scales*.

If we only look at raw magnitudes (`r - μ`):

* Group A → +3
* Group B → +30

So numerically, Group B looks *ten times “better”*, but that’s misleading —
because the entire scale of Group B’s rewards is about **10× larger** to begin with.

---

## 🧠 Step 2: Apply z-scores (divide by σ)

Compute:

[
z = \frac{r - \mu}{\sigma}
]

| Group | Raw diff (r−μ) | Std. Dev. (σ) | z-score    |
| ----- | -------------- | ------------- | ---------- |
| **A** | +3             | 1.4           | **≈ +2.1** |
| **B** | +30            | 14.1          | **≈ +2.1** |

Now both have **the same z-score**, meaning each reward is **about 2 standard deviations above average** within its own group.

---

## 🧭 Interpretation

Without dividing by σ:

> Group B’s reward looks 10× larger → the algorithm might think it’s *much* better.

With division by σ:

> Both are “equally impressive,” because each is 2 σ above its mean in its own context.

This prevents the *absolute magnitude* (e.g., 100-scale vs 10-scale) from dominating the update or gradient.

---

## 🔧 Why this matters (especially in ML / RL)

In algorithms like GRPO, or in feature standardization before model training:

* Rewards or features often live on **different numeric scales**.
* If you don’t normalize, the one with the largest numeric range produces the **largest gradients**, dominating learning even if it’s not semantically more important.
* Dividing by the standard deviation rescales everything into comparable, *unitless* terms — so learning depends on *relative deviation*, not *absolute magnitude*.

---

## 🧩 Summary

| Without / With σ | Effect                                                            |
| ---------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| ❌ Without σ      | Big-magnitude values dominate updates (misleading)                |
| ✅ With σ         | Each value is judged relative to its own spread → fair comparison |

---

So when we say “dividing by σ prevents magnitude from dominating,” we mean:

> It stops large-scale numerical values from overpowering smaller-scale ones that are *equally extreme relative to their own variability.*

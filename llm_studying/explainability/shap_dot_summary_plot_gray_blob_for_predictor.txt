In SHAP dot plots the color of each point is typically determined by the value of that feature—usually on a red (high) to blue (low) gradient. If you see a predictor’s points rendered entirely in gray, it generally indicates one of two things:

1. **Low or No Variability:**  
   The feature may be nearly or completely constant across your dataset. With little to no variation, all values end up mapping to the same midpoint in the colormap (often a neutral gray or white).

2. **Non-Numeric or Uninterpretable Values:**  
   If the feature isn’t numeric or isn’t being interpreted correctly for color mapping (for example, if it’s categorical without proper encoding), SHAP may default to a neutral color like gray.

In most cases, seeing gray means the feature’s values aren’t varying enough to show a gradient. You can verify this by checking summary statistics (like the standard deviation) for that predictor.

Additivity is one of the core theoretical properties of Shapley values—by definition, the sum of all feature contributions (plus the expected value) should equal the model's prediction. In other words, the SHAP values for a given instance should add up exactly to the difference between that instance’s prediction and the baseline (expected) value.

In practice, when using exact methods (like TreeExplainer on tree-based models), additivity is usually strictly maintained. However, if you're using approximation methods (such as KernelExplainer with a sampling strategy), you might observe slight deviations from perfect additivity due to estimation error. 

While small deviations may be acceptable for many applications, ensuring approximate additivity is crucial for the reliability and consistency of your explanations—especially in high-stakes scenarios. If the deviation is significant, it may indicate an issue with the explanation method or its parameters, and you might need to adjust your settings or use a more precise method.

So, while you might not check additivity for every single instance in every casual analysis, it is an important diagnostic property to verify periodically to ensure that your SHAP computations are behaving as expected.

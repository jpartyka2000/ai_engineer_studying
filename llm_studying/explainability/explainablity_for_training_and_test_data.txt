It usually makes more sense to generate SHAP explainability plots on your test (or holdout) data. This is because:

- **Generalization Insight:**  
  Explaining predictions on unseen data gives you a more realistic picture of how the model is performing in a real-world setting, rather than explaining patterns the model might have simply memorized from the training data.

- **Avoiding Overfitting Artifacts:**  
  The training data might contain idiosyncrasies or overfitting artifacts that could mislead your interpretation. Test data is not used during model training and thus helps ensure that your explanations reflect the model’s true generalization behavior.

- **Bias Reduction:**  
  Using test data helps reduce bias in the explanation process, providing a more objective assessment of which features are driving predictions when the model is deployed.

That said, there are cases where you might analyze training data—for instance, to diagnose issues during development or understand the behavior of the model in depth—but for communicating model decisions to stakeholders or ensuring robust performance insights, test data is typically preferred.

Yes, it's acceptable—especially during early iterations or model development—to generate explainability plots using training data. Doing so can help you:

- **Diagnose and Debug:** Early in development, explainability plots based on training data can help you understand whether the model is learning the expected relationships between features and predictions.  
- **Feature Importance Insights:** They can give you a preliminary idea of which features are driving the predictions, which is useful for iterating on feature engineering or model tuning.

However, keep in mind that training data explanations might be influenced by overfitting or idiosyncrasies in the training set. As the model matures, you'll want to confirm that these patterns hold on unseen (test or validation) data to ensure the explanations reflect generalizable behavior.

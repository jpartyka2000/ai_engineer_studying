When you compute a feature’s **MeanAbsSHAP**—that is, the average of its absolute SHAP values $\bigl|\phi_j(x^{(i)})\bigr|$ over $n$ randomly sampled instances—you’re estimating a **population mean** from a sample. The **standard error** (SE) of that estimate tells you how much variability you’d expect in your sample mean if you repeated the sampling process many times. Here’s why it’s valid:

---

## 1. SHAP values as a random sample

* You draw $n$ instances at random from your dataset (of size $N$).
* For each instance $i$, you compute $Y_i = \bigl|\phi_j(x^{(i)})\bigr|$.
* Under random sampling **without replacement**, the $Y_i$s are (approximately) **independent** and identically distributed (i.i.d.) if $n\ll N$.

## 2. Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

* The CLT says that, for i.i.d. $Y_1,\dots,Y_n$ with finite variance $\sigma^2$, the **sampling distribution** of the sample mean

  $$
    \bar Y = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i
  $$

  is approximately Normal with

  $$
    \mathbb{E}[\bar Y] = \mu, 
    \quad
    \mathrm{Var}(\bar Y) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}.
  $$
* Even if the individual $|\phi_j|$ values are skewed or multi‑modal, once $n$ is in the thousands, $\bar Y$ is well‑approximated by a Normal distribution.

## 3. Standard error formula

* The **standard error** of $\bar Y$ is defined as

  $$
    \mathrm{SE}(\bar Y)
    = \sqrt{\frac{\mathrm{Var}(Y)}{n}}
    \approx \frac{s}{\sqrt{n}},
  $$

  where $s$ is the **sample standard deviation** of your $|\phi_j|$ values.
* SE quantifies your uncertainty: a smaller SE means you’ve pinned down the true mean more precisely.

## 4. Finite‑population correction (FPC)

* Strictly, when sampling **without** replacement from a population of size $N$, you multiply by
  $\sqrt{\tfrac{N-n}{\,N-1\,}}$.
* But if $n \ll N$ (e.g.\ $25{,}000$ out of millions or billions), this factor is essentially 1 (e.g.\ 0.99999…), so we ignore it.

## 5. Why it matters for MeanAbsSHAP

1. **Ranking stability**
   When you compare mean absolute SHAP values across features, you want to know if differences are “real” or just sampling noise. SE (and confidence intervals) give you exactly that measure.
2. **Visualization**
   You can overlay error bars on a SHAP bar plot (mean ± 1.96 SE) to see which features are statistically distinguishable.
3. **Sampling trade‑off**
   You choose $n$ so that your SE is small enough—e.g.\ SE ≈ 0.01–0.005—so that the ranking and magnitudes are reliable.

---

### In a nutshell

Because a feature’s absolute SHAP values across a random sample behave like i.i.d. observations with finite variance, the **standard error of their mean** is a valid—indeed, classical—measure of how precisely that mean estimates the true (population) mean. As long as you sample at random and $n$ is large compared to 1,000, the CLT guarantees your SE formula applies and that you can trust both the point estimate and its uncertainty.

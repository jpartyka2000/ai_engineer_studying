Exactly—step 2’s invocation of the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is what lets us **treat the distribution of the sample mean** (here, the MeanAbsSHAP) as **approximately Normal**, which in turn justifies using the **standard error** and Normal‑based confidence intervals or z‑tests.

A bit more nuance:

1. **Standard Error (SE) by definition**

   $$
     \text{SE}(\bar Y) \;=\;\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}
   $$

   is simply the **true** standard deviation of the sampling distribution of your mean.  You can compute SE whether or not the sampling distribution is Normal.

2. **Why Normality matters**

   * If the sampling distribution of $\bar Y$ really is Normal, then

     $$
       \frac{\bar Y - \mu}{\text{SE}(\bar Y)}
       \;\sim\; N(0,1)
     $$

     exactly, so you can use z‑scores (e.g. $\pm1.96$) to form **95% confidence intervals**:

     $$
       \bar Y \;\pm\; 1.96\,\text{SE}.
     $$
   * If the sampling distribution is far from Normal, those Normal‑based intervals and tests would be invalid.

3. **Role of the CLT**

   * The CLT guarantees that, **regardless** of how skewed or heavy‑tailed the underlying $\lvert\phi_j\rvert$ values are, the **distribution of**
     $\bar Y = \frac1n\sum \lvert\phi_j(x^{(i)})\rvert$
     becomes **approximately Normal** once $n$ is “large enough” (often a few hundred or thousand).
   * Thus, for $n$ in the thousands or tens of thousands, you can safely assume
     $\bar Y$ ≈ Normal$(\mu,\,\sigma^2/n)$.

4. **Putting it together**

   * You **always** compute SE = $s/\sqrt{n}$, where $s$ is your sample’s standard deviation.
   * Thanks to the CLT, you can then say “$\bar Y\pm1.96\,\text{SE}$” gives me an approximate 95% confidence band for the **true** MeanAbsSHAP.

---

**In short:**

* **SE** quantifies the spread of the **sampling distribution** of your mean.
* **CLT** tells you that sampling distribution is (nearly) Gaussian.
* **Therefore** you can apply Normal‑based SE inference (CIs, hypothesis tests) to your MeanAbsSHAP estimates.

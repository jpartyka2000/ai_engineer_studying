LIME (**Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations**) is considered **local** because it explains the prediction of a machine learning model for a **specific instance** rather than providing a global understanding of the model.

### **Why is LIME Local?**
1. **Perturbs Only Around the Instance**  
   - LIME generates explanations by **sampling new data points near the instance being explained** (i.e., small perturbations around the input).
   - It does **not** consider the entire dataset but focuses on how the model behaves around that single point.

2. **Fits a Simple Model Locally**  
   - After generating perturbed samples, LIME trains an **interpretable model (e.g., linear regression)** on these perturbed data points.
   - This local model approximates the behavior of the complex model **only in the region around the original instance**.

3. **Different Explanations for Different Instances**  
   - Since LIME only explains a single instance at a time, **different inputs will have different local explanations**.
   - A globally important feature may not be locally important for a specific prediction.

4. **Model Behavior May Change in Different Regions**  
   - A black-box model may use different decision rules for different parts of the input space.
   - LIME captures **only the local decision boundary**, not the global rules of the model.

### **Analogy**
Think of LIME like using a **flashlight in a dark room**:  
- Instead of trying to understand the **entire room (global model behavior)**, it focuses on **illuminating just the area around a single point** to see how the model makes that specific prediction.

### **Key Takeaway**
LIME is **local** because it:
✅ Perturbs data **only near the instance**  
✅ Learns a simple model **only in that small region**  
✅ Provides explanations that **apply to that instance, not the whole dataset**  

For a **global** view, techniques like **SHAP summary plots** or **feature importance from tree models** are more appropriate. 🚀

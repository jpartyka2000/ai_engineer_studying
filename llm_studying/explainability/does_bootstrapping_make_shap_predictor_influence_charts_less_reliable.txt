Great question.

If you're referring to using bootstrapped training with an ExtraTreesClassifier and then visualizing predictor influence via something like a SHAP summary plot (or "snap" summary plot), then yes—bootstrapping can introduce more variability into the feature importance estimates, depending on how you compute and aggregate them.


---

Why bootstrapping may affect feature influence plots:

1. Bootstrap = less stable trees

Each tree sees only ~63% of the original dataset (with replacement), so:

Individual trees may split on different features due to random sampling noise.

This causes greater variation in which features are selected and considered “important.”


2. SHAP (or similar methods) are sensitive to tree structure

SHAP values are calculated based on:

Tree structure (which features are used in splits, and where),

Path-dependent contributions for each sample.


When trees are bootstrapped:

Some features may appear less frequently, lowering their influence in the aggregate plot.

Others may be overrepresented in some trees by chance.


Thus, SHAP values may fluctuate more, especially when the number of trees is low or the dataset is small.


---

But this variability isn’t necessarily bad:

It reflects model uncertainty in the presence of limited or noisy data.

It can regularize influence scores, preventing overreliance on particular predictors.

If you train enough trees (e.g. 200+), these fluctuations tend to average out.



---

Recommendations:

If consistency/stability is critical: consider disabling bootstrap (bootstrap=False) to ensure every tree sees the full dataset. This gives more deterministic influence scores.

If using bootstrap: use a larger number of trees (e.g. 300–500) to average out variability and stabilize SHAP plots.

Bonus tip: You can generate multiple models with different random seeds and average the SHAP values to get a more robust estimate of feature influence (this is a form of SHAP bagging).



---

TL;DR:

Yes, bootstrapping can lead to slightly less stable SHAP summary plots in ExtraTrees.

No, it doesn't necessarily make them unreliable, especially if you use enough trees and understand that it's reflecting natural uncertainty in the model's structure.

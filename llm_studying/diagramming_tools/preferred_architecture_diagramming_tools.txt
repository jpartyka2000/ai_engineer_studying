Not really. PowerPoint is generally seen as a suboptimal choice for architecture diagrams among software engineers. Here's the landscape:

## What engineers typically prefer

**Diagramming-as-code tools** (most popular in engineering culture):
- **Mermaid** — Markdown-like syntax, renders in GitHub, docs, etc.
- **PlantUML** — Text-based, great for sequence diagrams
- **Graphviz/DOT** — For graph-based layouts
- **D2** — Newer, modern syntax
- **Structurizr** — C4 model-focused

These are version-controllable, diffable, and live alongside code.

**Dedicated diagramming tools**:
- **Excalidraw** — Hand-drawn aesthetic, very popular for quick sketches
- **draw.io (diagrams.net)** — Free, solid, widely used
- **Lucidchart** — Polished, good for collaboration
- **Miro/FigJam** — More for collaborative whiteboarding

**For polished cloud architecture**:
- **Cloudcraft** — 3D AWS diagrams
- **Official icon sets** (AWS, GCP, Azure) in draw.io or Lucidchart

## Why PowerPoint falls short

- No snapping/alignment designed for diagrams
- Painful to maintain as architecture evolves
- Not version-controllable in a meaningful way
- Connectors between shapes are clunky
- Feels like "management tooling" rather than engineering tooling

## When PowerPoint is acceptable

- Presenting to non-technical stakeholders
- One-off slides for an exec review
- You're already in a slide deck and need a quick inline diagram

If you're documenting architecture for an engineering audience, Mermaid or draw.io are better defaults. If it's for a presentation, consider building in a proper tool and exporting an image into your slides.

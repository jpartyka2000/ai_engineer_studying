In Spark, **left-semi** and **left-anti** joins are special-purpose joins for filtering one DataFrame against another:

---

### Left-Semi Join

* **Syntax:**

  ```python
  df1.join(df2, on="key", how="left_semi")
  ```
* **What it does:**
  Returns **only the rows from `df1`** for which there exists *at least one* matching key in `df2`.
  No columns from `df2` are ever included—only the original `df1` columns are kept.
* **Equivalent to:**
  SQL’s `WHERE EXISTS` or pandas’ `df1[df1.key.isin(df2.key)]`.
* **When to use:**
  Fast “inner filter” when you just need to keep rows in `df1` that have a match in `df2`.

---

### Left-Anti Join

* **Syntax:**

  ```python
  df1.join(df2, on="key", how="left_anti")
  ```
* **What it does:**
  Returns **only the rows from `df1`** for which there is **no** matching key in `df2`.
  Again, you only get `df1`’s columns.
* **Equivalent to:**
  SQL’s `WHERE NOT EXISTS` or pandas’ `df1[~df1.key.isin(df2.key)]`.
* **When to use:**
  Quickly filter out any rows in `df1` that appear in `df2`.

---

#### Why these matter

* **Performance:** Spark can optimize semi/anti-joins more efficiently than a generic left-join + filter.
* **Clarity:** Makes your intent (“keep only matching” vs. “drop all matching”) explicit and avoids the need to add extra filter or drop steps.

---

**Example:**

```python
# keep only leads present in the “approved” list
approved_semi = leads_df.join(approved_df, on="LEAD_ID", how="left_semi")

# keep only leads NOT yet approved
approved_anti = leads_df.join(approved_df, on="LEAD_ID", how="left_anti")
```

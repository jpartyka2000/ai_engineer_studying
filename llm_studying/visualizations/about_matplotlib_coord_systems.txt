In Matplotlib, every drawing command (text, lines, patches) takes an (x, y) position that—by default—interprets those numbers in **data coordinates** (i.e. your actual axis scales).  The **Axes fraction** system is an alternative coordinate system where:

- **(0, 0)** is the **bottom-left** corner of the plotting area (inside the spines).  
- **(1, 1)** is the **top-right** corner of the plotting area.  
- Values **< 0** or **> 1** lie outside the axes region (useful for legends or annotations just beyond the plot).  

You activate it by passing  
```python
transform=ax.transAxes
```  
to your artist (e.g. `ax.text`, `ax.annotate`, `ax.add_patch`), so your x/y are interpreted as fractions of the axes width/height instead of data units.

---

### Why use it?

- **Position UI elements**: place legends, labels or info‐boxes in a way that’s independent of your data range.  
- **Consistent layout**: text at (0.5, 1.05) always sits centered just above the top spine, no matter how your data scales change.  
- **Overlay annotations**: draw on the figure relative to the axes, even outside the data region.

---

### Quick reference

| Coordinate System | Transform               | (x, y) meaning                       |
|-------------------|-------------------------|--------------------------------------|
| Data              | default (`ax.transData`)| your actual data values             |
| Axes fraction     | `ax.transAxes`          | fractions of [left→right, bottom→top]|
| Figure fraction   | `fig.transFigure`       | fractions of the entire figure area  |
| Display           | `fig.canvas.get_renderer()` / implicit | pixel coordinates in the window |

---

**Example**:  
```python
# put text at 50% across, 90% up the axes:
ax.text(0.5, 0.9, "Hello", transform=ax.transAxes,
        ha='center', va='center')
```

That “Hello” will always hover near the top-center of your plot, regardless of axis limits.

Yes — a bar chart where bars can go both **up (positive)** and **down (negative)** from a central baseline (usually zero) is called a **diverging bar chart**.

---

### ✅ Key Characteristics of a Diverging Bar Chart:

* Bars extend in **two directions**: positive values above the baseline, negative values below.
* The baseline is typically **zero**.
* Used to emphasize **differences, change, or polarity** (e.g., predicted vs actual, before vs after, agreement vs disagreement).
* Colors are often used to reinforce direction: e.g., **green for positive**, **red for negative**.

---

### 📊 Examples of diverging bar chart use cases:

* **Net change**: `(predicted - actual)`, as you're doing
* **Survey responses**: e.g. % of people who "agree" vs. "disagree"
* **Sentiment analysis**
* **Differences in averages** across categories

---

### 📘 Related chart types:

* **Tornado chart** (a specialized diverging bar chart, often used in sensitivity analysis)
* **Stacked bar chart** with positive/negative contributions (but not always called "diverging")

---

So yes — the chart you’ve built that visualizes `% difference` with positive and negative bars from a zero baseline **is correctly referred to as a diverging bar chart**.

Here’s what’s going on with  

```python
plt.gcf().subplots_adjust(right=0.75)
```

—broken into its two pieces:

---

## 1) `plt.gcf()`: “Get Current Figure”  
- **What it is**: a Matplotlib shortcut for `fig = plt.gcf()`, returning the active `Figure` object.  
- **Why use it**: if you didn’t already capture your figure via `fig, ax = plt.subplots()`, you can still grab it to tweak layout.

---

## 2) `Figure.subplots_adjust(...)`  
This method updates the **subplot parameters**—the margins and spacing that determine how the axes sit inside the overall figure. The most common keyword args are:

| Parameter  | What it controls                                            | Default |
|------------|-------------------------------------------------------------|---------|
| `left`     | Left margin: distance from figure’s left edge (as fraction of width) to the subplot left edge.   | 0.125   |
| `right`    | Right margin: distance from figure’s left edge to the subplot **right** edge.                    | 0.9     |
| `bottom`   | Bottom margin: distance from figure’s bottom edge (as fraction of height) to subplot bottom.     | 0.1     |
| `top`      | Top margin: distance from figure’s bottom edge to subplot **top** edge.                         | 0.9     |
| `wspace`   | Width between subplots (fraction of average subplot width).                                     | 0.2     |
| `hspace`   | Height between subplots (fraction of average subplot height).                                   | 0.2     |

All values run from **0** (very tight to the figure edge) to **1** (fills the entire figure).

---

### Putting it together

- **Before** calling `subplots_adjust`, your subplot might occupy from `left=0.125` to `right=0.9` of the figure width.  
- **With** `right=0.75`, you shrink the subplot’s horizontal span so it ends at 75% of the figure width, freeing up the rightmost 25% for things like legends or text boxes.

```python
fig = plt.gcf()
# shrink the axes area to the left 75% of the figure
fig.subplots_adjust(right=0.75)
```

This is exactly what you need when you add annotations, legends, or text “outside” your main axes—by contracting the axes region, you prevent those elements from overlapping your data.

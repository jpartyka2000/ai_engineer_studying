You can turn that “by-eye” sense of how smoothly your observed win-rates climb across quartiles into a handful of quantitative checks. Here are a few of the most popular approaches, with tiny code sketches you can drop into your pipeline:

---

## 1. Monotonicity via Spearman’s ρ  
Checks whether your sequence is *perfectly* increasing (ρ=1) or has violations (ρ<1).

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import spearmanr

# suppose you have your observed rates in a list or array:
obs = np.array([0.10, 0.30, 0.55, 0.80])
quarts = np.arange(1, len(obs)+1)

rho, pval = spearmanr(quarts, obs)
print(f"Spearman ρ = {rho:.3f}")
```

- **ρ≈1** ⇒ strictly increasing (no dips)  
- **ρ<1** ⇒ one or more non-monotonic steps

---

## 2. Step-Size Consistency (Std of First Differences)  
Measures how *evenly* your rates are rising. Lower standard-deviation of the differences ⇒ more uniform slope.

```python
diffs = np.diff(obs)                   # [0.20, 0.25, 0.25]
smoothness = 1 / (1 + np.std(diffs))   # higher ⇒ smoother
print(f"1/(1+std(diff)) = {smoothness:.3f}")
```

---

## 3. “Roughness” via Sum of Squared Second Differences  
Penalizes big changes in slope (i.e. curvature).  

\[
\text{Roughness} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{n-2} \bigl(obs_{i+2}-2\,obs_{i+1}+obs_i\bigr)^2
\]

```python
sec_diffs = np.diff(obs, n=2)
roughness = np.sum(sec_diffs**2)
print(f"Roughness = {roughness:.4f}")
```

- **0** ⇒ perfectly linear  
- **>0** ⇒ curvature or kinks

---

## 4. Fit & R² of a Linear Trend  
How close your points are to being on a straight line.

```python
from sklearn.linear_model import LinearRegression

X = quarts.reshape(-1,1)
lr = LinearRegression().fit(X, obs)
print(f"Linear R² = {lr.score(X, obs):.3f}")
```

- **R²≈1** ⇒ almost exactly linear  
- **R²<<1** ⇒ more bend/zig-zag

---

### Which to pick?  
- **Spearman’s ρ** is great for checking *pure monotonicity*.  
- **Std(diff)** or **R²** tell you about evenness of the slope.  
- **Roughness** zeroes in on curvature (second-order change).

You can compute one or all, then report something like:

> “Spearman ρ=0.98 (almost strictly increasing), linear‐fit R²=0.95 (very linear), roughness=0.002 (nearly no kink).”

That way you’ve turned “looks pretty smooth” into hard numbers.

No—there’s nothing special about a MultiIndex that magically prevents duplicate labels.  By default, any Index (including each level of a MultiIndex, or even the full tuple‐keys) is allowed to contain duplicates.

If you do

df = pd.DataFrame(
    {'Value': [1,2,3,4]},
    index=pd.MultiIndex.from_tuples(
        [('A','x'), ('A','x'), ('B','y'), ('B','y')],
        names=['L1','L2']
    )
)

you’ll end up with repeated tuples (('A','x') appears twice, etc.), and Pandas is perfectly happy with that.

Likewise, you can have duplicates within a single level even if the full tuples are distinct:

tuples = [('A','x'), ('A','y'), ('A','z')]
# 'A' repeats in level 0, but level‐1 labels differ


The only time you can’t have duplicates is when you explicitly enforce uniqueness—e.g. by converting to an index type that forbids repeats, or by checking index.is_unique and raising an error yourself.  But out of the box, Pandas imposes no “one‐label only” rule on multi-level indexes.

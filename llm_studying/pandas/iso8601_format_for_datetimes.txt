Good question — that `format='ISO8601'` part is a **special flag** introduced in modern versions of Pandas (v2.0+) for `pd.to_datetime`.

Here’s what it means:

---

### 🧠 Explanation

Normally, `pd.to_datetime()` tries to infer the date format automatically (which can be slow and sometimes ambiguous).
If you **know** your timestamps follow the **ISO 8601** standard — like:

```
2025-10-20T14:33:05Z
2025-10-20T14:33:05+00:00
2025-10-20 14:33:05
2025-10-20T14:33:05.123456Z
```

—you can tell Pandas explicitly by setting:

```python
format='ISO8601'
```

That tells Pandas to use a **fast, built-in C parser** optimized for ISO 8601 timestamps, rather than guessing the format.

---

### ⚙️ In your line

```python
campaign_df['start_dt'] = pd.to_datetime(
    campaign_df['start_ts'], format='ISO8601', utc=True
).dt.tz_localize(None)
```

Here’s what happens step by step:

1. `pd.to_datetime(..., format='ISO8601', utc=True)`
   → Parses your `'start_ts'` strings assuming they’re ISO8601 timestamps, and converts them to timezone-aware datetimes in UTC.

2. `.dt.tz_localize(None)`
   → Strips the timezone info, making them *naive* datetimes (no tzinfo attached).

---

### ✅ When to Use

Use `format='ISO8601'` when your timestamps are in one of the standard ISO formats (with or without time zone indicators like `Z` or `+00:00`).
This is both faster and more reliable than letting Pandas infer the format automatically.

---

### ⚠️ Note

* Prior to Pandas 2.0, `format='ISO8601'` wasn’t supported — you’d just use `pd.to_datetime(..., utc=True)` without `format`.
* You can’t mix `format='ISO8601'` with other custom format strings like `"%Y-%m-%d"`. It’s a special keyword, not a format pattern.

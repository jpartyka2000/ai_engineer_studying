Here are the “special” string keywords that `pd.to_datetime()` recognizes beyond `"today"`:

1. **`"now"`**

   * Calling

     ```python
     pd.to_datetime("now")
     ```

     returns a `Timestamp` for the **current** UTC time (including hours, minutes, seconds) rather than just midnight of today. ([Stack Overflow][1])

2. **`"today"`**

   * As you know,

     ```python
     pd.to_datetime("today")
     ```

     yields a `Timestamp` for **today at 00:00:00** (midnight), which you can normalize or compare against other dates. ([Stack Overflow][1])

3. **Missing‐value strings**

   * If you pass in a scalar that signals “not a time,” such as `"NaT"`, or any `None`/`NaN`-like value, Pandas will convert it to a **`NaT`** (its datetime “missing” sentinel). ([Pandas][2])

---

**Beyond these**, most string inputs are parsed by the standard date parser (powered by `dateutil`), so you can still feed things like `"2025-07-28"`, `"Feb 1, 2025 14:30"`, or even ISO8601 variants—just not as *keywords* but as date formats. If you need relative dates like “yesterday” or “tomorrow,” you’ll have to compute those yourself (e.g. via `pd.Timestamp.now() - pd.Timedelta(days=1)`).

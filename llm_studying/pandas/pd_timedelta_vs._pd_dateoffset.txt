There’s actually no `pd.DateTimeOffset` class in pandas—you’ve probably seen **`DateOffset`** and **`Timedelta`**, which serve related but distinct purposes:

1. **`pd.Timedelta`**  
   - **Fixed‐duration** spans (days, hours, minutes, seconds, …) just like Python’s built-in `datetime.timedelta`.  
   - Use it when you want to add/subtract a *constant* amount of time:  
     ```python
     import pandas as pd
     td = pd.Timedelta(days=2, hours=3)        # exactly 2 days+3 hours
     pd.Timestamp('2025-01-01') + td           # → 2025-01-03 03:00:00
     ```
   - Great for “add 90 minutes” or “subtract 7 days” operations.

2. **`pd.DateOffset`**  
   - **Calendar‐aware** offsets (months, years, quarters) where the actual length can vary.  
   - Use it when you need things like “add one month” or “roll to next quarter”:  
     ```python
     do = pd.DateOffset(months=1)
     pd.Timestamp('2025-01-31') + do           # → 2025-02-28 00:00:00
     ```
   - Under the hood it handles month‐length and leap‐year quirks.

---

### TL;DR

- **Use `pd.Timedelta`** when you want the pandas analog of `datetime.timedelta`—a fixed span of time.  
- **Use `pd.DateOffset`** when you need calendar‐based shifts that respect month/year boundaries.

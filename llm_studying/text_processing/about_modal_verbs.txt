A **modal verb** is a helper verb that shows **possibility, ability, permission, or obligation** - basically, it tells us the "mood" or "attitude" about the main action.

## The Common Modal Verbs:
- can / could
- will / would  
- shall / should
- may / might
- must

## What They Do (in Plain English):

**They show HOW LIKELY or NECESSARY something is:**

- "I **can** swim" → ability
- "You **must** leave" → obligation/necessity
- "It **might** rain" → possibility
- "**May** I help you?" → permission
- "We **should** go" → recommendation
- "I **would** help" → conditional willingness

## Key Features:
1. **Always need another verb:** "I can" ❌ vs "I can **swim**" ✅
2. **Don't change form:** "He can" NOT "He cans"
3. **No "to" after them:** "I can swim" NOT "I can to swim"

## Think of them as "attitude words":
- **Regular verb:** "I swim" (just states a fact)
- **With modal:** "I **can** swim" (adds information about ability)
- **With modal:** "I **should** swim" (adds information about recommendation)
- **With modal:** "I **might** swim" (adds information about possibility)

## Easy Test:
If you can replace it with "be able to" (can), "be required to" (must), or "be possible to" (might), it's probably a modal verb!

They're super common in English - we use them constantly to soften commands ("Could you..."), make suggestions ("We should..."), or express uncertainty ("It might...").
